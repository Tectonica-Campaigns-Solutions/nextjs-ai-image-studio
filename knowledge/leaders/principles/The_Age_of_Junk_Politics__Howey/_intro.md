## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

The load-bearing claims the book returns to. Each is individually retrievable and citable; Layer 2 entries point here by name via `anchors to:`.

**Transformation vs. transaction.** A transactional practice trades *existing* power and asks no one to change; a transformational one *moves* people — their hearts, identity, sense of place — and so builds *new* power. The distinction matters because the two call for opposite leadership behaviors, and reaching for the transactional out of habit (it's faster and easier to measure) quietly hollows out a group. Howey takes the framing from James MacGregor Burns (1978), for whom transformational leadership goes beyond exchange to a process where leaders and followers raise one another "to higher levels of morality and motivation," through shared purpose.
flagship example: After a protest whose demands were ignored, a young Howey asked an older activist whether it was worth it if nothing changed; the reply was, "it changed us." The campaign lost on its own transactional terms yet transformed the people in it — the value the transactional ledger never records.
source:  Howey — *The Age of Junk Politics*, ch.3.2, ch.3.4 (drawing on Burns, *Leadership*, 1978)
governs: [A2.5] [A3.3] [A3.1] [A9.1]

**People form beliefs by taking part.** You cannot install a belief in someone by argument; belief is rooted in who a person is, and shifts through the *experience* of participating — having a voice, making a decision, encountering others. The leader's job is therefore not to convince but to "open the way": create the participation through which people transform themselves. As Howey puts it, "they form their beliefs by taking part," reversing the common-sense order in which belief is assumed to precede action.
flagship example: Howey's parents avoided discussing his sexuality for years. Invited to a "Gay Awareness Day," his mother heard hostile out-of-town speakers attack people like her son — and stood up and gave the woman "an earful" in front of the crowd. The act of having a voice, more than the confrontation itself, "solidified her identity as an ally." Participation transformed her.
source:  Howey — *The Age of Junk Politics*, ch.3.4
governs: [A3.2] [A3.4] [A2.4] [A6.6]

**Belonging is primary to belief.** Connection to other people — not the strength of a message — is the real engine of transformation; belonging is "primary to belief," and the relationships built through shared participation are what keep people in and moving. This cuts against the market-borrowed idea that beliefs form in isolation: we move *with* the people we feel we belong to. For a volunteer group it means relationships are the actual material of the work, not a soft add-on.
flagship example: adrienne maree brown's formulation, which Howey adopts: "the strength of our movement is in the strength of our relationships" — measured by depth, so that "scaling up" means going deeper and more vulnerable, not wider.
source:  Howey — *The Age of Junk Politics*, ch.3.6, ch.4.1 (quoting brown, *Emergent Strategy*)
governs: [A4.1] [A2.4] [A6.3] [A4.3]

**Power-with, not power-over.** The most effective leadership does not command others to act regardless of their agency ("power-over"); it facilitates connection and liberates the power people already have ("power-with"), keeping their agency at the center. This grows the total amount of power rather than merely transferring it, and it is the only mode under which transformation is possible, since transformation requires the other person's agency. Howey reaches for the Latin American organizing idea of *acompañar* — accompaniment, walking alongside — which neither controls nor isolates but stands in solidarity, amplifying the other.
flagship example: *acompañar* itself: in Latin American movements, to accompany is to amplify another's power without taking it — the antithesis of the campaign that wins by most controlling the people it "targets."
source:  Howey — *The Age of Junk Politics*, ch.3.6, ch.3.9.4
governs: [A7.1] [A7.3] [A1.1] [A1.2]

**Build leadership, experientially.** Power scales not by raising more money or crafting a better message but by developing the leadership capacity of more and more people — and that capacity is grown through *experience* (with space for reflection and coaching), not delivered in a training. To let it grow, a leader must cede some real decision-making to those ready to take it. This is how transformation moves from the individual to the group to something larger.
flagship example: Hahrie Han and Elizabeth McKenna's account of Obama's 2008 campaign (*Groundbreakers*): the campaign's 2.2 million volunteers were organized on Ganz's "snowflake" model, where organizers "build power by developing the leadership capacities of individuals" and bringing them together to act — which is why so many participants still name it as the moment that redirected their lives.
source:  Howey — *The Age of Junk Politics*, ch.3.8, ch.3.9.4 (citing Han & McKenna, *Groundbreakers*; Ganz)
