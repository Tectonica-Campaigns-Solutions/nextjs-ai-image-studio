## Reframe & flags (read first)

**Audience reframe — dyadic → group leader.** The book is written for *any* two-person hard conversation: firing an old friend, a barking-dog neighbour, a mother-in-law, asking for a raise. None of it is about volunteer leadership. But the craft transfers almost wholesale once the *who* is fixed. Throughout this summary, every concept is bent onto the three conversations a first-time leader actually faces: (1) **leader → member** — raising a hard thing with someone flaky, dominating, underperforming, or leaving; (2) **member ↔ member** — a clash the leader has to help sort out, which is exactly the book's *Third Story / mediator* move; (3) **member → leader** — taking hard feedback without losing your footing (the *Identity Conversation*). This mirrors the Moray summary's `boss→volunteer leader` reframe and sits consistently beside it.

**Footprint.** This is a *narrow, deep* source. It fills **A8 (Conflict, Dissent & Accountability)** heavily and legitimately — A8.1, A8.5, A8.3, A8.4 — with strong secondary weight in **A1.4/A1.6** (the Identity Conversation = the leader's own wobble), **A4.3 + A4.2** (listening as relational craft), **A9.6** (giving feedback = coaching a member), **A6.5** (the learning stance creates safety to dissent), and **A7.2** (joint problem-solving). **A8's volume here is genuine, not padding** (overweighting guard): this is the corpus's specialist conflict/feedback/listening source, a domain Fisher, Ganz, Pink, and Han do not cover. There are **no `H:` handoffs** in the whole summary — interpersonal-conversation craft has no sibling coach (`H:comms` is tooling and channels, not conversations) and it is understanding, not execution, so it stays here at depth.

**FLAG 1 — "Feelings" has no register leaf.** The entire Feelings Conversation (emotions leak when unexpressed; sort feelings from judgments) is a cross-cutting *layer*, not a domain. Per the agreed decision it is tagged onto its nearest leaves (A8.1 the relationship layer of conflict, A6.5 safety to express, A8.5 repair) and flagged here rather than given a new code. Candidate for a new leaf — "emotions / emotional regulation" — after tag-testing. (Same move as Moray's somatics flag.)

**FLAG 2 — the "Three Conversations" is a meta-frame, not one leaf.** Its three parts are tagged to components: *What Happened* → A8.1; *Feelings* → A8.1/A6.5; *Identity* → A1.4. The whole-frame entry carries A8.1 + A1.4 as primaries.

**Coverage gaps (domains the book does not reach).** Being dyadic and conversation-focused, the book is effectively silent on, and should *not* be stretched into: **A2** (purpose, story, shared identity), **A3** (motivation and commitment), **A5** (group design and structure — there is no group here, only a pair), **A7.1/A7.3/A7.4/A7.6** (building power, autonomy frameworks, hierarchy), **A6.1/A6.2/A6.3** (norms-as-culture, stages, cohesion), **A9.1–A9.5** (developing and distributing leadership — the book grows *conversational* skill, not a leadership pipeline), and most of **A10** (burnout, celebration, retention). Its A9 contribution is confined to A9.6 (coaching via listening). Where any entry leans past what the book strictly supports, a `Source reach:` line marks it.

## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

**A difficult conversation is really three conversations.** Beneath every hard exchange run three layers at once: the *"What Happened?"* conversation (who's right, who meant what, who's to blame), the *Feelings* conversation (the emotions present, named or leaking), and the *Identity* conversation (what this says about me). Difficulty comes less from the topic than from mishandling all three simultaneously — so the first skill is simply to *see* which conversation you're really in.
flagship example: Jack stays up all night to rush a print job for his friend-and-client Michael, who texts back that Jack "screwed up" a barely-misaligned chart. On the surface they argue about the chart (What Happened); underneath, Jack is furious and unappreciated (Feelings) and privately wondering whether he's incompetent (Identity) — none of it spoken, all of it driving the wreck.
source: Stone, Patton & Heen — *Difficult Conversations*, ch.1
governs: [A8.1] [A1.4] [A8.5] [A6.5]

**Shift from delivering a message to a learning stance.** Most of us enter a hard conversation to deliver a verdict — to prove we're right, assign blame, or get them to change. The authors call this "the hand-grenade business": coated in tact or not, the message still does damage. The move is to trade the battle of messages for a *learning conversation* — exploring each other's stories, sharing impact, and working the problem jointly. Once you see the genuine complexity, you often find you no longer have a message to deliver, only things to share and ask.
flagship example: Ruby can't get Trevor, a social worker, to turn in paperwork on time; telling him repeatedly only annoys him. When she stops trying to change him and first asks to understand — learning he's overwhelmed by his caseload, while he learns the downstream work his lateness creates for her — he starts hitting deadlines.
source: Stone, Patton & Heen — Intro, ch.1, ch.2
governs: [A8.2] [A6.5] [A8.3]

**Abandon blame; map the joint contribution system.** Blame asks "whose fault is this?" — it judges, looks backward, demands an either-or answer, and triggers defence. *Contribution* asks "what did each of us do or fail to do, and how can we change it?" — it understands, looks forward, and assumes the result is almost always a system both people built. As the authors put it, blame is about judging and contribution is about understanding. Mapping the system, not pinning the blame, is what produces lasting change.
flagship example: When a company's bad strategic call surfaced, management fired the VP who made it and hired a "better" one — but never asked why everyone who foresaw the failure stayed silent. They swapped one part and left the system intact, so the dynamic persisted.
source: Stone, Patton & Heen — ch.4
governs: [A8.5] [A6.4] [A8.3]

**Disentangle impact from intention.** We judge people by the impact their actions have on us, but we *invent* their intentions — and when hurt, we reliably invent the worst. Intentions are invisible; we infer them from impact and are wrong more often than we think. Two errors follow: assuming we know their intent, and assuming that proving our own good intent erases the impact we caused. Separate the two and the conversation stops being a battle over motives.
flagship example: Dad misses his teenager's soccer game; the teen reads it as "he doesn't care." In fact Dad badly wanted to come but had to handle a work emergency or risk his job. His good intention is real — and it does nothing for the teen's disappointment until the impact is acknowledged.
source: Stone, Patton & Heen — ch.3
governs: [A8.1] [A8.5]

**Ground a complex identity.** Hard conversations destabilise us because they touch the *Identity* question — am I competent, good, worthy? All-or-nothing self-images ("I'm competent or I'm not") are brittle; one piece of negative feedback forces either denial or collapse. The fix is to complexify: accept three things — you *will* make mistakes, your intentions are *complex* (not purely good), and you *have* contributed to problems. A self-image that already holds your imperfections can absorb a blow without an "identity quake."
flagship example: Diana broke a friend's confidence to "get advice" and kept arguing she'd meant to help — until she realised she was arguing only because she couldn't admit to *herself* that she'd broken her word. Once she accepted the mistake, the conversation with Isaiah moved.
source: Stone, Patton & Heen — ch.6
governs: [A1.4] [A1.6]
