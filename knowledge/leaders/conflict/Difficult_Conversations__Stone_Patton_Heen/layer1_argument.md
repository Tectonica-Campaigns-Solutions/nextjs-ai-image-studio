# Difficult Conversations — ## Layer 1 — The Overall Argument (book order)


**1. The avoid-or-confront dilemma.** Opens with the trap: raise the hard thing and risk damage; stay silent and the issue festers and you lose self-respect. Tact is no escape — a sugar-coated hard message is still a hand grenade. Sets up that the answer isn't better delivery but a different stance.
concepts: [A8.3] [A1.5]

**2. Sort out the three conversations.** Every difficult conversation is three at once — What Happened, Feelings, Identity — each with predictable errors. Naming the structure is itself the first improvement. Sets up the whole book's architecture.
concepts: [A8.1] [A1.4]

**3. Explore each other's stories (the Truth error).** We argue because we each assume *I'm right, you're wrong*; really we hold different stories built from different information and interpretation. Shift from persuading to understanding. Sets up curiosity over certainty.
concepts: [A8.1] [A7.2]

**4. Disentangle intent from impact.** We invent others' intentions from impact and assume the worst; good intent doesn't cancel harm. Sets up the And Stance.
concepts: [A8.1] [A8.5]

**5. Abandon blame, map contribution.** Blame judges and looks back; contribution understands and looks forward and is almost always joint. Sets up forward-looking, system-level repair.
concepts: [A8.5] [A6.4]

**6. Have your feelings (or they'll have you).** Feelings aren't a byproduct of conflict, they're at its core; unexpressed, they leak or burst and block listening. Sort the real feeling out from the judgment riding on top of it. Sets up emotional honesty as a skill.
concepts: [A8.1] [A6.5]

**7. Ground your identity.** Conversations threaten self-image; all-or-nothing identities shatter under feedback. Complexify the self-image to keep your balance. Sets up the inner stability the conversation requires.
concepts: [A1.4] [A1.6]

**8. What's your purpose? When to raise it, when to let go.** Not every conversation should be had; sometimes the move is to change your own contribution instead. Drop the three purposes that never work (deliver a message, change them, prove you're right) for learning, sharing, and problem-solving.
concepts: [A8.3] [A1.5]

**9. Getting started — the Third Story.** Open from the neutral frame a mediator would use, one that rings true to both sides, then invite the other person to join you in sorting it out. Sets up a non-defensive opening.
concepts: [A8.1] [A8.5]

**10. Learning (listening).** Listening, done with genuine curiosity, transforms the conversation and even makes the other person a better listener. Inquiry, paraphrasing, acknowledgment — but authenticity over technique. Sets up the receptive half of the craft.
concepts: [A4.3] [A9.6]

**11. Expression.** You're entitled to speak; say what you mean rather than easing in; offer your views as views, not as the truth. Sets up the assertive half.
concepts: [A8.2] [A9.6]

**12. Problem-solving — take the lead.** Reframe whatever they throw at you back toward learning; name the dynamic when it stalls; invent options and be clear about who decides, staying open to persuasion but not captive to their yes. Then chapter 12 returns to Jack and Michael to show it whole.
concepts: [A7.2] [A8.5]
