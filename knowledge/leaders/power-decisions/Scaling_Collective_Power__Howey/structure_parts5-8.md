# Scaling Collective Power — Structure & Leadership (Parts 5–8)

## Part Five: What Kills Volunteer Leadership, and What Builds It

If leadership is what we need at every level, the practical question is why it so rarely emerges — and the answer is usually that organizations actively, if unintentionally, kill it.

### What Kills It

**Micromanagement.** When every decision must be checked, every action approved, every piece of work corrected, the message is unmistakable: you are not trusted to lead. Capable people do not stay in such conditions. They leave, or they shrink to the size of the role they have been given.

**Authority ambiguity.** When a leader does not know what they are actually allowed to decide, they default to doing nothing — or they overstep and get slapped back, which teaches everyone watching not to try. Ambiguity about authority is as paralyzing as having no authority at all, often more so, because it adds the fear of getting it wrong.

**No culture.** When there is no shared, explicit understanding of what the group is doing, why, and how it treats its people, the gaps get filled by default behaviors — and default behaviors in volunteer settings tend toward dysfunction: whoever talks loudest wins, conflict goes unaddressed until it explodes, and newcomers cannot find their footing.

**Fiefdoms.** The flip side of too much control is too little. When structure is absent, some individuals accumulate informal power that no one can check or challenge. The group becomes their personal territory. New people cannot enter, dissent cannot surface, and the organization loses the ability to course-correct.

### What Builds It

**Explicit scope and authority — held consistently.** Leaders need to know exactly what they can decide and do on their own, and that boundary needs to hold even under pressure. The consistency matters as much as the clarity: nothing destroys trust faster than authority that is granted in calm moments and withdrawn the instant something feels risky.

**Culture as a deliberate activity.** Culture does not happen on its own; or rather, it always happens, but if you do not shape it intentionally you get whatever emerges by accident. It has to be named, modeled, built into how meetings and trainings and materials feel, and continuously maintained as a living practice rather than a static statement.

**Peer support structures.** Leaders should not be alone with their hard calls. When leaders can turn to each other — for advice, for backup, for the simple reassurance that someone else has faced the same thing — the whole structure becomes more resilient, and the staff stops being the only source of support.

**Investment in the person, not just the role.** The organizations that develop real leaders invest in people as people — in their growth, their development, their sense of purpose — not merely in extracting the function the role requires. This is what signals to a person that they are seen as a leader rather than used as a resource, and it is what earns their full self.

> *You are not building the campaign. You are building the team that delivers it — and then carries it into the next one.*

## Part Six: The Keys to Distributed Organizing

These six principles summarize what the evidence shows — across many models, contexts, and campaigns — about what actually makes distributed organizing work. They are not a model to copy. They are the conditions any model has to satisfy.

**1. Leadership development is the work, not a precondition.**
Every structure eventually runs on the quality of its leaders. The question is never whether to develop them but how deliberately. Organizations that treat leadership development as something that will simply happen — that good people will rise on their own if given enough to do — are precisely the organizations that end up in the horror stories. Development is intentional, it happens before deployment and continuously after, and it is the central activity, not a nice extra.

**2. Autonomy without alignment is chaos; alignment without autonomy is dependency.**
This is the permanent, central tension of all distributed organizing. The goal is not to resolve it — it cannot be resolved — but to manage it deliberately and continuously. Leaders need real authority within real limits. The limits must be explicit, consistent, and maintained even when things get tense. Tip too far toward alignment and you crush the agency that makes the whole thing work; tip too far toward autonomy and you get fiefdoms and chaos.

**3. Culture is the infrastructure.**
Here is the test that makes it concrete: when a volunteer leader faces a hard call at nine o'clock on a Tuesday night, with no staff member available to ask, what guides them? Not the org chart. Not the policy document. Culture — the internalized, shared understanding of what we are doing, why, and how we treat each other. Culture is what holds the structure precisely when the formal structure is not in the room. And it only exists if it has been deliberately built.

**4. Small groups are the unit of power.**
Treating a large base as a single group is not distributed organizing. Distributed models depend on multiple small groups, deliberately kept small enough to sustain real personal relationships and shared responsibility. The relationship *between the members of a group* is the organizing — not just the relationship between the group and the staff. This is where connection and belonging live, and without them you have an audience, not a movement.

**5. Every engagement must have a visible next step.**
Distributed structures plateau wherever there is no clear path deeper. The engagement journey only works if every touchpoint connects explicitly to the next: every event leads somewhere, every training connects to action, every welcome opens into belonging. Dead ends — moments of engagement with nowhere to go afterward — are where momentum dies.

**6. Leadership compounds across campaigns.**
You rarely see the return immediately. The volunteer who stumbles through leading their first event, who grows over the course of a campaign, who carries that hard-won capacity into the next effort — that is where the payoff lives. Distributed organizing does not promise that you will win this campaign. It promises that every effort, win or lose, builds leadership capacity that carries forward and compounds. This is also why a "losing" campaign can be one of the most valuable things an organization ever does, if it develops people.

## Part Seven: Structure — The Most Powerful Lever You Have

### A Question: Structure or Coaching?

In the workshop, before getting into the models, the room is asked a question. In the 1970s, researchers studied customer service teams at Xerox and asked which had a greater impact on team performance: structure or coaching? Before reading on, sit with your own instinct. Which would you bet on?

Most people guess coaching. It feels more active, more human, more like the thing a good leader does. The answer was structure — and by a wide margin. But the reason is the genuinely useful part.

It was not that coaching does not matter. It was that coaching was wildly inconsistent: some coaches helped enormously, some actively made things worse, and across the whole population the effects roughly cancelled out. Structure, by contrast, was stable. When it was in place, it reliably held. And critically: when the structure was sound, coaching worked — good structure created the conditions in which good coaching could actually land. Without sound structure underneath, even excellent coaching dissipated.

The lesson lands hard for organizers, who tend to spend most of their energy on the coaching layer — the encouragement, the feedback, the difficult conversations — while the structural layer was never properly designed in the first place.

### The 60:30:10 Rule

The research synthesized in Colin Fisher's *The Collective Edge* (building on the work of Richard Hackman and Ruth Wageman) puts numbers on it:

- **60%** of a group's effectiveness is determined by its **structure** — its goals, tasks, composition, and norms. These are largely set before the group ever meets.
- **30%** is determined by the **launch** — whether the group's purpose is articulated clearly, whether members understand why they are there and feel they belong, whether the norms for how the group operates are established from the very start.
- **10%** is determined by ongoing **coaching** — the nudges, feedback, and course corrections along the way.

The implication reorganizes where you put your effort. The most consequential work happens *before the group exists* and *at the moment it launches*. A structurally broken group cannot be saved by good management afterward — you will be coaching against the current indefinitely. The person who seems not to pull their weight is usually not lazy; they are unclear on their role or were set up badly. People who do not understand the goal wander in different directions and then blame each other for ending up in the wrong place.

### The Four Structural Elements

The workshop focuses on the four elements that make up that decisive 60%, framed as a system: **Structure → Process → Effectiveness**, with a feedback loop running back from effectiveness to structure, and with psychological safety and coaching supporting the process layer.

**Goals.** What is this specific group actually trying to achieve? Not the overall campaign goal — the group's own concrete goal. Is it specific enough to act on? Does every member understand it the same way? Vague goals are the root of divergent effort and mutual blame.

**Tasks.** What does the group actually do, concretely? Are the tasks matched to the group's real capacity and authority? Is it clear who does what — and crucially, who decides when something falls outside the group's scope?

**Composition.** Who is in the group, and why? Does each member understand their role and feel they belong for a genuine reason? Groups fail not because their members are incompetent but because composition was never thought through — the wrong mix of skills, unclear roles, no shared sense of identity.

**Norms.** What are the operating agreements? How does the group make decisions, handle conflict, communicate? Norms that are not named explicitly get filled in by default — and the default norm in most volunteer structures is that whoever talks loudest wins.

The takeaway that carries into everything else: **every group you launch is a structural intervention. The most important leadership you exercise in distributed organizing happens before the group ever meets.**

## Part Eight: The Structural Forms

There is no single correct structure for distributed organizing. There are several recognizable forms, each suited to different conditions. The workshop maps four, presented along a rough spectrum from more centralized to more distributed.

**Chapter-based / Federated.** Local chapters operate within a region or country under a shared identity and framework. There is real local structure and real local leadership, coordinated through a federated relationship with the center. Strong for building durable local institutions; requires careful attention to the relationship between local autonomy and national alignment.

**Staff-supported, Volunteer-led.** A central staff supports a structure that is led by volunteers — staff enable and develop, volunteers lead and act. This is often the practical heart of distributed organizing: real volunteer leadership, with staff in a development-and-support role rather than a directing one.

**Snowflake / Relational.** The classic relational organizing structure, where leaders develop other leaders in a branching pattern — each leader responsible for a small team, those team members becoming leaders of their own teams. Built explicitly around the multiplication of leadership and the maintenance of real relationships at every node. This is the form most directly designed around the "relationships scale relationships" principle.

**Network / Coalition.** A decentralized network of partner organizations or nodes, connected to each other rather than radiating from a single center. Powerful for its flexibility — different partners can contribute in different ways — but it carries particular challenges around power dynamics between partners, which must be negotiated explicitly rather than left to emerge.

The choice between these is strategic and contextual. What matters is that it is a *choice*, made deliberately, with the autonomy-alignment tension and the six principles in view — not a structure inherited by default or copied because it worked somewhere else.
