## How to Read This Booklet

This is the full content of the half-day workshop, written so that someone who was never in the room can get the same ideas, in the same order, and arrive at the same conclusions. Where the live session used exercises, this version describes what the exercise asked, what typically happens when a room does it, and what the exercise is designed to reveal — so you get the insight even without the activity.

It is organized as the workshop is: we start with what leadership actually is, build toward why distributed organizing is not just efficient but necessary, look honestly at why it so often fails, and end with what it takes to do it well.

A note before we begin, the same one the session opened with: much of what follows is grounded in research and in patterns observed across many campaigns and organizations. But every organization is its own context. Treat this as a set of frameworks to think with, not a set of rules to follow. The goal is to deepen the work you are already doing — not to replace the knowledge you already have of your own people and your own moment.

## Part One: What Is Leadership?

### Almost Everything Requires the Power of Other People

Start with something simple. Think about the last time you got something genuinely difficult done — something that mattered. Not something you could do entirely alone, but something that required others: their energy, their judgment, their relationships, their effort.

Now ask: what did you actually do to make it happen?

In the workshop, this is the opening exercise. Participants are asked to recall a specific situation where they accomplished something that required other people, and to name the tactic, skill, or approach they used to bring people along — something that is genuinely theirs.

What happens in the room is consistent every time. People describe a rich and varied repertoire: listening for what someone actually cares about, not just what they say. Connecting two people who should know each other. Trusting someone with something slightly beyond what they thought they could do. Following up. Holding a person to a commitment without making them feel small. Telling a story about why something matters. Making space for someone quieter to step forward. Sharing credit. Absorbing blame.

These are not management techniques. They are leadership practices, and the room is full of people who use them fluently — in their families, their friendships, their neighborhoods, their previous work.

Then comes the turn that gives the exercise its purpose. We look at that list of things people do naturally and well, and we ask: how much of this are we actually asking our volunteer leaders to do?

The answer, almost always, is: very little.

This is the central gap the entire workshop is built around. The people in our organizations are capable of genuine leadership. They do it everywhere else in their lives. But the structures we build for them rarely ask for it. We ask for their time. We ask them to complete tasks. We ask them to show up. We do not ask them to lead — to make decisions, to hold relationships, to take responsibility for outcomes, to bring their judgment.

The conclusion the exercise drives toward is this: **the constraint on distributed organizing is almost never the capability of our people. It is the willingness of our organizations to trust them with real leadership.**

There is a principle underneath all of this, and it is worth stating plainly:

> **Leadership is needed for anything that requires the power of others to do.**

Despite the myths of rugged individualism, what any one of us can accomplish entirely alone is extremely limited. The moment a goal requires other people — and almost every goal worth having does — you are in the territory of leadership. Not because you have a title, but because you need to move others to act. This is true of a campaign director and it is equally true of a volunteer having a conversation on a doorstep. Leadership is not a position. It is what happens at every point where one person moves another to act.

### What Leadership Is Not

Before defining leadership, the workshop clears away three things it is commonly confused with.

**Leadership is not management.** Management directs tasks. It tells people what to do and checks that they did it. It is sometimes necessary, but it creates dependency: the people being managed do not develop the capacity to act on their own, and the manager becomes a bottleneck through which everything must pass.

**Leadership is not authority, expertise, or charisma.** These can accompany leadership, but none of them is leadership. Plenty of people with authority cannot move anyone. Plenty of experts cannot bring others along. Charisma can open a door, but it does not, by itself, develop anyone or build anything durable.

**Leadership is not doing more than everyone else.** This is the most important one for organizers to internalize, because it is the most seductive failure mode. The person who works hardest, takes on the most, carries the heaviest load — this person looks like a leader and often believes they are one. But if they are doing the work instead of developing others to do it, they are not leading. They are creating an organization that depends entirely on them and collapses when they leave. We will return to this as the "boss" problem.

### The Leadership Spectrum: Burns's Framework

The foundational text here is James MacGregor Burns's *Leadership* (1978), which introduced a distinction that has shaped the entire field since. The workshop presents it as a spectrum.

**Management** sits at one end. Directing tasks, creating dependency.

**Transactional leadership** is next. It operates through exchange: I give you something, you do something for me. You canvass, I give you recognition or a stipend. You show up, I provide a good experience. This works — transactional leadership is real and often necessary — but its commitment is exactly as deep as the transaction. When the exchange stops, the behavior stops. It produces compliance, not ownership.

**Transformational leadership** sits at the far end. It operates at the level of values and higher purpose. It does not ask only what someone wants right now; it connects their action to what they care about most deeply, and in doing so, it changes them. Burns's insight was that transformational leadership is mutual and developmental: leaders and followers raise one another to higher levels of motivation. The process changes both. The follower becomes more of a leader. The leader is changed by the relationship.

Burns's most fundamental claim is one that reframes everything: leadership exists only in relation to followers. A person acting alone is not leading — they are simply acting. Leadership is, by definition, relational.

In his words:

> *"Leaders inducing followers to act for certain goals that represent the values and the motivations — the wants and needs, the aspirations and expectations — of both leaders and followers."*

And:

> *"Leadership is nothing if not linked to collective purpose... Leadership mobilizes, naked power coerces."*
>
> — James MacGregor Burns, *Leadership* (1978)

That last line is the hinge. Mobilizing and coercing both produce action. But coercion commands action regardless of the other person's will. Leadership engages their will. This difference — between commanding action and engaging will — turns out to be the entire question of whether organizing can scale and transform, or whether it can only extract and deplete. We will come to why.

### The "Boss" Problem

There is a particular failure mode worth naming directly, because it is the one most organizations fall into without realizing it.

A "boss" — in the sense the workshop uses it — is someone who appears to be a strong leader because their group is active and productive. But the activity all runs through them. They do most of the work themselves, hold most of the relationships, make most of the decisions. They are tireless and competent and the group seems strong.

The group is not strong. It is fragile. Everything depends on one person. When the boss makes a mistake, the whole group makes the mistake. When the boss burns out or leaves — and they always eventually do — the group falls apart, because nothing was ever built outside of them. A boss gives the people around them very little room to learn, grow, take responsibility, or develop. They produce followers and dependents, never leaders.

This matters enormously for organizing because the boss model feels like success right up until it fails. The metrics look good. The energy is high. And then the central person leaves and there is nothing underneath.

The alternative is a leader whose entire orientation is toward developing other leaders — distributing responsibility, tolerating the slower pace and the inevitable mistakes that come with letting others learn, and building something that can survive their absence and grow beyond their personal capacity.

## Part Two: What Actually Mobilizes People to Act

This is the heart of the workshop, and the part most worth slowing down on. If leadership is about engaging the will of others rather than commanding their action, then everything depends on understanding what actually moves people — what makes a person choose, freely and with their whole self, to act.

### People Do Not Act Because You Told Them To

The transactional model of organizing assumes that people act because you asked, because you incentivized them, or because you persuaded them with the right argument. Each of these can produce a discrete action. None of them produces a person who brings themselves fully to the work over time.

Consider what most campaign infrastructure is actually built to do. It is built to extract a specific behavior from a person as efficiently as possible: a vote, a donation, a shift, a signature. The person is treated as a resource — a source of hours, money, or reach — and the goal is to get the resource at the lowest cost. This is mobilization in its purely transactional form, and within its limits, it works.

But it has a ceiling, and the ceiling is low. People treated as resources behave like resources: they give the minimum the transaction requires and no more. They do not bring their creativity, because creativity was not asked for. They do not bring their networks, their judgment, their vision, because the structure has no place for those things. And they do not stay, because nothing in the exchange has changed who they are or how they see themselves.

### Transformation Comes From Experience, Not Messaging

Here is the counterintuitive claim at the center of the framework, and it is worth stating as directly as the workshop does:

> **People don't just take part because of their beliefs. They form their beliefs by taking part.**

The conventional model has the arrow pointing one way: a person believes something, and therefore they act. Change the belief (through messaging, argument, advertising) and you change the action. This is the assumption underneath nearly all of modern political communication.

It is largely wrong, or at least it captures only the shallowest layer. A digital ad will not change someone's fundamental convictions. Neither will a TV spot, a billboard, a tweet, or a lawn sign. These things can inform, remind, and reinforce — but they do not transform. Genuine shifts in what a person believes, who they understand themselves to be, and what they are willing to act for come from a different source entirely.

They come from experience. And the experience that campaigns and organizations can actually offer — the one within their control — is the experience of participation.

There is a story the workshop draws on that captures this. A longtime activist, asked whether a protest had been worth it when the demands were completely ignored by those in power, answered: "It changed us." The external goal was not met. But the people who participated were transformed by the act of participating — and that transformation carried forward into everything they did afterward.

There is another, smaller story that makes the mechanism precise. Reflecting on how a family member's political conviction was crystallized, the realization was that it was not being confronted by the other side's arguments that did it. It was being given the opportunity to speak herself — to articulate, out loud, in her own voice, what she believed. The act of participating in the conversation, not the content of the conversation, was what concretized the belief.

This is why participation is the engine of transformation. It is not that we participate because we have been convinced. It is that participating is itself the thing that convinces us — that changes us, deepens us, and binds us to a cause and a community.

### The Two Essential Elements: Participation and Human Contact

If transformation comes through experience, then the practical question is: which experiences? What can a campaign or organization actually offer that has transformational potential?

The framework identifies two essential elements:

**1. Participation — the human experience of doing.**
Sourced in our drivers of **will** and **agency**.

**2. Human contact — encountering the other.**
Sourced in our drivers of **connection**, **belonging**, and **conflict**.

Let's take each seriously, because the detail is where the insight lives.

### Participation, Will, and Agency

Participation is transformational only when it is real — when the person is genuinely doing something, exercising their own will, making their own choices, and seeing the consequences. This is why the *kind* of participation matters so much.

Filling envelopes that someone else designed, reading a script someone else wrote, completing a task someone else defined — this is participation in the thinnest sense. It occupies time but it does not engage will or agency in any meaningful way. The person is executing, not deciding. And experiences that do not engage our agency do not transform us. They cannot, because there is no self in them.

Agency is the crucial word. **Agency is the will to participate with meaning** — to act not as an instrument of someone else's plan but as a genuine author of one's own contribution. When a person exercises real agency in an organizing context, something fundamental happens: the work becomes theirs. They are no longer helping with someone else's campaign; they are pursuing their own purpose through a shared effort. This is the moment a supporter becomes a leader, and it cannot happen without agency.

This is also why people need to bring their full selves to the work, and why a structure that does not make room for the full self is leaving its most important power on the table. When we treat a person only as a source of hours, we get their hours. When we make room for their whole self, we get access to everything they uniquely carry:

- Their **insights and vision** — they see things about their own community and context that no staff member ever could
- Their **creativity and strategic thinking** — given a real problem and real authority, people generate solutions no central plan would have produced
- Their **skills** — the specific capabilities they have built across their whole life, most of which your role descriptions never thought to ask for
- Their **networks** — the relationships only they hold, which cannot be transferred or replicated
- Their sense of **which issues matter most** — their own prioritization, which keeps the work connected to what people actually care about
- Their **leadership** — their capacity to move others, which is the thing that ultimately scales everything

None of these can be extracted through a transaction. They can only be brought, freely, by a person who has been given the agency and the trust to bring them. This is the practical, strategic reason that bringing the full self matters: it is not a nicety. It is the difference between accessing a fraction of a person's value and accessing all of it.

And there is a further point about why this is worth the effort even though most people will not take it up. Most supporters, when offered real participation, will decline it — and that is fine. We live in an age of deep distrust, especially toward anything that carries the scent of establishment authority. The responsibility to rebuild that trust falls on those of us who design the structures, not on the public to change on its own. But here is the thing: even the supporters who never step forward are affected by the existence of a real seat at the table. Seeing that a campaign genuinely offers people a place of value — a voice, a role, real agency — is itself a signal that the campaign is authentically people-powered. It builds trust even among those who only watch. The offer of meaningful participation changes the character of the whole enterprise, whether or not any given person accepts it.

### Human Contact, Connection, Belonging, and Conflict

The second transformational element is human contact: the experience of genuinely encountering another person. This is sourced in three deep human drivers — connection, belonging, and conflict.

Connection and belonging are intuitive. People are transformed by the experience of being part of something with others, of being known, of mattering to a group. A person who joins a campaign and finds genuine relationships there is changed by those relationships, and bound to the cause through them. This is also why small groups are the unit of real organizing, a point we will return to: it is in the small group, where actual relationships are possible, that connection and belonging live. A "group" of ten thousand people is an audience. A group of eight is a place where a person can be known.

Conflict is the less obvious one, and the framework's inclusion of it is deliberate. Encountering genuine difference — talking to someone who does not already agree, working through real disagreement, being changed by an honest exchange across a divide — is among the most transformational experiences available. The grandmother who shifted her view on a referendum did so not because she was messaged at, but because she was given the chance to voice her concerns openly and have a real human exchange about them. Each genuine dialogue with someone different is an opportunity to change hearts and minds — in both directions — and it builds in the participant the increasingly rare skill of communicating across difference. That skill then travels with them into their personal relationships and their wider community. The transformation extends far beyond any single campaign.

This is what is lost when we replace human contact with broadcast technology. When we optimize the human encounter out of our political practice in the name of efficiency, we should ask seriously what we are losing — because what we are losing is the only thing that actually changes anyone.

### Why This Is the Case for Leadership at Every Level

Put the two elements together and the strategic picture becomes clear. The objective of organizing is not just to have conversations or to occupy people with activity. It is to motivate genuine action in others — to move people — and to do so in a way that transforms them into people who will, in turn, move others.

That is leadership. And it explains why leadership is needed at every single point of contact, not just at the top. The canvasser at the doorstep is practicing leadership when they engage a stranger's will rather than reciting at them. The captain training that canvasser is practicing leadership. The staff member developing that captain is practicing leadership. At each level, the same thing is happening: one person is engaging the agency of another and, in doing so, growing both the campaign's power and that person's capacity.

The critical distinction, again, is between *power-over* and *power-with*. Power-over commands action regardless of the other person's agency — it can produce compliance but never transformation, because it bypasses the will entirely. Power-with means the one leading and those following share a higher purpose that sits above the transaction of power between them. The agency of those following is at the center. This is the only kind of leadership relationship that *grows* power rather than merely transferring it — because it leaves the follower more capable, more committed, and more able to lead others than before.

This is the mechanism by which transformation scales from the individual to the community to society: not through better messaging, but through structures that make space for leadership at every level, so that transformed people transform others, who transform others again.

## Part Three: The Limits of Personal Capacity and the Logic of Distribution

### The Hard Limit of One Person

There is a reason the boss model fails, and it is not only cultural — it is mathematical. Any one human being can sustain only so many genuine relationships. The research associated with the anthropologist Robin Dunbar suggests an outer limit of roughly 150 stable relationships, with much smaller numbers — perhaps 15, perhaps 5 — for the closer, more intensive ones. These are biological constraints, not failures of effort or will.

For organizing, this is decisive. If the real work of transformation happens through genuine relationships — and everything in Part Two argues that it does — then the transformational reach of any single organizer is capped by their personal relational capacity. You can be the most gifted organizer alive and you will still hit the wall of how many people you can actually know.

This leads to the line that anchors the whole workshop:

> **The only thing that scales relationships is relationships.**

You cannot broadcast a relationship. You cannot automate the kind of human contact that transforms people. The only way to have more transformational relationships in the world is to have more people capable of holding them. Which means the only way to scale transformational organizing is to develop more leaders — each of whom holds their own network of genuine relationships, each of whom develops still more leaders.

### From Personal Capacity to Collective Capacity

This is the conceptual shift from a centralized model to a distributed one. In the centralized model, there is a single hub — the organizer or the boss — connected directly to every participant. Its reach is bounded by that one person's capacity, and its quality degrades as it scales, because the central person's attention is divided ever more thinly.

In the distributed model, there is no single hub. There is a network of interconnected leaders, each of whom is themselves a hub for their own cluster of relationships. The organizer's job changes fundamentally: instead of holding all the relationships, they develop and support the leaders who hold them. The capacity of the system is no longer the capacity of one person. It is the sum — and through leadership development, the multiplication — of everyone's capacity.

This is what "collective capacity" means, and it is the structural reason distributed organizing can do what centralized organizing cannot. It is not merely more efficient. It is the only form that allows transformation, which is inherently relational, to operate at scale.

## Part Four: How We Hold Power

To lead in this way — power-with rather than power-over — requires understanding power itself more carefully than we usually do. The workshop uses the framework from Pavini Moray's *How to Hold Power*, which distinguishes four modes.

**Power-over** → control → compliance → dependency.
Authority exercised through control and hierarchy. It produces compliance in the short term and dependency over time. It is not always wrong — there are genuine emergencies and genuine cases where it is appropriate — but it is routinely overused and mistaken for the default nature of leadership. It cannot transform anyone, because it bypasses their agency.

**Power-under** → resistance → disengagement → disruption.
The experience of being subjected to power without genuine consent or agency. People held in power-under may comply for a while, but the underlying current is resistance, which expresses itself as disengagement and eventually as disruption. This is what volunteers experience when they are activated into tasks without being given real ownership — and it is why such volunteers quietly drift away or, occasionally, blow up.

**Power-with** → shared power → ownership → collective leadership.
Authority that emerges from relationship and mutual accountability. Power is shared rather than held over others, which produces genuine ownership and, ultimately, collective leadership. This is the mode organizing requires. It is also the most demanding, because it depends on continuous investment in relationship and trust — it cannot be set up once and left to run.

**Power-within** → self-mastery → presence → grounded leadership.
The internal dimension: a leader's own sense of groundedness, self-mastery, and presence. This is the somatic, embodied side of leadership — how you actually hold yourself when you are standing in front of a group, navigating conflict, or making a hard call. It can be read about but is ultimately developed through real experience and reflection. It is the foundation that makes power-with sustainable, because holding shared power under pressure requires a leader who is grounded in themselves.

The practical lesson: the kind of power you hold is not a stylistic choice. It determines what your leadership produces. Power-over produces dependent followers. Power-with produces leaders. And only the latter can grow.
