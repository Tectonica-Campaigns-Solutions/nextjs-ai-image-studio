## Reframe & flags (read first)

**Audience reframe — org→volunteer group.** This is the corpus's hardest altitude reframe. Han et al. study *organizations* (LUCHA, ISAIAH, AMOS, New Virginia Majority, PLAN, KFTC) and mostly *professional* leaders building *independent political power* against elites, funders, and electoral targets. The campaign/electoral/measurement apparatus does not pass the **leader-not-org** gate and is excluded or routed to **H:org-level**. What transfers — and transfers richly — is the book's people-side core: *how you build members who are committed, agentic, and able to act on their own.* On that core this is the strongest single source in the corpus for A9 (distributing leadership), A7 (power/decisions), and the mobilizing-vs-organizing lens. Throughout, "organization" is read as "group," "constituency/base" as "members," "organizer/staff leader" as "volunteer leader." Where a claim leans on electoral leverage, funders, or campaign mechanics, a `Source reach:` line marks it.

**FLAG 1 — "commitment-and-flexibility" has no single leaf.** The book's signature people-finding (members loyal to each other are *more* adaptable, not less) is a paired construct. Tagged across A3.4 + A2.4; flagged as a candidate lens or leaf after tag-testing, not minted here.

**FLAG 2 — "civic feedbacks" has no leaf.** The transferable kernel — *how* you engage people today shapes their capacity and commitment tomorrow — is distributed across A3.4 / A6.6 / A9.1. Flagged, not given a code.

**FLAG 3 — "distributed strategists" sits on the A9/A7 seam.** It is develop-others (A9) × shared-decisions/autonomy (A7) at once. Primary tag is A9.5; A7.3 carried inline. Watch whether retrieval wants A7 as primary.

**Genre note.** As Moray is the corpus's inner-game source, this is its *organizing / people-building* source. It is rich on A2, A3, A4, A6.6, A7, A9 and near-silent on **A5 (structure), A8 (conflict), A10 (sustaining/burnout)** — don't stretch it to fill those; other sources carry them.

**Size note.** Summary ≈ 16K words ≈ ~18% of the ~90.7K-word source — mid-range, justified by heavy org-level exclusions (the entire measurement chapter, campaign mechanics, and funder/coalition structure are cut, leaving a dense people-building core).

## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

**People are power, not props.** A group's strength is not its headcount, email list, or volume of activity — it is the number of people in authentic, accountable relationship who will act *again and again*. Because real power is the capacity to move people with recurrence (Hansen), a list you cannot move twice is not power; only people who are agents rather than interchangeable units (Brown) constitute durable strength.
flagship example: An Arizona leader said he was proud of 300 members he knew by name — whose children and stories he knew, developed over ten years — over a 120,000-name email list and 20,000 Facebook likes, which looked bigger but, in his words, "those aren't real." The 300 were the people who showed up every Monday and held him accountable.
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4
governs: [A7.1] [A3.2] [A2.5] [A4.1] [A10.6]

**Commit people to each other, not to the issue.** Build members who have chosen to *stand together*, not members bound to one outcome; commitment to people, not to a policy or candidate, is what makes a group both durable and flexible. Because loyalty to peers survives losses and pivots that loyalty to an issue cannot (Hirschman: voice needs loyalty), a people-committed group can change course without falling apart.
flagship example: An ISAIAH "faith delegate" had donated to, phoned for, and house-partied for one candidate — then the group collectively decided to back a different candidate who could actually win. She voted with the collective against her own favorite, explaining "if we don't stay as a collective, we don't have any power."
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4 (drawing on Ganz; Hirschman)
governs: [A2.4] [A3.4] [A2.3] [A4.6]

**Develop distributed strategists, not task-takers.** The highest-leverage thing a leader builds is members who can analyze a situation and decide for themselves — not members who wait for direction. Because the hard moments are unpredictable, only people equipped to think and act on their own can act *at all* when they arrive; and capacity is built by handing real responsibility out, not by hoarding it.
flagship example: A young KFTC member kept deferring decisions to the staff organizer — "isn't that your job?" — and the organizer kept answering, "No, this is," pushing the strategic work back onto the member until she could hold it herself.
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5
governs: [A9.5] [A9.1] [A9.2] [A7.3] [A3.2]

**Relationships are the medium, and you build them on purpose.** A group is held together by a constantly growing latticework of ties — created, not merely inherited — and reinforced by shared ritual and story. Because people arrive for an issue but stay for the relationships and the vision, the ties are the actual glue; and they have to be regenerated, not assumed (Ganz: movement groups don't just draw on relationships, they create new ones).
flagship example: KFTC's director observed that people come because they want to work on something like voting rights, "but they stay because of the vision [and] the relationships, the family atmosphere, the team." The group opened its statewide retreat with everyone reading its vision statement aloud, in unison.
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5
governs: [A4.1] [A4.4] [A4.3] [A6.3] [A2.2]

**Build bridges, not just bonds.** Deliberately build ties across difference, not only within the in-group, or a relationship-rich group turns insular and parochial. Because a group strong enough in its own identity can afford to reach across lines, bridging widens the base, guards against cliquishness, and — for marginalized members — unlocks alliances that pure in-group solidarity never could.
flagship example: At a caucus, a white ISAIAH delegate found she had twenty supporters in her sub-caucus when the viability threshold was sixteen; she handed four of her people to a Muslim delegate so his group could become viable too — "their issue became my issue," she said, after learning about his.
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5
governs: [A4.6] [A2.4]

**Learn in loops; even a loss can build capacity.** Make reflection and debrief a built-in practice, and judge an action by what it built — leaders, relationships, confidence — not only by whether it won. Because adaptive judgment ("second-order capability") is what makes a group able to handle the next surprise, and that judgment grows only through structured reflection, a well-designed loss can leave a group stronger than a sloppy win.
flagship example: After volunteers met city officials, an AMOS organizer debriefed not only the officials' interests but the volunteers themselves: "what did you learn about how you act around people in power?" In Arizona, leaders ran a sheriff-recall campaign they expected to lose, designing it to register voters and develop leaders so they could win later.
source: Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4
governs: [A6.6] [A1.6] [A9.6]

## Layer 1 — The Overall Argument (book order)

**1. Participation feels disconnected from power.** The book opens with Arizona's SB 1070 and two mid-level organizers, Jeff and Michele, asking "What do we do?" It frames its whole inquiry as: how do ordinary people turn participation into real power when the link between the two seems broken? Sets up power, not any single win, as the object of study.
concepts: [A7.1] [A2.5]

**2. Most groups chase scale; scale is not power.** The dominant model treats collective action as the additive sum of individual actions and measures success by volume — signatures, clicks, bodies. The authors argue this mistakes resources for power and treats people as interchangeable. Sets up the alternative.
concepts: [A7.1] [A2.5] [A3.2]

**3. The prism: what's inside determines what you project.** The metaphor: a group refracts members' action into power, and the *internal design choices* (how you build people) shape the power that comes out. The key resource is a base that is independent, committed, and flexible. Sets up that internal people-building, not external tactics, is the lever.
concepts: [A7.1] [A4.1] [A3.4]

**4. What power actually is (most of this chapter is cut).** Power is relational, dynamic, and partly invisible — not a static possession. The retained kernel is Obama's framing: your power is whether you have a community that will stand behind you. Sets up that a leader's real influence rests on people who will move again and again.
concepts: [A7.1] [A1.1]

**5. Members as an independent source of power; agents vs props.** Leaders wanted power that didn't depend on elites' or funders' goodwill, so they grounded it in members instead. The authors contrast "people are power, not props" with treating people as a spigot. Sets up the agency-building that follows.
concepts: [A7.1] [A3.2] [A2.5]

**6. How you engage people now shapes what they can do later ("civic feedbacks").** It is not enough that members act; *how* they're engaged feeds back to widen or narrow what the group can do next. Engaging people as agents builds the commitment and skill that future challenges will require. Sets up the long-horizon view of leadership choices.
concepts: [A3.4] [A6.6] [A9.1]

**7. Pragmatism over purism (lighter — partly org).** Grounded in their people rather than in a fixed position, leaders could be both principled and flexible — "it's a false choice." For a group, the transferable nub is that staying anchored in members, not in a rigid stance, keeps a group adaptable.
concepts: [A2.5] [A3.4]

**8. Learning loops are the engine of strategy.** Strategy is a craft, not a formula; the groups built reflection, debriefs, and leadership development into every action, treating even losses as capacity-building. Sets up learning as a design choice, not an afterthought.
concepts: [A6.6] [A1.6]

**9. Building people I — the latticework, ritual, and story.** Members were enmeshed in a constantly expanding web of created relationships, reinforced by rituals (vision read in unison, weeklong trainings, celebrations) and the intentional sharing of personal stories. Sets up relationships as the substrate of everything else.
concepts: [A4.4] [A4.1] [A2.2]

**10. Building people II — distributed strategists.** The groups built members who could negotiate and decide on their own, rather than relying on central staff. Sets up leadership as something spread out, not concentrated.
concepts: [A9.5] [A7.3]

**11. Building people III — simultaneous commitment and flexibility.** Because members committed to *each other* rather than to a single issue, they showed unusual flexibility across candidates, issues, and campaigns. Sets up the paired construct as the core people-finding.
concepts: [A2.4] [A3.4]

**12. Building people IV — bridging identities.** The groups deliberately built collective identities that bridged across race, class, faith, and geography, guarding against insularity. Sets up bridging as strategy, not nicety.
concepts: [A4.6] [A2.4]

**13. Mutual accountability — rights and responsibilities.** Self-governance gives members voice (rights) but also obligates them to each other (responsibilities); leaders and members mutually constrain one another. Sets up accountability as the discipline that makes voice work.
concepts: [A7.5] [A8.3] [A7.2]

**14. Fragility and the tensions leadership must hold.** Power is tenuous; these groups are rare because the leadership they require — top-down *and* bottom-up, inside *and* outside, warm *and* ruthless — is hard and learnable, not born. Closes on the demands the whole model places on a leader.
concepts: [A7.6] [A9.5] [A1.4]

## Layer 2 — Concept Entries

### People are power, not props
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  People are power, not props
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Stop measuring your group's strength by headcount, sign-ups, or how much activity you can generate. The real measure is how many people are in genuine, accountable relationship with the group and with each other — people who will show up *repeatedly* when it counts. A big list you can mobilize once is not power; a smaller circle you can move again and again is. Treat members as agents who shape the work, not as bodies to be counted [A3.2].
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors borrow from John Mark Hansen: influence comes from being able to deliver people with *recurrence*, not from one-time scale. They contrast people as "props" — interchangeable units summed for effect — with people as agents, drawing on Wendy Brown's warning that reducing people to market-style units makes self-rule "incoherent." A group that engages people as props gets compliance once; a group that builds agency gets commitment over time.
- **Example in context:** An Arizona leader said he'd tell you his membership was 300, and be "super proud" of it, because he knew their children and their stories and it took ten years to develop them — Monday meetings drew 60–100. He could also cite 120,000 emails and 20,000 Facebook likes, "but those aren't real." The 300 were who held him accountable and who he could actually move.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader under pressure to show big numbers can pour energy into list-building that produces no durable power. Across all six cases, the through-line was the same: depth of relationship, not breadth of contact, was the source of leverage. The authors note repeatedly that resources and power are not the same — research finds no linear relationship between numbers (or money) and influence.
- **Related concepts:** commitment-to-people [A2.4] (what makes the relationship hold); distributed strategists [A9.5] (agency is the alternative to props); satisfaction-and-retention [A10.6] (real relationship is why people stay).
- **Source reach:** The book backs this for *political* power against elites; the transfer to a small volunteer group is sound but the eCoach should attribute the depth-over-breadth principle, not the electoral-leverage claim.

### Power is relational and earned through recurrence
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent]
situations:
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  People are power, not props
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat your authority as something earned and re-earned, not held. Power is not a thing you possess; it is a relationship — it exists only in your ability to move people in concert, and only as long as they keep choosing to move with you. The practical implication for a leader is to invest in the relationships that let you "deliver" your group repeatedly, rather than relying on title, position, or a one-time turnout.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors define power as interactional (it lives between actors) and multi-faced (much of it is invisible — agenda-setting, narratives, what people believe is possible). They open with Obama's reflection that at every level the decisive question is the same: "Do you have a community that stands behind what you stand for?" If yes, more power; if no, less. For a volunteer leader whose authority is non-positional, this is the whole game — influence rests on consent and recurrence, not compulsion [A1.1].
- **Example in context:** New Virginia Majority's Tram Nguyen commanded enough respect in the statehouse that, one official said, some legislators would carry a bill for her without even asking what it was. That standing came not from any office she held but from years of demonstrating she could reliably deliver an organized constituency.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** First-timers often assume the role itself confers authority; it doesn't. A network survey in this case found NVM — only ten years old — ranked among the most influential organizations in the state on "strategized with" and highest of all on brokerage, on par with century-old institutions. Influence was a function of relationship, not size or seniority.
- **Related concepts:** people-are-power [A7.1] (what the relationship is made of); mutual accountability [A7.5] (recurrence requires being accountable back); leading-by-earned-authority [A1.1] (the volunteer leader's case).
- **Source reach:** The statehouse mechanics route to **H:org-level**; the eCoach keeps only the relational definition of power and the earned-authority principle.

### Commitment to people, not the issue
concepts:    [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging] [A3.4 commitment-over-time]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Commit people to each other, not to the issue
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Aim to bind members to *each other and to the group's shared purpose*, not primarily to a single issue or outcome. People who have chosen to stand together will weather setbacks, pivots, and disappointments that people attached only to a specific result will not. Don't recruit single-issue purists if you can build people who have committed to the collective; the latter give you a group that can adapt.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors lean on Marshall Ganz's etymology: the root of "constituent" is *constare*, "to stand together" — distinct from "client" (one who leans on), "consumer" (one who uses up), or "citizen." A constituent is defined by a *choice to stand with others*. Drawing on Hirschman's exit/voice/loyalty, they argue loyalty to people is what keeps members exercising voice through the ups and downs, where loyalty to an outcome evaporates the moment the outcome is lost.
- **Example in context:** A PLAN leader came in through immigration (she had DACA), got invested through trainings and relationships, and now works on *mining justice* — an issue she knew nothing about and wasn't initially passionate about. Her commitment was to the people and the group, which let her move across issues entirely.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer group whose members are bonded only by one cause dissolves when that cause stalls or shifts. The authors found this commitment-to-people pattern across cases and argue it is precisely what gave these groups their unusual resilience — the opposite of the "uncompromising ideologue" stereotype the literature assumes.
- **Related concepts:** commitment-and-flexibility [A2.5] (this commitment is what *enables* flexibility); values-as-glue [A2.3] (shared purpose is the other half of the bond); group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (the felt "we" being built).
- **Source reach:** Fully supported.

### Commitment and flexibility are not opposites
concepts:    [A3.4 commitment-over-time] [A2.5 transformational-vs-transactional]
situations:  [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Commit people to each other, not to the issue
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Don't assume that a highly committed group must be a rigid one. Built right, deep commitment *produces* flexibility rather than fighting it. A group whose members are loyal to each other can change tactics, drop a losing fight, or back a second-choice option without fracturing — because the bond doesn't depend on getting any one thing. Cultivate that paired quality deliberately; it is the people-finding at the heart of the book.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The usual assumption is that commitment means ideological inflexibility. The authors invert it via Hirschman: loyalty (commitment) generates flexibility, because members who've committed to a *common good* and to one another have implicitly agreed to move together even when they'd individually choose otherwise. The flexibility is a downstream consequence ("civic feedback") of having built commitment to people rather than to policy [A6.6].
- **Example in context:** The ISAIAH faith delegates were courted daily by competing candidates, yet held together as a bloc and backed a candidate many of them hadn't personally preferred — because their commitment was to the collective, not the candidate. One delegate, asked why, said being alone "is not hopeful"; the collective was where her power and her people were.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a volunteer leader, this reframes a fear — that asking for strong commitment will make the group brittle or factional. The book's cases show the reverse: the most committed groups were the most adaptable. The authors flag the genuine risk (committed groups can turn authoritarian or insular) and name the antidote — bridging ties [A4.6].
- **Related concepts:** commitment-to-people [A2.4] (the precondition); decisions-as-collective-deliberation [A7.2] (how a flexible bloc actually decides); bridging identities [A4.6] (the guardrail against the dark side of commitment).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the "civic feedbacks" framing is the authors' and should be attributed as their concept, not stated as settled fact.

### Distributed strategists, not task-takers
concepts:    [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership] [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A7.3 autonomy-vs-direction]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Develop distributed strategists, not task-takers
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build members who can think strategically and make calls on their own, not just execute instructions. This is the book's signature move and the highest-leverage thing a leader does. It means resisting the urge to keep strategy in your own hands "because it's faster," and instead equipping members to analyze a situation, weigh options, and act when you're not in the room. Direct the *what* while leaving members real autonomy on the *how* [A7.3].
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because the decisive moments are unpredictable and can unfold anywhere, a group that routes all strategy through one person can only act where that person is. Distributing strategic capacity multiplies the points at which the group can respond well. The authors distinguish "distributed strategists" from task-oriented engagement: the former negotiate and decide; the latter wait for direction. Capacity is built by giving people genuine responsibility and the analysis to wield it.
- **Example in context:** A KFTC member kept asking the organizer to lead conversations — "isn't that your job?" — and was told "No, this is," until she could hold it. When a welfare-rights group she'd helped build got stonewalled by a chief of staff, the lead woman, with no prep, put her hands on her hips and said they should take the meeting so she could explain why they'd protested — a moment the organizer called "super powerful."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader's most common trap is becoming the bottleneck — doing or deciding everything. The cases show that members who'd been trusted with real strategic work could act with courage in unscripted moments (the student who pulled a fire alarm to enable a walkout) precisely *because* the groundwork had been laid.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes [A9.2] (the mechanism of handing it off); autonomy-vs-direction [A7.3] (what you keep vs. release); ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (why people who decide also commit).
- **Source reach:** Fully supported; this is the book's strongest and most directly transferable contribution.

### Pushing responsibility out is how people grow
concepts:    [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks] [A9.1 developing-leaders]
situations:  [S:i-cant-let-go] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Develop distributed strategists, not task-takers
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand members ownership of real outcomes — not just chores — even when they feel unready, and even when you could do it better yourself. Trusting people with meaningful responsibility, and equipping them to meet it, is what develops them. Design your meetings, events, and campaigns so that members learn *through* doing the work, rather than treating development as a separate activity.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The groups deliberately "pushed responsibility out" to widening circles of members, naming capacity-building as an explicit goal of each activity. The mechanism is learning-by-doing plus reflection: people stretch into roles they're trusted with. One leader put it as building "the people and the work" together — "they build capacity through the work," not as something separate from it.
- **Example in context:** A KFTC member-leader recalled being "trusted with responsibilities even though I was young," doing fair-housing lobbying at twenty-one despite feeling unqualified, because she had "valued lived experience." AMOS chose to send volunteer teams (not just staff) to meet officials, then debrief — sacrificing efficiency for development.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Leaders who hold everything themselves cap the group at their own capacity and burn out. The cases consistently show groups treating delegation as development: a single ISAIAH organizer activated 2,000+ voters in three months by building tiers of volunteer leaders who each recruited and developed others, rather than doing it all directly.
- **Related concepts:** distributed strategists [A9.5] (what the delegation builds toward); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (the failure mode it prevents); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (how you support the stretch).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the tiered-leadership figure is org-scale, so attribute the *principle* of building leaders who build leaders, not the specific numbers.

### The growing latticework of relationships
concepts:    [A4.4 the-relationship-web] [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium, and you build them on purpose
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat building relationships — between members, and between you and members — as the actual work, not a warm-up to it. And build them actively: don't rely only on the ties people already have; create new ones, and keep creating them. Make space at every gathering for people to connect, and turn private connections into shared public commitments.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors found the distinctive thing was not that groups drew on existing networks (everyone does) but that they *constantly generated new* horizontal ties (member–member) and vertical ties (member–leader). Citing Ganz, they note movement groups don't just use relationships, they regenerate and create them. Those ties are what make participation stick — the well-established finding that social relationships sustain involvement over time.
- **Example in context:** After Arizona's SB 1070 vigil, people who'd never met linked arms and went on to build the organizations that now anchor the state's progressive ecosystem; the durable product of the 104-day action was the *network of leaders* it created. An AMOS member who found his first congregation "cliquish" stayed elsewhere because "if you want to do something, do it… we'll find something for you" — he got enmeshed through created ties.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Low attendance and newcomers who don't stick are usually relationship problems, not logistics problems. The cases show relationship-building as the highest-return investment a group makes — the "glue that held the base together."
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1-relational-meeting [A4.2] (the unit of building ties); cohesion-and-belonging [A6.3] (what the web produces); trust-building [A4.3] (what flows along the ties).
- **Source reach:** Fully supported.

### People come for the issue but stay for the relationships
concepts:    [A6.3 cohesion-and-belonging] [A10.6 satisfaction-and-retention]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium, and you build them on purpose
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize that the issue gets people in the door, but relationships and a felt sense of belonging are what keep them. If you want retention, invest in the "we" — the family atmosphere, the trust, the sense that these are my people — not just in the cause. People stay where they feel they belong and are known.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Belonging is built through cooperation and shared experience over time, not declared. KFTC's director articulated the split directly: people *come* for an issue but *stay* for the vision and relationships. The commitments to people come to transcend the original issue commitments — "incredibly little" of the "my-thing-is-most-important" friction, in his account.
- **Example in context:** A Lutheran pastor said he'd stayed with ISAIAH largely because of leaders he'd known for "five, ten, fifteen, twenty years" whom he trusted — "you stick with people that you trust." A KFTC leader described the group as formative to her very identity as a Kentuckian and her decision to stay in the state.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader watching people drift away after an exciting launch is usually watching a belonging gap, not an interest gap. Across cases, retention tracked relationship depth; satisfaction and belonging, not issue intensity, were what held people through losses.
- **Related concepts:** the-latticework [A4.4] (where belonging is built); group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (the identity side of the felt "we"); volunteer-burnout [A10.1] (isolation is what relationships buffer).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the eCoach can state the come-for-issue/stay-for-relationships pattern as the authors' cross-case finding.

### Ritual, vision, and shared story as glue
concepts:    [A2.3 values-as-glue] [A2.2 public-narrative] [A6.1 how-norms-form-and-become-culture]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:     [H:events]
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium, and you build them on purpose
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Use shared rituals and the intentional telling of personal stories to turn a collection of people into a "we" with a felt purpose. Repeated practices — reading a vision aloud together, marking wins, an annual gathering, a regular round of story-sharing — express and reinforce members' commitment to each other and to the group's values. (Keep the *why* here; for how to actually run a story-sharing meeting or launch event, route to **H:events**.)
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Rituals act as "patterned expressions" of shared commitment; set practices harden over time into the group's culture [A6.1]. Story-sharing — especially of struggle — locates an individual's experience inside a collective one and builds the public narrative (the story of self, us, and now) that motivates action [A2.2]. The values become *manifest* through the relationships the rituals create.
- **Example in context:** KFTC opens meetings by reciting its vision statement, and opened a statewide retreat with everyone reading it aloud in unison; its director said the foundational documents are how culture is "developed and then reinforced and then sustained." ISAIAH trained hundreds of leaders to run "pain to power" house meetings that drew out personal struggles and connected them to a shared story — longtime members reported telling each other things they'd never shared in sixteen years.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that has "lost its why" or feels transactional is usually missing shared ritual and story. The authors tie this to research on religious and historic civic associations (the Elks, Masons) where joining rituals and shared practices were central to holding members across difference.
- **Related concepts:** values-as-glue [A2.3] (what the rituals carry); public-narrative [A2.2] (the story-of-self/us/now structure); how-norms-form-and-become-culture [A6.1] (rituals → culture over time).
- **Source reach:** The understanding transfers fully; the *mechanics* of running house meetings / story workshops are execution → **H:events**.

### Bridging vs bonding identities
concepts:    [A4.6 cross-cutting-ties-before-conflict] [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Build bridges, not just bonds
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build ties that reach *across* difference, not only ones that deepen the in-group. Bonding ties (within a group of similar people) create commitment, but on their own they breed insularity and cliquishness. Deliberately build bridging ties (across race, class, age, faith, geography) so the group's identity is open rather than closed — and so members come to see others' concerns as their own.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** From the social-capital literature: bonding capital strengthens shared identity; bridging capital links across divides. The authors argue a group must be strong enough in its own identity to *afford* to bridge, then must build identities that "transcend difference." Bridging is treated as strategy, not nicety — it widens the base and, for marginalized members, unlocks alliances pure in-group solidarity can't reach. It's also the named antidote to the authoritarian/insular risk of highly committed groups.
- **Example in context:** A white ISAIAH delegate gave four of her caucus votes to a Muslim delegate so his group could hit viability; learning about his concerns, she said "their issue became my issue… there was no such thing as 'this is not my problem.'" A Somali Muslim leader, bombed mosque and airport detention behind him, said the training taught him "I didn't know white people had pain" — the discovery of common humanity became the bridge.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A relationship-rich group can quietly become a clique that newcomers can't penetrate. The authors found the anchor organizations worked *intentionally* on bridging — across race especially — and saw it as essential to their power, not as optional goodwill.
- **Related concepts:** commitment-and-flexibility [A2.5] (bridging guards its dark side); group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (identities as fluid, in formation); the-relationship-web [A4.4] (bridging extends the web outward).
- **Source reach:** Fully supported; the cross-racial framing is central to the book and should be attributed.

### Learning loops: reflection as a built-in practice
concepts:    [A6.6 learning-together] [A9.6 coaching-group-members]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Learn in loops; even a loss can build capacity
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Make reflection a designed, recurring part of the work — debrief after every significant action, ask members what they learned (about the situation *and* about themselves), and feed it into the next move. Strategy is a craft built through experience and reflection, not a formula you pick once. Build the loop in deliberately; don't leave learning to chance.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors distinguish "zero-order" capabilities (operations anyone can run) from "second-order" capabilities — judgment, which can only be grown over time through reflection and is therefore the durable competitive edge. Echoing Burawoy's "instrumental" vs. "reflexive" learning, they show the groups cultivating reflexive judgment in members, not just teaching technique. Reflection after action is the engine that turns experience into adaptable judgment.
- **Example in context:** AMOS volunteers who'd met city officials debriefed in a circle on the officials' interests and power — and then on themselves: "what did you learn about how you act around people in power?" The director Troy Jackson kept three years of weekly written reflections, including standing sections on "Risks in the Past Week" and "Risks Not Taken."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that loses momentum after a launch, or can't make decisions, often lacks any structured way to learn from what just happened. The authors found that *if there was one universal practice* across all six cases, it was creating learning loops — they call it the one thing every case shared.
- **Related concepts:** even-a-loss-builds-capacity [A1.6] (the same loop reframes failure); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (reflection as how a leader grows people); decisions-as-collective-deliberation [A7.2] (reflection feeds better decisions).
- **Source reach:** Fully supported; "learning loops" is the authors' term and the most universal finding in the book.

### Even a loss can build capacity
concepts:    [A1.6 failure-as-learning] [A6.6 learning-together]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Learn in loops; even a loss can build capacity
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Judge an action by what it *built* — leaders, relationships, registered voters, confidence, a shifted sense of what's possible — not only by whether it won. Some fights are worth taking on even when you expect to lose, *if* you run them in a way that develops the group. Reframe setbacks as information and as capacity, so a loss leaves you stronger than before.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because power-building is a long, uncertain game, single outcomes are poor measures; what matters is the trajectory of the group's capacity. The authors show leaders treating defeat not as a verdict but as a designed learning input — they "only took on a fight if they could do so in a way that built their capacity." A loss processed through a learning loop adds judgment and leaders; a loss treated as failure sends people home.
- **Example in context:** Arizona leaders ran a sheriff-recall campaign they were "very, very" likely to lose, deliberately doing it so they "got more voters registered, developed more leadership, built their credentials" to take him on again. PLAN lost a mining-tax fight by one percent, but the campaign seeded the constituency capacity that later passed a $1.5 billion corporate-profits tax — "no defeat is made up entirely of defeat."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader can be crushed by a loss and read it as proof they're not cut out for the role. The cases reframe losing as a normal, even strategic, part of building — many of the book's "defeats" (SB 1070 passing, a failed recall) were the crucibles that produced the strongest later leaders.
- **Related concepts:** learning-loops [A6.6] (the mechanism that converts loss to capacity); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (holding the group through a loss); stepping-into-the-role [A1.4] (not reading a loss as personal disqualification).
- **Source reach:** Supported; frame the "run a fight you'll lose" advice carefully for a small volunteer group — the principle is capacity-over-outcome, not recklessness.

### Mutual accountability: rights and responsibilities
concepts:    [A7.5 collective-agreement-tension] [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  People are power, not props
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build a group where voice comes paired with responsibility. Giving members a real say (rights) only works if they also accept obligations to each other (responsibilities) — heeding peers, honoring shared decisions, showing up on commitments. Mutual accountability runs both ways: members hold the leader to the group's agreed vision, and members hold each other. Don't offer voice without the matching responsibility, or you get relitigation and flakiness.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Drawing on Manville and Ober's model of self-governance — a participatory structure, communal values, and practices of engagement — the authors argue self-rule is not leaders merely mirroring preferences; it's a mutual relationship of accountability that constrains everyone. In Hirschman's terms, choosing voice means choosing loyalty: to enjoy the benefits of a say, members must participate as community members, not isolated agents.
- **Example in context:** AMOS's Troy refused to cut a deal with coalition partners without first getting his members' support — knowing acting otherwise would carry "negative consequences" with his base. The "People's Platform," a values statement members publicly voted to ratify, became the tool members used to hold both the coalition *and* their own leaders accountable.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Flaky volunteers and decisions that keep getting reopened are often symptoms of voice without responsibility. The authors describe a "constant push and pull" between leaders and members — leaders building members' capacity, members then using that capacity to hold leaders to the shared vision. That tension is the discipline that makes voice productive rather than chaotic.
- **Related concepts:** peer-accountability-without-authority [A8.3] (holding each other as equals); decisions-as-collective-deliberation [A7.2] (where agreement is forged); ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (the rights side of the pairing).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the self-governance theory (Manville/Ober, Hirschman) is the authors' framing and should be attributed.

### Decisions as collective deliberation
concepts:    [A7.2 decisions-as-shared-process] [A7.5 collective-agreement-tension]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Commit people to each other, not to the issue
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat important decisions as a shared deliberation the group works through together, not as a tally of individual preferences or a directive from the top. When members have to weigh competing options and decide *as a collective* — including agreeing to support a choice that wasn't their personal first pick — the decision holds, and the group's power holds with it. Create the space and the questions for that deliberation rather than just calling a vote.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A group's strength depends on being able to act *together*; a collective that fractures into individual preferences loses its leverage. The authors show leaders posing the deliberative questions — how much power did we build, why, what do we want, will we move as a collective even if it means a choice we don't love — and then letting the group debate and decide. Deliberation, not advocacy or aggregation, is what produces a decision members will actually stand behind.
- **Example in context:** Holding 140 ISAIAH faith delegates together as a bloc — while candidates called them individually every day — took five retreats and constant conversation. Leaders convened the delegates to deliberate collectively about whether to act as one, and the delegates chose to, even when it meant individuals abandoning their preferred candidate.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that "can't make decisions" or relitigates them endlessly often hasn't built a genuine deliberative process — it's aggregating preferences or deferring to the leader. The faith-delegate case shows deliberation is hard and slow but produces a decision with real holding power, where a quick vote would have fractured.
- **Related concepts:** mutual-accountability [A7.5] (deliberation requires accepting the group's call); commitment-to-people [A2.4] (why members will back a non-preferred choice); collective-agreement-tension [A7.5] (the individual-vs-collective strain being managed).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the political-bloc context routes out, but the deliberation principle transfers directly.

### Crucible moments forge belonging and leaders
concepts:    [A6.2 stages-and-development] [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium, and you build them on purpose
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.1, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize that intense shared experiences — a crisis, a hard fight, a high-stakes action — can become turning points that forge belonging and develop leaders faster than ordinary activity. When a charged moment arrives, treat it not just as something to survive but as a chance to deepen relationships, surface new leaders, and build the group's identity. Don't waste a crucible.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because identities are "constantly in formation" rather than fixed, unsettled moments are when people most readily reinterpret who they are and what they're part of. A shared ordeal binds people to *each other* (not just to the cause) and reveals capacities that calm conditions never test. The authors describe these as moments that locate an individual's experience inside a collective one — "there's more people like me."
- **Example in context:** Arizona's 104-day vigil against SB 1070 began with seven people and became, in the authors' words, "a crucible for learning, leadership development, organization building, and strategizing" — undocumented women and students who'd never met became the core, and many now lead the state's organizations. A man dragged to a house meeting by a neighbor heard everyone's undocumented stories and thought, "there's more people like me."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Groups often lose momentum after a launch because the launch was treated as an endpoint, not a crucible. The cases show the opposite instinct — leaders mining hard moments for relationship-building and leadership development, turning a legislative *defeat* into a decade-long movement.
- **Related concepts:** stages-and-development [A6.2] (groups change across their life and crises reshape them); the-latticework [A4.4] (crucibles create dense new ties fast); even-a-loss-builds-capacity [A1.6] (a defeat can be a crucible).
- **Source reach:** Supported; the dramatic Arizona case is illustrative — most volunteer groups won't face anything this intense, so frame as "use the charged moments you do get."

### Holding the leadership tensions
concepts:    [A7.6 hierarchy-just-enough] [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership] [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  Develop distributed strategists, not task-takers
source:      Han et al. — *Prisms of the People*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Accept that leading this way means holding tensions rather than resolving them. You have to be top-down *and* bottom-up — distributing power so members develop strategic independence, while keeping just enough coordination to bring that decentralized energy into a single effort. You stay accountable to the group while still steering it; you make room for people's full humanity while still being able to act decisively. Don't expect the tension to disappear; expect to hold it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors locate the rarity of these groups partly in the *leadership* they demand: power distributed enough for distributed strategists, yet centralized enough that decentralized leadership can be marshaled when it counts. Holding only as much hierarchy as coordination requires [A7.6] is the balance point — too much and you crush agency, too little and the group can't act together. These capacities are "made, not born," learned through relationship and practice.
- **Example in context:** The book describes strategic leaders who must be able to "work inside and outside the system simultaneously," create space for people's full humanity yet be "ruthlessly strategic when the politics demand it." The recurring image is of leaders holding contradictions — warmth and rigor, structure and openness — rather than picking a side.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-timer naturally wants a clean rule (be flat, or be in charge); the cases show neither pole works alone. The authors note relational leadership also carries real interpersonal and emotional risk — naming this as hard and learnable, not a personal failing, helps a leader stay in the role.
- **Related concepts:** distributed strategists [A9.5] (the bottom-up half); functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (leadership as functions met by whoever can); failure-as-learning [A1.6] (the craft is grown, not innate).
- **Source reach:** The book frames this at org-leadership altitude; **H:org-level** carries the program-design slice, while the eCoach keeps the "hold the tensions" stance for a single group leader.
