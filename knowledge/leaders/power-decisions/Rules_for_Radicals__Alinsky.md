# _RAGSRC__ Rules for Radicals (Alinsky) — Source Summary for the Group eCoach RAG

**Source:** Saul D. Alinsky, *Rules for Radicals: A Practical Primer for Realistic Radicals* (Random House/Vintage, 1971; Knopf Doubleday ebook ed.)
**Register:** Group eCoach Tag Register (10 domains · 60 leaves · 22 situations · 6 handoffs)
**Audience:** first-time volunteer leader of a small group · **Gates applied:** leader-not-org · understanding-not-execution · minus-irrelevant
**Depth/quote policy:** verbatim quotes are short (well under 15 words), attributed, one per entry, used only where exact wording carries the concept. Paraphrase otherwise; this is a retrieval aid, not a substitute for the book.
**Coverage note — read first:** Alinsky is a *foundational-concepts* source and an explicit *route-out* source (corpus spec: `A7.1, A4 (route-out)`), not a coverage source. *Rules for Radicals* is a ~61k-word manual for building **mass organizations to take power from an external enemy** — an org-level, adversarial, tactical altitude. Both gates cut hard: **leader-not-org** removes the "organize a community against the establishment" frame; **understanding-not-execution** routes the famous material (the 13 power-tactics, the symphony/O'Hare/watermelon/gum actions, the means-and-ends realpolitik) out to `H:org-level` / `H:events` / `H:comms`. What remains is a concentrated set of *organizing* ideas a first-timer can use — what power is and where a group's power comes from; the leader as catalyst who fades; communicating inside people's experience and self-interest; participation as the point. The summary therefore runs ~6% of source **by design** (gates, not thinning); the 15–25% rule is a proxy for "transformative and non-substituting," which a heavily-routed foundational source satisfies far below that band.
**Standing Source-reach caveat (applies doc-wide):** Alinsky's frame is the *organizer of a mass organization fighting an external power-holder*, not a peer leading a small volunteer team. Where his point is about the adversarial fight, the *organizing principle* transfers to a 3–7-person group but the *combat framing* does not; entries say so rather than overclaiming. His richest small-scale illustrations — the guided-questions dialogue, the Back-of-the-Yards confidence-building, the sewing machines — are used as flagships wherever they fit.

## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

The load-bearing claims the relevant slice rests on — the baselines a coach returns to across a whole class of problems. Each is individually retrievable and citable; Layer 2 entries point here by name via `anchors to:`.

**Power is the leader's business, and it isn't a dirty word.** Power is organized energy — the capacity to act — neither good nor evil in itself; flinching from the word doesn't make a leader virtuous, it leaves them unable to name where their group's strength comes from. Because *power*, like *self-interest* and *conflict*, triggers a reflexive recoil, leaders swap in soft synonyms that quietly change the meaning and dull their own thinking.
flagship example: Alinsky devotes a whole chapter to rehabilitating "tainted" words, arguing that substituting "harnessing energy" for "power" leaves you "averse to thinking in vigorous, simple, honest terms" — an aseptic imitation of the real thing.
source:  Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "A Word About Words"
governs: [A7.1] [A1.4]

> verbatim: "To know power and not fear it is essential to its constructive use." — Alinsky

**A group's power is its organized people — nothing else.** Change comes from power, and power comes from organization; for a group with no money, the one real resource is people acting together, so building and holding that membership *is* the work, not a precondition to it.
flagship example: Alinsky's flat formula — a church, a political party, and a union all exist for the same single reason: organized strength for a common purpose.
source:  "In the Beginning"
governs: [A7.1] [A4.1] [A2.4]

> verbatim: "Power and organization are one and the same." — Alinsky

**Build power for others to use — the leader is a catalyst, not the hero.** The whole point is to create capacity *others* wield, then get out of the way; the leader who needs the power for themselves stunts the group and becomes its ceiling. The catalyst deliberately fades, and the measure of success is being needed less over time.
flagship example: Alinsky's sharp contrast — the leader is "driven by the desire for power," the organizer "by the desire to create"; the organizer's job is weaning the group off any dependency on him, and once that is done, his job is done.
source:  "The Education of an Organizer," "Communication"
governs: [A1.2] [A9.3] [A9.4]

> verbatim: "The organizer finds his goal in creation of power for others to use." — Alinsky

**Meet people inside their own experience and self-interest, not your ideals.** Both communication and commitment run through the other person's lived reality; an appeal pitched to altruism or to your values lands as noise. The leader's first move is to learn the other person's world and enter it, not to broadcast their own.
flagship example: Alinsky never approached clergy through the Sermon on the Mount — only through their own self-interest and their church's concrete welfare; the moralistic pitch gets you politely shown the door the moment you leave.
source:  "Communication"
governs: [A4.3] [A3.2]

> verbatim: "People only understand things in terms of their experience." — Alinsky

**Participation is the point; policy follows power.** Don't do *for* people what builds their power to do for themselves — a gift handed down without participation takes dignity rather than gives it. And people who feel powerless don't even think about their problems, so the act of building real capacity to change something is what makes people start asking the right questions.
flagship example: Mexico returned pawned sewing machines as a Mother's Day gift; within three weeks the identical number were back in the pawnshop. The thing given without a fight for it didn't hold.
source:  "In the Beginning"
governs: [A3.2] [A1.2] [A7.1]

> verbatim: "the dignity of the individual" — Alinsky

## Layer 1 — The Overall Argument

Alinsky's spine, in his order, with the out-of-scope arenas (the means-and-ends ethics, the tactics chapters, the revolution/Have-Not framing) cut and logged in Excluded. Paraphrased throughout.

**1. The purpose.** Alinsky opens by addressing the powerless on how to *get* power, insisting you must start from "the world as it is," not as you wish it — and warning against the "Do-Nothings" who block action with "I agree with your ends but not your means." This installs realism and a bias toward action as the ground for everything after. *(Most of this chapter — the revolution thesis, the Have/Have-Not/Have-a-Little trinity — is org/political and Excluded; the realism + Do-Nothings thread is what carries forward.)*
concepts: [A1.6 failure-as-learning] [A7.1 building-and-using-power]

**2. A word about words.** Alinsky rehabilitates the words a polite culture flinches from — *power, self-interest, compromise, ego, conflict* — arguing that dodging them dulls the leader's grasp of reality. This is the conceptual core: you cannot lead a group's strength if you cannot think clearly about power and interest.
concepts: [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A3.2 ownership-and-voice] [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]

**3. The education of an organizer.** Having reclaimed the vocabulary, Alinsky describes the person who does the work and draws the book's pivotal distinction: the *leader* wants power; the *organizer* wants to create power for others. This sets up the whole enabling-not-doing, distribute-leadership stance.
concepts: [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing] [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck] [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]

**4. Communication.** Alinsky argues you can only communicate — and only build commitment — inside the other person's experience; the chapter turns this into craft (listening, guided questions, weaning the group off the leader). This operationalizes "meet people where they are."
concepts: [A4.3 trust-building] [A9.6 coaching-group-members] [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]

**5. In the beginning.** Finally, entering a group from scratch: earning a license to lead, breaking through rationalizations, the policy-follows-power insight, and "the dignity of the individual" as the purpose that participation serves. This is where the abstract claims become a starting playbook for a new leader.
concepts: [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent] [A3.2 ownership-and-voice] [A7.1 building-and-using-power]

**6. Tactics / The Genesis of Tactic Proxy / The Way Ahead.** Specific power-tactics against an external enemy. → **Excluded / route-out** (`H:org-level`, `H:events`, `H:comms`): this is execution, and adversarial-org execution at that — out of altitude on both gates.

## Layer 2 — Concept Entries

### Power is not a dirty word
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Power is the leader's business
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "A Word About Words"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat *power* as a neutral, necessary word and a normal part of leading — not something to apologize for or rename. Don't reach for softer substitutes ("influence," "energy," "empowerment") in the hope of sounding nicer; the substitution quietly changes what you mean and weakens your grip on the real question, which is where your group's strength comes from and how to grow it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's point is semantic and psychological: a culture that codes power, self-interest, and conflict as sinister trains people to flinch from naming them, and "striving to avoid the force, vigor, and simplicity" of the word makes you averse to thinking clearly about the thing [A8.2]. Power is simply the capacity to act; the only real choice is between organized and unorganized power, not between power and purity.
- **Example in context:** He marshals Hamilton, Pascal, and St. Ignatius to show that everyone who actually moved history used the plain word — and notes that even Lord Acton's famous line is routinely misquoted ("power corrupts" for "power *tends* to corrupt"), because the conditioned mind can't read it straight.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time volunteer leader who is squeamish about "power" tends to under-ask, under-organize, and mistake niceness for strength. Naming power lets you ask the useful question — what can this group actually make happen, and what would make it able to make more happen.
- **Related concepts:** group's-power-is-its-people [A7.1] (what the power actually consists of); ego-not-egotism [A1.4] (the inner confidence to claim a role); conflict-as-engine [A8.2] (the linked word a polite culture also dodges).
- **Source reach:** Alinsky's worked cases are all power *against an enemy*; the reclamation-of-the-word argument transfers cleanly, the combat application does not — attribute the principle, not the fight.

> verbatim: "Power is the very essence, the dynamo of life." — Alinsky

### A group's power is its organized people
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  A group's power is its organized people
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand that your group's only real resource is people acting together, so growing and holding that membership is not a chore that precedes the work — it *is* the work. Don't treat recruitment and turnout as logistics to get past before the "real" organizing; the organized people are the power. *(How-to recruiting/turnout → H:recruitment.)*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky collapses any gap between organization and power: a church, a party, and a union all exist for the one reason of organized strength toward a common purpose [A2.1]. Change requires power, and for those without money, power is numbers brought into coordinated action — so the question behind every move is whether it adds to, or subtracts from, that organized base.
- **Example in context:** No in-context small-group example given; Alinsky's illustration is structural (the church/party/union parallel) rather than a single anecdote.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a leader fretting that "people aren't showing up," this reframes attendance as the substance, not a symptom to fix on the side: a group losing members is losing power, and rebuilding the base is the central act, not a distraction from it.
- **Related concepts:** power-is-not-a-dirty-word [A7.1] (what "power" here means); relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (the ties that hold the base together); policy-follows-power [A7.1] (why a powerless group can't even think straight).
- **Source reach:** Alinsky writes at mass-organization scale; the "power = organized people" claim is fully attributable, but a volunteer leader's "organization" is a handful of people, so soften any implication that scale itself is the goal.

> verbatim: "Change comes from power, and power comes from organization." — Alinsky

### The catalyst who fades: organizer vs leader
concepts:    [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing] [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:
anchors to:  Build power for others to use
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "The Education of an Organizer," "Communication"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Aim to build capacity that *other people* carry, and treat your own gradual irrelevance as the goal, not a loss. Don't measure yourself by how central you are; measure yourself by how much the group can do without you. The catalyst sets things in motion and then steps back.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's pivotal distinction is between the leader, "driven by the desire for power," and the organizer, "driven by the desire to create" — to make power others use [A9.5]. The mechanism is deliberate withdrawal: the organizer recedes from the decision-making circle as members grow, so that dependency on any one person never hardens into the group's single point of failure [A9.4].
- **Example in context:** He describes his own arc inside a group — beginning "the general," knowing the moves but never wearing the stars, then steadily handing decisions back until the group runs itself and his work is finished.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the antidote to the founder who quietly becomes the bottleneck: a leader who can't fade keeps the group small and fragile, because everything routes through them and nothing survives their absence [A9.3].
- **Related concepts:** guided-questions [A9.6] (the everyday craft of fading); be-the-shield [A9.1] (the early-stage inverse — out front before you step back); developing-leaders [A9.1] (who picks up what you set down).
- **Source reach:** Strong and directly transferable; Alinsky's "organizer" is a paid professional, so for a volunteer peer-leader read it as a stance ("create, don't accumulate"), not a job description.

> verbatim: "weaning the group away from any dependency upon him" — Alinsky

### Guided questions, not answers
concepts:    [A9.6 coaching-group-members] [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Build power for others to use
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "Communication"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Move a group toward good decisions by asking, not telling — surface the weaknesses of bad options and the merits of good ones through questions, so the choice is genuinely theirs. Resist the pull to "explain" and issue the answer, even when you're sure you have it; the answer that arrives as an order builds resentment, the one the group reaches builds ownership.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky lays out a Socratic exchange in which the organizer, convinced of a course, never names it — instead probing each proposed tactic with questions until the group itself lands there [A3.2]. He's candid that this is a form of manipulation, "just as a teacher manipulates," and that its legitimacy rests entirely on it being education toward the group's own judgment, not a trick to extract a predetermined result — including the discipline to let the group be *right* when their answer beats yours.
- **Example in context:** His scripted dialogue (Organizer + Leaders 1–3) shows a preferred tactic being talked *out of* the room by the members themselves through questioning, with no one losing face — and he adds the honest coda: sometimes the questions reveal that your own pre-chosen answer was wrong.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For "no one takes initiative," this is the mechanism: people who are handed conclusions stay passive, while people walked through the reasoning start owning it and, over time, stop needing the walk-through at all.
- **Related concepts:** the-catalyst-who-fades [A1.2] (questions are how the fade happens); participation-is-the-point [A3.2] (why the group must reach it themselves); communicate-within-experience [A4.3] (the questions have to start in their world).
- **Source reach:** The "manipulation" framing is Alinsky's and worth handling honestly — present it as facilitation toward the group's own judgment, which is what his own caveat ("let someone else have the answer") points to.

> verbatim: "people have to make their own decisions" — Alinsky

### Be the shield early, give away the credit
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Build power for others to use
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** In the early days, take the exposed position yourself — absorb the risk and the blame — and route the credit to members; then, as the group's confidence grows, deliberately let them step out front to take both. Don't hoard the visible role, and don't leave beginners exposed before they have the backing to handle it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's image is the organizer as "the septic tank in the early stages" — if it goes wrong it's his fault, if it goes right the credit is the locals' [A1.3]. The mechanism is a confidence ladder: shielding people from consequences they can't yet bear lets them act at all, and as power accrues the risks shrink, so the leader can progressively hand the front line over [A9.4].
- **Example in context:** No single anecdote; the principle is stated as a rule of the opening phase, paired with his observation that early on the establishment can still get someone fired or evicted, which is exactly why the leader stands in front first.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-timer who grabs the credit kills initiative; one who hides behind beginners burns them. The sequence — shield first, cede later — is how members move from spectators to leaders without being thrown in over their heads.
- **Related concepts:** the-catalyst-who-fades [A1.2] (the later half of the same arc); developing-leaders [A9.1] (what the shielding is *for*); lead-by-example [A1.3] (taking the risk yourself models it).
- **Source reach:** Alinsky's "risk" is jobs and evictions in a power fight; for a volunteer group read it at proportionate stakes (taking the awkward ask, owning the failed event), not literal danger.

> verbatim: "he gets all the shit" — Alinsky

### Communicate within their experience
concepts:    [A4.3 trust-building] [A4.2 the-1:1-relational-meeting]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Meet people inside their own experience
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "Communication"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build understanding and trust by getting inside the other person's experience first — learn their world, listen, and pitch everything in terms they've actually lived. Communication is two-way: if you talk past someone's experience, or ignore what they tell you, you aren't communicating regardless of how right or eloquent you are. *(Channels, tooling, content production → H:comms.)*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's claim is that people only take in what connects to their own lived reality, so the organizer must "get within their experience" — and where that experience offers no point of contact, you sometimes have to *create* an experience to make the idea land [A9.6]. Relationship is the gate: sensitive things simply aren't heard until there's a tie strong enough to carry them.
- **Example in context:** The chicken-liver-omelet and the give-away-a-$10-bill demonstrations: by stepping just outside a waiter's or a stranger's frame, Alinsky shows his trainees, concretely, how communication collapses the instant you leave the other person's experience.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a group that "feels transactional," this is the fix that isn't a script: relationships warm when people feel met in their own terms, and a leader who leads with their own values or jargon stays a stranger no matter how often they show up.
- **Related concepts:** self-interest-moves-people [A3.2] (the specific thing to listen for); guided-questions [A9.6] (communication as asking, not telling); trust-as-the-gate (sensitive asks wait for the relationship).
- **Source reach:** Fully transferable as a relational principle; Alinsky's examples are an organizer entering a strange community, so for a peer leader it reads as "lead with their world, not yours."

> verbatim: "People only understand things in terms of their experience." — Alinsky

### Self-interest, not altruism, moves people
concepts:    [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Meet people inside their own experience
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "Communication," "A Word About Words"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Ask people in terms of what genuinely matters to *them*, not in terms of your cause or an appeal to their better nature. Don't assume people will show up for the mission in the abstract; find the real stake each person has and connect the work to it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky treats self-interest as a plain fact of motivation, not a flaw — and notes it's fluid, shifting as circumstances change, so the leader's job is to read the *current* live interest rather than a fixed one. He's blunt that he reaches clergy through their church's welfare, never through scripture, because the moral appeal sits outside their operative interest and so does nothing.
- **Example in context:** No single small-group anecdote; the worked illustration is his standing practice with religious leaders — self-interest in, moralism out.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For attendance and "transactional"-feeling groups, this is the diagnostic: people drift when the work stops connecting to anything they actually want, and re-engagement starts from their stake, not a guilt trip.
- **Related concepts:** many-issues-many-members [A3.4] (self-interest varies person to person); communicate-within-experience [A4.3] (the same "enter their world" move); participation-is-the-point [A3.2] (interest deepens into ownership through acting).
- **Source reach:** Use with care: Alinsky's bare "self-interest" can read as transactional, and it sits in tension with Pink's intrinsic motivation [A3.1] and the transactional-vs-relational-ask lens [A3.3]. The honest synthesis — start from real interest, then build it into shared ownership — is the coachable version; attribute the raw claim to Alinsky and don't let it crowd out the relational frame.

> verbatim: "I approach them on the basis of their own self-interest." — Alinsky

### Many issues, many members
concepts:    [A3.4 commitment-over-time]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:group-lost-its-why]
handoff:
anchors to:  Meet people inside their own experience
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "The Education of an Organizer," "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize that each person carries their own hierarchy of priorities, so a group built on a single issue stays small and short-lived. Don't expect everyone to care equally about the one thing you care most about; a durable group holds enough live concerns that different people each find their own reason to act.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky argues a single issue is "a fatal strait jacket": it limits appeal to the few who rank that issue first, and it guarantees a lull in action — and a group, like a person, dies without action [A6.4]. Multiple issues mean continuous activity and a reason for many different people to stay in.
- **Example in context:** His block of neighbors — one fighting urban renewal to save his house, one focused on drug pushers and her kids' schools, one on welfare income, one on consumer prices — each turning out hard for *their* No. 1 priority and only lightly for the others.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For low attendance or a group that's "lost its why," this reframes the problem: the group may be running on one concern that only a few people rank first, and breadth (more than a sharper pitch on the single issue) is what brings people back.
- **Related concepts:** self-interest-moves-people [A3.2] (each priority *is* a self-interest); a-group's-power-is-its-people [A7.1] (breadth is how the base grows); commitment-over-time [A3.4] (continuous action keeps people in).
- **Source reach:** Alinsky's unit is a multi-issue mass organization; a small single-purpose volunteer team is a real and valid form, so transfer the *insight* (people rank differently; sustained action matters) without implying every group must broaden its mission.

> verbatim: "Many issues mean many members." — Alinsky

### Participation is the point: the dignity of the individual
concepts:    [A3.2 ownership-and-voice] [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Participation is the point
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Never do *for* people what builds their capacity to do for themselves — and don't mistake delivering outcomes *to* members for serving them. Helping people while denying them a real part in the action takes their dignity rather than gives, and produces nothing that lasts.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's compass is "the dignity of the individual": self-respect comes only from playing an active role in solving one's own problem, so a benefit handed down without participation has nothing holding it up [A9.2]. He's frank that the hard part is the leader's own surrender of the quiet belief that they know best.
- **Example in context:** Mexico returned pawned sewing machines as a Mother's Day gift; within three weeks the same number were back in the pawnshop — the unearned gift didn't hold. He pairs it with Liberia, "given" freedom it never had to fight for, and reads its political sterility from that.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the deep why under enabling-not-doing: a leader who solves everything for members keeps them passive and the work fragile, while one who builds their part in it grows both the people and the group's durability.
- **Related concepts:** the-catalyst-who-fades [A1.2] (participation is what you fade *into*); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (a real part means real ownership); policy-follows-power [A7.1] (capacity to act is what unlocks engagement).
- **Source reach:** Directly transferable; the principle holds at any scale even though Alinsky's cases are national.

> verbatim: "the dignity of the individual" — Alinsky

### Policy follows power: the powerless don't engage
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Participation is the point
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand that people don't think hard about problems they feel powerless to change — so apparent apathy is often just an accurate read that nothing can be done. The lever is not to lecture people into caring; it's to give them genuine capacity to act, after which the engagement, the questions, and the competence appear on their own.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky's formula is "policy follows power": absent any means to change a thing, thinking about how to change it is pointless fantasy, so people don't — what looks like resignation is rational [A6.4]. Once a real instrument of power exists, the same people start asking sharp, specific questions and seeking expertise, because now there's a reason to know. (His complementary point on apathy — that an organizer surfaces latent dissatisfaction so people will act — sits here too, but its agitate-against-the-enemy framing is org-level; keep only the diagnostic.)
- **Example in context:** Ask a powerless person what's wrong with the schools and you get clichés and a shrug; build an organization with real leverage and the same people start asking what makes a good teacher, why communication fails, how to fix it.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For "no one takes initiative," this redirects the leader from motivation-talk to capability: people step up when acting can actually change something, so the question is whether your group gives members a real handle on outcomes, not whether they care enough.
- **Related concepts:** participation-is-the-point [A3.2] (capacity is what makes participation real); a-group's-power-is-its-people [A7.1] (where the capacity comes from); common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (apathy as a symptom of powerlessness, not character).
- **Source reach:** The diagnostic transfers cleanly; Alinsky's prescription (manufacture controversy, "rub raw the resentments") is adversarial-org and routes out — and the conflict-as-engine claim is held more squarely by Burns [A8.2], so lean on Burns there.

> verbatim: "policy follows power" — Alinsky

### Ego, not egotism: grounded confidence
concepts:    [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role] [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter] [S:scared-to-ask-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Power is the leader's business
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "A Word About Words," "The Education of an Organizer"

- **Claim / recommendation:** Cultivate grounded confidence that the group can succeed — and keep it strictly separate from arrogance. *Ego* in Alinsky's sense is unselfconscious belief in your ability to do what must be done; *egotism* is the vanity and impatience that repels people. Start from where you actually are ("the world as it is"), not from a wished-for version of yourself or the group.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky insists that without the conviction you can win, "the battle is lost before it is even begun" — and that this confidence has to be real, because no contrived humility can hide an underlying arrogance, and nothing alienates people faster than flashes of vanity and contempt. Egotism, he notes, is mostly a defensive reaction to *felt inadequacy*; settled confidence has no need to perform.
- **Example in context:** He recalls trained organizers sent to a new city of 100,000 who "take one look and promptly wire in a resignation" — the failure is confidence, not skill; the real organizer can look at the same task and believe it's doable.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For the imposter feeling and the fear of asking, this separates the useful thing (quiet belief you can do the role) from the thing to avoid (arrogance), and locates the block where it usually is — in shaky self-belief, which the work itself, started from reality, gradually firms up.
- **Related concepts:** stepping-into-the-role [A1.4] (claiming the identity); power-is-not-a-dirty-word [A7.1] (confidence about the group's strength); fear-of-asking-or-delegating [A1.5] (what wobbly confidence produces).
- **Source reach:** Alinsky's "monumental ego" language is pitched for a lone professional taking on corporations; for a volunteer peer-leader, scale it down to "settled, non-defensive confidence" — the egotism/ego distinction is the durable part.

> verbatim: "Ego is unreserved confidence in one's ability." — Alinsky

### Rationalizations vs real reasons
concepts:    [A6.4 common-stall-patterns]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Participation is the point
source:      Alinsky — *Rules for Radicals*, "In the Beginning"

- **Claim / recommendation:** When a group offers reasons it can't move, treat them as rationalizations to see past, not problems to solve. Don't get drawn into debating the surface excuse as though it were the real obstacle — recognize it as a defense and work through to what's actually blocking action.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Alinsky borrows the therapeutic frame: people throw up "defenses" — often a subconscious justification for not having acted sooner — and chasing each one on its own terms is futile [A1.6]. The skill is to name the defense as a defense and break through, rather than spending the conversation arguing about a reason that was never the point.
- **Example in context:** His exchange with Canadian Indian leaders who said they "can't organize" because of "creative fishing" and "creative welfare" — Alinsky bluntly refused to chase the rhetoric ("I think that that's just a pile of shit"), which broke the loop and got them to the real issue; one leader later said it was the first time a white man had talked to them as equals.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a group that's stalled, this stops the leader from getting trapped litigating excuses ("we're too busy," "that's not how we do things"). Naming the pattern gently and moving past it is faster than refuting each reason, which only generates the next one.
- **Related concepts:** common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (rationalization as a recognizable stall); policy-follows-power [A7.1] (powerlessness is often the reason under the excuse); failure-as-learning [A1.6] (the guilt the defenses cover).
- **Source reach:** The diagnostic transfers; Alinsky's confrontational delivery (calculated profanity) was a specific judgment call inside an established relationship — present the *recognize-and-break-through* move, not the bluntness as technique.

> verbatim: "Chasing rationalizations is like attempting to find the rainbow." — Alinsky

## Excluded from this summary

Cut as out of scope for a first-time leader of a small distributed-organizing group, and logged so the choice is reviewable. The dominant reasons are **wrong altitude** (mass-organization / fighting an external power-holder, not leading a peer team) and **execution** (specific tactics), both routed out per the corpus spec's `route-out` designation.

- **"Of Means and Ends" (entire chapter)** — the eleven rules on the ethics of means and ends. Excluded in full per direction: it is an ethics of *adversarial political action* (war, revolution, the Resistance, Hiroshima, Gandhi's pragmatism), out of altitude for a volunteer group leader, and it sits in real tension with Tectonica's designed-friction / build-the-leader's-judgment stance. The only adjacent ideas kept are "start from the world as it is" (folded into "Ego, not egotism") and the "Do-Nothings" critique (noted in Layer 1).
- **"Tactics," "The Genesis of Tactic Proxy," "The Way Ahead" (entire chapters)** — the 13 power-tactics rules and the worked actions (the Rochester symphony "shit-in," the O'Hare airport action, the watermelon and bubble-gum tactics, the slum-landlord suburban picketing, "pick the target, freeze it, personalize it, polarize it"). All execution, and adversarial-org execution against an external enemy. → `H:org-level`, `H:events`, `H:comms`. Per direction, the why-level tactical axioms ("power is what the enemy thinks you have," "the threat is more terrifying than the thing itself," "a good tactic is one your people enjoy") are **not** carried as entries — the organizing concepts are what this RAG needs from Alinsky, not the tactics.
- **The revolution thesis and the Have / Have-Not / Have-a-Little "trinity"** (Prologue, "The Purpose") — a macro-political theory of social change and class; org/movement altitude with no small-group mechanism.
- **The "political schizoid" / polarize-to-100% material** ("Education of an Organizer," "Tactics") — a combat posture for mobilizing against an enemy; not how a peer leads a small group, and at odds with the designed-friction stance.
- **The community-organizing-against-the-establishment frame throughout** — earning a "license to operate" by baiting the establishment into branding you "dangerous," "community disorganization as the first step," agitating to manufacture controversy. The *diagnostic* under it (powerlessness reads as apathy) is kept; the agitate-the-enemy *prescription* is org-level and routed out, and the conflict-as-engine claim is held more squarely by Burns [A8.2].
