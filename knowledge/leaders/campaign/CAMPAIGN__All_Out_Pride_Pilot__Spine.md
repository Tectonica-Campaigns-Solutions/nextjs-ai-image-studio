# **Part I — The Campaign**

## **What this is**

A one-month, group-based fundraising pilot for All Out — the global movement fighting for LGBT+ rights, for love and equality, everywhere. Thirty members across the U.S. and UK are each founding a small fundraising group in their own community: naming it, recruiting it, deciding together how it raises, and running a fundraiser during Pride season. The pilot runs from mid-June through **July 15**. All money raised must be in by then.

Every group leader in this campaign is one of those thirty. They answered an invitation to do something different this Pride — not just march, not just post, not just donate, but build something with other people. Most have never organized or fundraised before. That is by design, and it is fine. The promise made to them was: *you only need to know how to type*. The coach's job is to keep that promise.

## **The shape of it**

Three movements, compressed into four weeks:

1. **Form a group fast.** Find your people, ask them, get them committed, get them in a room (or on a call).  
2. **Decide together.** Name the group, agree what you're doing and how, plan the fundraiser as a group.  
3. **Raise the money.** Run the fundraiser, coordinate, follow through, finish strong by July 15\.

A useful truth that governs the whole arc: **most of a group's outcome is decided before the fundraising begins.** The recruiting, the founding meeting, the agreements made together — that's where the money is actually raised. The fundraiser itself just collects it.

## **The goal**

**Each group aims to raise at least $2,000 (or £2,000).** This is a steer, not a contract — but it is a serious number worth taking seriously, because of what it represents: in many of the places All Out works, £2,000 can keep an LGBT+ shelter open for a month. Everything raised is unrestricted and goes directly to All Out's global fight — overturning unjust sentences, keeping shelters open, defending Pride where it is banned, fighting anti-LGBT+ laws — wherever the need is greatest.

The money matters because the moment is hard: more countries than ever criminalise being LGBT+, trans rights are being rolled back in the U.S. and UK, "propaganda" laws are spreading across Eastern Europe, and funding cuts have left frontline groups facing impossible choices. All Out itself is a team of just 13 people. The strength of this movement has never come from big institutions. It comes from members.

## **The goal behind the goal**

This campaign has a second objective that is just as real: **give people a chance to stand up for one another, together.** The frame is *we are one* — one community around the world, made of many communities, that sticks together and fights for each other. A group that organizes, raises money, and holds each other accountable generates a kind of power that a thousand individuals acting alone cannot. If a group ends the month with $2,000 raised *and* a set of relationships that didn't exist in May — people who now know they can do things together — the pilot has done its real work.

So the coach never treats the group as merely a fundraising machine. The group itself is the point.

## **The leader's role**

The leader is a **facilitator and convener, not the doer of everything**. Their job is to bring people together, help the group decide, make sure agreements are kept, and keep everyone connected to the purpose. The expected commitment is 4–8 hours a week — and the surest way to blow past that is to start doing the group's work *for* it. When a leader starts carrying everything, that is a signal to redistribute, not a virtue.

The leader decides how to lead. There is no fixed role definition — leaders can define and even name their own role. What the campaign does ask of every leader: bring significant plans to the group for input and consent before acting on them, and involve the group in shaping anything it will be asked to carry out. People commit to what they helped build.

## **How time works**

Four weeks compresses everything and forgives a lot. There is no time for a perfect plan, a complete group, or a polished launch — and none of those are needed. The sequence below looks linear; in practice the early stages overlap and should. A leader can be inviting people while scheduling the first meeting, and recruiting can continue after the group has launched. Speed beats polish at every stage. The one thing the calendar will not forgive is waiting: a group that hasn't met by the end of week one is spending money-raising days on logistics.

A rough arc:

* **Week 1:** find your people, make the asks, get commitments, schedule and hold the first meeting.  
* **Week 2:** founding decisions, fundraising plan made together, work divided, fundraiser launched or imminent.  
* **Weeks 2–4:** run the fundraising activities. Coordinate, communicate, adjust.  
* **Final days:** last push, money in by July 15, thank everyone, close the loop.

# **Part II — The Stages**

Eight stages. Each one says what it's for, what to actually do, the advice that matters, where leaders predictably get stuck, how to know it's done, and where the system picks up the execution. **A leader can enter at any stage** — the coach meets them where they are.

---

## **Stage 1 — Identify your people**

### **What this stage is for**

Everything downstream — the energy of the meetings, the follow-through on tasks, whether the fundraiser actually happens — is set by who is in the group. This stage is about choosing deliberately rather than broadcasting and hoping. The group being assembled is small: **about 5–10 people plus the leader**. At that size, every person shapes the whole.

### **What you actually do**

* Make an actual list of names. Not categories — names. Friends, colleagues, neighbours, people from other parts of your life. Write down more people than you think you need.  
* For each person, ask: would I genuinely want to work with them for a month? Do they care — about LGBT+ rights, about this community, about you? Would they actually show up?  
* Notice who brings something extra: people who know other people, people with a useful skill (hosting, baking, design, spreadsheets, being funny on the internet), people whose yes makes other yeses likelier.  
* Decide your reach-out approach (see Key advice) and start moving down the list. Do not wait until the list is perfect.

### **Key advice**

* **Invite more people than you need — significantly more.** Some people will say no. Some will say yes and drift. Expect roughly half to two-thirds of interested people to fall away between "sounds great" and sustained participation. A working rule: **invite two to three times the number you want to end up with.** To land a group of 8, reach out to 16–24 people. This is normal attrition, not failure.  
* **Two valid approaches — pick one consciously.** *The wide net:* contact many people quickly, see who responds, build from the responders. Faster, lower effort per person, more drift. *The deep ask:* choose fewer people and invest real time in each — a conversation, not a message. Slower per person, but people brought in this way show up more and stay longer. Most leaders should blend: deep asks for the 4–5 people they most want, a wider net around them.  
* **Recruit through relationships, not lists.** The people most likely to commit are people with whom there is already some thread of relationship — and the ask works best when it connects to what *they* care about, not only what you need.  
* **Don't build a club of people identical to you.** It's natural to list only your closest circle. Push one ring wider — the colleague, the neighbour, the friend-of-a-friend. Groups drawn from slightly different worlds reach more people when it's time to fundraise, and they're more interesting rooms to be in.  
* **Reliability beats enthusiasm.** A quietly dependable person who answers messages is worth more over four weeks than a wildly excited person who vanishes. Best of all is someone with both — but when in doubt, weight follow-through.  
* **People who seem "flaky" often just haven't been given anything real to do yet.** Don't write someone off for past flakiness in low-stakes things; do notice patterns in things that mattered.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **"I don't know who to ask."** Almost always means the list-making hasn't physically happened. Walk through life domains one at a time: who do you text? Who do you work with? Who came to the last thing you hosted? Who posts about these issues? Ten names exist; they need extracting.  
* **"I only know 4 people who'd do this."** Fine — start with 4 deep asks and ask each of *them* who they'd bring. A group of 5 committed people beats a group of 12 maybes. The 5–10 range has a bottom for a reason.  
* **"What if people say no?"** They will, and it costs nothing. A no to this invitation is not a judgment of the leader. Keep the list long enough that any single no doesn't matter.  
* **Perfectionism about the list.** Two days spent optimizing who to ask is two days not asking. The list needs to be good, not complete.

### **You're done when…**

You have a written list of 2–3× your target group size, you've chosen your approach, and you've started reaching out — *started*, not finished. Stage 2 begins with the first ask, while this list keeps growing in the background.

### **Where the system helps**

The coach helps build the list, think through fit, and choose the approach. For deeper recruitment craft — outreach channels, messaging at scale — the **Recruitment eCoach** is the future home; in this pilot, the coach provides what's needed and Tectonica's drop-in sessions cover the rest.

## **Stage 2 — Make the ask**

### **What this stage is for**

An invitation exists now: *join my group, raise money with me for the global fight for LGBT+ rights, one month, Pride season.* This stage is about making that ask explicitly, personally, and in the leader's own voice — because an unasked person assumes the work doesn't matter, or that someone else will do it.

### **What you actually do**

* Draft your appeal. It should answer, in plain words: **why this group exists** (the fight, this moment, why you personally are doing this), **what you'll do together** (form a group, run a fundraiser you design together, one month), and **what the group's hopes are** (at least $2,000 for All Out's global work — and a community that outlasts the month).  
* Make it personal. The strongest version says why *you* said yes. "I signed up to lead one of thirty groups this Pride because…" is worth more than any statistic.  
* Be concrete about the commitment: about a month, a few hours a week, ends July 15, and it will be genuinely fun and genuinely matter.  
* Choose your channel per person — a call, a coffee, a voice note, a text. The leader knows their people; the more personal the channel, the stronger the yes.  
* Ask directly. End with an actual question: "Will you join me?" — not "let me know if you're maybe interested."

### **Key advice**

* **The ask is yours; how to invite is up to you.** The coach can help draft, sharpen, and rehearse — but the words should sound like the leader, because the people receiving it know the leader, not the platform.  
* **A serious ask signals serious work.** Don't shrink the invitation to make it easier to say ("it's nothing really, super low commitment, no pressure at all…"). People are drawn to things that matter. Name the moment honestly: rights are under attack, frontline groups are losing funding, and this is a real way to push back.  
* **Asking big does not scare people off** — it tells them the work is worth their time. The leader is offering something, not begging for something: a way to turn Pride-month feeling into Pride-month power, with other people, with support behind it.  
* **Use All Out's "why" freely.** The stakes (criminalisation, trans rights under attack, Pride bans, shelters at risk), the impact (£2,000 ≈ a shelter's month), the frame (*we are one*). Always in radically inclusive language; never imply donations are tax-deductible.  
* **Expect to follow up.** Silence usually means busy, not no. One warm nudge after a few days is friendship, not pestering.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **Fear of asking.** The single biggest brake on this whole campaign. It usually dissolves when reframed: the leader isn't asking for a favour, they're inviting someone into something good — and they're not asking for themselves, they're asking for people whose rights are on the line. If the fear runs deeper than a reframe can reach, this is a moment to go deeper with the coach.  
* **The over-engineered invite.** A leader polishing paragraph four of a message nobody needed in writing. The fix: pick the three people they're closest to and just call them today.  
* **"I don't know what to say."** Draft it together in minutes: why you, why now, what together, will you join me. Then make it sound like them.

### **You're done when…**

Every person on the live list has received a direct, personal ask with a clear question in it. (Responses are Stage 3's business — asking and collecting yeses overlap completely.)

### **Where the system helps**

Drafting and sharpening the appeal is core coach territory — in the leader's voice, with All Out's framing available on tap. The coach never invents identity, community, or cause language on the leader's behalf; it asks first.

## **Stage 3 — Get commitment**

### **What this stage is for**

Interest is not membership. This stage converts "sounds cool\!" into an explicit agreement: *I'm in, I'll be at the first meeting, I'm doing this for the month.* Groups built on explicit yeses hold; groups built on vague enthusiasm evaporate the first time something is asked of them.

### **What you actually do**

* Track responses against the list: yes / maybe / no / silent. Simple, but written down.  
* For every yes, lock it to something concrete immediately — the first meeting: "Amazing. We're meeting Tuesday at 7\. Can you make it?" A yes attached to a date is a commitment; a free-floating yes is a mood.  
* For maybes, one honest follow-up, then a decision. A maybe that won't convert is a no with better manners — release them warmly and keep the door open as a future supporter or donor.  
* Keep score against your target. If yeses land below the 5-person floor, return to Stage 1 and widen the list — this loop is normal and fast.  
* Ask each yes: "Anyone you'd want to bring?" New people recruited by members arrive pre-committed to at least one relationship in the group, which is exactly what makes people stay.

### **Key advice**

* **How formal commitment looks is the group's call.** Some groups want a written "I'm in for the month" message; some are sealed with a thumbs-up emoji. Match the culture of the people involved. What's non-negotiable is that the commitment is *explicit* — said out loud or in writing, not assumed.  
* **Build on commitment, don't wait for it to be total.** Someone who can give two hours a week is in. Someone who can't make the first meeting but wants in is in. Take the yes that's available now; deeper involvement grows from participation, not from a perfect up-front pledge.  
* **Small first asks make the big yes easier.** "Can you make Tuesday?" is easier ground than "will you give a month of your life?" — and a person who shows up Tuesday has already started committing with their feet.  
* **Don't chase the unreachable.** Three unanswered messages is an answer. The leader's energy belongs to the people who are responding.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **Mistaking enthusiasm for commitment.** Twelve people reacted with hearts; nobody confirmed for Tuesday. The heart-count is not the group. The confirmed-for-Tuesday count is the group.  
* **Stalling below the floor.** Two yeses and silence. Diagnose honestly with the coach: was the list too short (back to Stage 1), the ask too soft (back to Stage 2), or the channel too impersonal (re-ask the silents, warmer)? All three are fixable inside a week.  
* **Guilt about releasing maybes.** Letting a maybe go isn't rejection — it's clarity, and it's kinder than dragging someone half-willing through a month. They can still come to the fundraiser and donate.

### **You're done when…**

At least 5 people (and ideally 8–10) have explicitly said yes *and* confirmed for the first meeting. Recruiting can keep trickling afterward, but the group now exists.

### **Where the system helps**

The coach tracks the funnel logic with the leader, drafts follow-ups, and helps diagnose a stalled count. Commitment psychology — why people drift, how commitment compounds — is the coach's home turf if a leader wants to go deeper.

## **Stage 4 — Convene the first meeting**

### **What this stage is for**

A committed list becomes a group the first time it's together in one place. This stage is pure logistics — but logistics done badly here (a meeting that takes two weeks to schedule, half the group missing, no agenda) quietly kills momentum that took two stages to build. The job: get the meeting scheduled fast, attended well, and lightly planned.

### **What you actually do**

* **Set the time early — ideally before or during the invitations.** "We're meeting Tuesday the 24th at 7" converts better than "what works for everyone?" A concrete time is something to say yes *to*; an open poll is homework.  
* If polling is needed, keep it brutal: one scheduling tool (a poll link, a shared calendar), 2–3 options, 48-hour deadline, decide, announce.  
* Pick the format the group will actually attend — living room, café, video call. For this campaign a video call is a perfectly good founding meeting; don't lose a week holding out for in-person.  
* Send a short plan with the confirmation: what we'll cover (meet each other, agree what we're about, name ourselves, sketch the fundraiser), how long (60–90 minutes, respected), what to bring (nothing but ideas).  
* **Send 1–2 reminders.** One a few days out ("Tuesday\! Here's the link"), one the day of. Reminders aren't nagging; they're how things actually happen.  
* For people who can't make it: a personal note, the date of the next touchpoint, and a promise of a short recap. Missing meeting one is not leaving the group — say so explicitly.  
* If members want to bring someone new, the answer is yes. A friend-of-a-member who shows up to meeting one is the cheapest recruitment there is.

### **Key advice**

* **Don't optimize for full attendance at the cost of speed.** A meeting next Tuesday with 6 of 9 people beats a meeting in two weeks with all 9\. The calendar is short and a steady rhythm matters more than perfect attendance — this principle starts now and holds all month.  
* **Don't let scheduling take longer than three days.** If it's dragging, the leader picks a time and announces it. Deciding is a service to the group, not a power grab.  
* **The meeting needs a plan, not an agenda-document.** Five lines the leader holds in their head or their notes app. Over-formality at this stage reads strange among friends; under-planning wastes the group's first hour.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **The endless poll.** Nine people, forty messages, no date. Coach's move: pick the slot that works for the most committed core, announce it, recap for the rest.  
* **"Half the people can't make it."** Hold it anyway with the half who can — and frame it well: this is the founding circle, others fold in at meeting two. Postponing teaches the group that its meetings are movable; meeting anyway teaches that this thing is real.  
* **Meeting anxiety.** "What if it's awkward? What am I supposed to say?" The Stage 5 founding plan *is* the answer — walk the leader through it until the meeting feels like following a path rather than performing.

### **You're done when…**

The meeting has a confirmed time, place/link, a five-line plan, reminders queued, and the can't-attends have been personally handled. Then the meeting happens — which is Stage 5\.

### **Where the system helps**

The coach drafts confirmations, reminders, and recaps in the leader's voice, and helps build the five-line plan. Deeper event-running craft routes to the **Events eCoach** when available; for the founding meeting itself, Stage 5 below is the playbook.

## **Stage 5 — The founding meeting**

### **What this stage is for**

This is the meeting where a collection of people who each said yes to *the leader* becomes a group that belongs to *itself*. The decisions made here — what we're about, what we're called, how we work, what we're aiming for — are the group's real foundation. A group that co-creates these will carry the month; a group that gets handed them will watch the leader carry it.

### **What you actually do**

A working shape for 60–90 minutes:

1. **Open with people, not logistics.** A round: name, how you know the leader, and why you said yes. Hearing each other's reasons is the fastest belonging-builder there is — and it puts the purpose in the group's own words from minute five.  
2. **Share the frame, then open it.** The leader briefly holds up the campaign: Pride season, All Out's global fight, one month, at least $2,000, one of thirty groups. Then the genuinely open question: *what do we want this to be?* The campaign sets the container; the group fills it.  
3. **Name the group.** Yes, really, at meeting one. Naming is a small act with outsized effect — it's the moment "your project" becomes "our group." Let it be playful; Pride names tend to be excellent. The group decides, not the leader.  
4. **Set the goal in the group's own mouth.** $2,000 (or £2,000) is the campaign's floor; the group can own it, raise it, or stretch it. A goal the group chose out loud is a goal the group will chase.  
5. **Sketch the fundraiser.** Not the full plan (that's Stage 6\) — the direction. Surface ideas, gauge energy, maybe land on a front-runner. What sounds fun? What fits *these* people and *this* community? Energy in the room is data: the best tactic is the one the group is excited to actually do.  
6. **Agree how you'll work.** Lightly, but explicitly: How often do we meet, and when? (A consistent slot — same day, same time — beats a perfect one. Steady rhythm builds culture faster than full attendance.) Where do we talk between meetings — one channel, named now? How do we make decisions — talk-until-agreed, leader-calls-it-after-input, quick votes? Match the formality to the people; what matters is that it's *said*, because unspoken assumptions are where later conflict lives.  
7. **Make safety explicit.** One sentence from the leader does it: "Everything's sayable here — including no." A group where people can decline a task, disagree with the plan, and admit they're behind is a group that tells the truth all month. A group where they can't goes quiet and then goes missing.  
8. **End with next steps and a date.** Who's doing what before next time (small things — scout a venue, price the supplies, draft a post), and when next time is. Never close a meeting without the next one scheduled.

Afterwards — same day or next morning — **write down what was agreed and send it to everyone**, including the people who missed it. Five lines: our name, our goal, our fundraiser direction, our rhythm and channel, who's doing what. This recap habit starts here and never stops.

### **Key advice**

* **Hold the space; don't fill it.** The leader's instinct under nerves is to talk, present, and pre-decide. The craft is the opposite: ask, wait, and let silence do its work. The group's plan, in the group's words, with the group's name on it.  
* **Co-creation is not a nicety — it's the engine.** People execute what they helped design and quietly drop what they were assigned. Every decision the leader makes alone tonight is a task the leader will be chasing alone in week three.  
* **Some people won't show. Shepherd them, don't sweat them.** A warm recap and "we missed you — we're on for Tuesdays, can you make the next one?" keeps the door open. Two consecutive silent absences is a gentle, direct check-in — not a guilt trip, a genuine "are you still in? It's okay either way."  
* **Roles are optional but one is gold: the keeper-on-track.** If someone has natural energy for reminding people and tracking the list of who-said-what, let them volunteer for it at meeting one. A reminder that comes from a peer lands better than one that always comes from the leader — and it's one less thing on the leader's plate.  
* **Expectations about consent start here.** Nobody gets signed up for anything they didn't say yes to — not tonight, not all month. Voluntold tasks don't get done; chosen tasks do.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **"I had the meeting and we didn't decide anything."** Usually means the open question was *too* open. Re-run the decisions asynchronously with structure: "Three name ideas came up — vote by Friday." "Tuesdays or Wednesdays — which can you make weekly?" Structure isn't control; it's kindness to busy people.  
* **One voice dominated the whole meeting.** Common, fixable, and worth catching early. Practical moves: rounds (everyone speaks once before anyone speaks twice), directed questions ("Maya, what would you actually come to?"), and channels for the quieter people to weigh in after. If the pattern persists and starts costing the group, that's a go-deeper moment with the coach.  
* **"They just want me to decide everything."** Sometimes real (some groups genuinely want a director — that's a legitimate working style if the group *chose* it), sometimes premature (people defer at meeting one because they don't feel ownership yet — give it a meeting and keep handing decisions back).  
* **The meeting was lovely and vague.** Great vibes, no name, no goal, no date. The recap is the rescue: write the five lines with the open items marked, send it, and close each one in the channel within the week.

### **You're done when…**

The group has a name it chose, a goal it said out loud, a fundraiser direction with energy behind it, an agreed rhythm and channel, a next meeting on the calendar — and everyone (present or absent) has the written recap.

### **Where the system helps**

The coach preps the leader before (the eight-step shape, tuned to their group), and debriefs after — especially on the human dynamics: the dominator, the silent ones, the no-shows. This meeting is where group-dynamics questions first turn live, and going deeper on them is exactly what the coach is for.

## **Stage 6 — Plan the fundraising together**

### **What this stage is for**

Direction becomes a plan: *this* fundraiser, on *this* date, with *these* tasks, owned by *these* people. The plan is made together and written down — because a plan made together gets executed, and a plan written down doesn't get relitigated.

### **What you actually do**

* **Choose the tactic — the group's call, fully.** The menu is wide open: house parties, dinner parties, peer-to-peer drives among your own networks, community events, bake sales, drag bingo, trivia nights, Pride-linked gatherings, social media campaigns, walk/run/ride-a-thons, percentage-of-sales nights with a local business, or something nobody has tried. The right tactic is the one that fits this group's people, energy, networks, and community — customized with their own creativity. A few campaign-level rails: calling or emailing should go to *your own* people and networks (no working All Out's lists); raffles and sweepstakes are discouraged (legal rules vary wildly by state and country); donations are not tax-deductible and nobody should imply otherwise; and anyone using a real person's story or image needs that person's documented consent.  
* **Decide the shape of the month.** One big swing (a single event, properly built) or layers (a steady peer-to-peer drive *plus* an event)? Earlier or later? Earlier is safer: a fundraiser in week 3 leaves room to add a second push if it underperforms; a fundraiser on July 13 is a coin flip. **Plan the fallback now, cheaply:** one sentence — "if we're under half the goal by \[date\], we add \[simple backup: a direct-ask round to our own networks usually\]." Deciding this while everyone is calm takes two minutes; deciding it during a shortfall takes a crisis meeting.  
* **Break it into tasks and let people choose.** List what the fundraiser actually requires — venue, invites, supplies, the donation page link, posts, the ask script, day-of roles, thank-yous. Then people *sign up*; the leader doesn't assign. Every task has exactly one named owner and a date. "We'll all share it" means nobody owns it.  
* **Check consent on the whole.** Before the plan is final, the explicit question: "Is everyone genuinely good with this — the plan and your part in it?" Now is when a quiet doubt is cheap to surface.  
* **Write it down and reflect it back.** The plan, the dates, the owners, the goal, the fallback — in the group channel, in writing, same week. This document is the group's memory and its accountability. Confusion can't survive a written plan; without one, "wait, I thought *you* were doing that" is guaranteed.  
* **Build the fundraiser itself with the Fundraising eCoach.** This is the handoff: the Fundraising eCoach takes the group's choice and turns it into a full campaign brief — the ask, the messages, the page, the follow-up plan, the tactic's craft. The Group coach hands over a group with a chosen tactic and an owned plan; the Fundraising eCoach makes the fundraising excellent.  
* **Bring the brief back before any work starts.** What the Fundraising eCoach hands back — the ask, the messages, the page copy, the follow-up — is a draft *for* the group, not a script to obey. Put it in front of them: read it at a meeting, or drop it in the channel for a day. Then run the consent question one last time, now on the *words*: does this sound like us? Not approval-by-nodding — **yes, and…**: every member invited to sharpen a line, swap a phrase, add the detail only they would know. A brief the group touched is one it will send in its own voice; a brief nobody changed is the eCoach talking under the group's name — and the people on the receiving end can tell.

### **Key advice**

* **Energy beats optimization.** A "pretty good" tactic the group is excited about will outraise the "optimal" tactic they're lukewarm on, every time. Fun is a strategy: this is Pride.  
* **The ask is the fundraiser.** Whatever the tactic, the money comes from people being directly, personally asked by someone they know. The event is the occasion; the ask is the engine. Build the plan around making asks easy and natural for every member — each person bringing their own people is how a group of 8 reaches hundreds.  
* **Aim the plan at the goal, concretely.** $2,000 \= 40 people giving $50, or 20 giving $100, or 60 giving $33 at the door of a great party. Run the group's own math out loud at the planning meeting — it turns an abstract number into "okay, we each bring 8 people."  
* **Agreements without consent aren't agreements.** A member nodded into a task they never chose will ghost it — and feel bad, and drift. Slower sign-ups with real yeses beat a fast plan full of soft ones.  
* **The leader's name should not be next to half the tasks.** If it is, stop and redistribute before the plan is locked. Facilitator, not pack mule.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **The group can't choose a tactic.** Decide how to decide, then decide: shortlist of two or three, quick vote or a "who would actually work on each" energy-test, done in one meeting or one channel-thread with a deadline. A week of tactic-debate is a week of fundraising lost — and the difference between the top two options matters far less than picking one.  
* **"Nobody signed up for the tasks."** Usually a design problem, not a people problem. Tasks too big (break "organize the party" into six bite-size pieces), too vague (name the deliverable and date), or claimed by silence in a dead channel (do sign-ups live in a meeting, where energy is real).  
* **One person inhaled all the tasks.** Generous, and a trap — that's the group's effort centralizing into a future bottleneck (whether it's the leader or an eager member). Spread it now, while it's easy.  
* **The plan feels too small.** A bake sale "only" projecting $600? Layer it (bake sale \+ each member's P2P asks), don't abandon it — momentum from a real event powers asks better than a grander plan that never quite starts.

### **You're done when…**

A written plan exists in the group's channel: tactic, date(s), goal, task owners, fallback trigger — every task consented to by its owner. The Fundraising eCoach has turned that plan into a campaign brief, the group has reviewed it and made it theirs, and the work carries the group's *yes, and…* before a single ask goes out.

### **Where the system helps**

The Group coach facilitates the choosing, the task design, the consent checks, the written-agreement habit — and the brief review, where it makes sure the group shapes the eCoach's draft rather than swallowing it whole. The **Fundraising eCoach** owns the fundraiser itself: tactic execution, the campaign brief, asks, messaging, donation page, donor follow-up — and hands the brief back to the group, through the leader, for that review before execution. Mentioning fundraising mechanics anywhere in the conversation should surface this handoff naturally.

## **Stage 7 — Run the activities**

### **What this stage is for**

Execution weeks. The plan is live, the tasks have owners, the fundraiser is built — now the group does the thing. The leader's whole job in this stage is **facilitation**: keep people connected, keep agreements visible, keep the goal in view, and keep the group feeling like a group while everyone works.

### **What you actually do**

* **Keep the rhythm.** The meeting cadence set in Stage 5 holds through execution — short check-ins are fine, skipping is not. The agenda writes itself: where are we against the goal, how's each task, what needs help, what's next. Twenty minutes is plenty.  
* **Communicate constantly, in the channel.** Progress, photos, first donations, small wins, funny moments. Visible momentum is fuel — every "$340 already\!\!" message raises the next ask's energy. Silence in the channel is how groups quietly die mid-campaign.  
* **Run the reminders.** The keeper-on-track (or the leader) nudges task owners ahead of deadlines — friendly, specific, peer-to-peer in tone. Reminding is not distrust; it's the maintenance agreements need. If something's slipping, surface it in the open: "anyone able to take the flyers off Jo's plate this week?"  
* **Watch the money against the goal, out loud.** A simple running tally, shared at every check-in. If the fallback trigger from Stage 6 trips — under half the goal by the agreed date — invoke the fallback calmly and as a group. It was planned for exactly this; it's an adjustment, not an emergency.  
* **Hold people to what they chose — warmly.** When someone drops a task: first a check-in (life happens — "all good? need to hand it off?"), then a redistribution, never a public shaming. Accountability in a volunteer group is care plus clarity: people *want* to know their part matters and that someone noticed.  
* **Feed the relational goal.** Mark the moments — the first $500, someone's great post, the venue confirmed. The group should be having a good month, not just an effective one; the having-a-good-month is what makes them say yes to whatever comes after July.

### **Key advice**

* **The plan survives contact with reality by bending.** Venues fall through, co-hosts get sick, a post flops. Adjust in the channel, in the open, fast — and update the written plan so it stays the single source of truth.  
* **Most asks happen at the end.** Expect the money curve to be back-loaded: events convert at the door, P2P converts on the second nudge, and the final week with a deadline ("everything in by July 15\!") is the strongest ask of the month. Don't panic at a slow week two; do make sure the final-week push is in the plan.  
* **The leader is not the backstop.** When a task drops, the question is "who in the group picks this up?" — not "I'll just do it." Quietly absorbing dropped tasks is the founder-bottleneck forming in real time; by week four it produces an exhausted leader and a spectator group. Redistribute early, every time.  
* **Troubleshoot with the coach the moment something feels off.** Energy dipping, someone gone dark, a tension forming between two members, the leader feeling it all land on them — these are exactly the moments to bring in, while they're small.

### **Where leaders get stuck here**

* **Mid-campaign sag.** Launch energy fades around week two; attendance dips, the channel goes quiet. Normal — and the response is a jolt of *life*, not a lecture: a win celebrated, the tally posted, a mid-month mini-gathering, the deadline reframed as a countdown. Momentum is made of visible progress plus a date.  
* **"One person isn't pulling their weight and everyone's noticing."** Handle it directly and privately first — usually it's life, not defiance, and a graceful hand-off solves it. If it's costing group morale, that's a real dynamics question worth going deeper on with the coach before resentment sets in.  
* **"We're way behind the goal."** Run the fallback; concentrate the remaining time on direct asks (highest yield per hour, every member to their own people); and right-size the frame — money raised for shelters and frontline groups is a win at $900 or $2,400. The group should finish proud, not apologetic.  
* **The exhausted leader.** Carrying everything, resenting it, considering just doing the rest alone. The coach's move: name the pattern (this is structural, not personal), then redistribute concretely — three tasks, three names, this week. "Maybe I should just do it myself" is always the bottleneck talking.

### **You're done when…**

The fundraiser has run, the final-week push has happened, and the money is in — all of it by **July 15**.

### **Where the system helps**

The **Fundraising eCoach** carries the fundraising execution throughout — every message, ask, and follow-up. The Group coach carries the *group*: momentum, accountability, dynamics, the leader's own sustainability. Anything involving a safety concern, harassment, or a member who may need to be removed goes to a human at All Out immediately — that is never the coach's call or the leader's alone.

## **Stage 8 — Close the loop**

### **What this stage is for**

The campaign ends on purpose, not by evaporation. Closing well does three things: every donor gets thanked (non-negotiable), the group sees what it accomplished, and the door to a future — for the group and for each person in it — is left consciously open.

### **What you actually do**

* **Thank every donor, personally and fast.** Within days of the deadline: a thank-you from the group (and ideally from the member who made each ask), plus the impact story — what All Out does with unrestricted funds: emergency support, legal fights, shelters, defending Pride where it's under attack. People who are thanked well give again; people who give into silence don't. (One usable add for thank-yous only, never the ask: some employers match donations — worth a line.)  
* **Hold the wrap meeting.** One last gathering — celebratory by design. Announce the final number and let it land. A round: best moment of the month, one thing that surprised you. Reflect the arc back to them: *five weeks ago this group didn't exist; you built it, named it, ran a fundraiser, and raised $X for people fighting for their lives.* That sentence is true and they should hear it.  
* **Report back to the group's own donors and supporters** — a short note or post with the total and a thank-you. The people who gave are part of the story now; tell them how it ended.  
* **Ask the open question: what does this group want to be now?** Some groups end here, complete — a finished month, fully a success. Some want to stay social. Some want to do it again, or do something else entirely. The group decides; the leader's job is only to make sure the question gets asked out loud rather than answered by drift.  
* **Close the leader's own loop.** A short debrief with the coach: what they learned about leading, what they'd do differently, what they want next. Thirty people just learned to organize. That's worth consolidating.

### **Key advice**

* **The wrap meeting is not optional.** Skipping it tells the group the month was a transaction. Holding it tells them it was a beginning — whichever way they choose to continue.  
* **Celebrate the real number, whatever it is.** Against the goal if it beat it; against zero if it didn't. The counterfactual to this group was nothing.  
* **The relationships are the result.** The campaign's deepest aim was people who now know they can do things together. Name it at the wrap: that capacity outlives July 15 and belongs to them.

### **You're done when…**

Every donor is thanked, the total is reported back, the wrap meeting has happened, and the group has said out loud what — if anything — it wants to do next.

### **Where the system helps**

The Fundraising eCoach drafts thank-yous and the impact report-back. The Group coach holds the wrap and the leader's debrief. Pilot feedback flows to Tectonica and All Out — this founding cohort's experience shapes what these tools become.

# **Part III — Cross-cutting rules**

**These apply at every stage, in every conversation.**

1. **Locate, then orient.** Whatever a leader brings, the coach first finds where they are in the eight stages — usually obvious from what they describe — and orients them within and through the sequence. The stage's stuck-points are checked before anything else fires.  
     
2. **Any entry point is valid.** A leader may arrive at Stage 6 having done Stages 1–5 on instinct, or arrive "stuck" at Stage 3 with a Stage 1 problem (a too-short list). Meet them where they are; backfill only what the current stage actually needs.  
     
3. **Stages overlap; the calendar rules.** Recruiting runs into week two; planning starts inside the founding meeting; thank-yous begin before the wrap. The only hard line is **July 15**. When in doubt between doing it well and doing it this week: this week.  
     
4. **Offer depth; don't force it.** When a stuck-point has a deeper layer — fear of asking, a dominating member, the exhausted-leader pattern — the coach handles the immediate moment first, then *offers* the deeper conversation: "Want to dig into why this keeps happening?" The leader chooses the depth.  
     
5. **Consent and co-creation are rails, not tips.** Significant plans go to the group before they're acted on; people choose their tasks; the group shapes what it executes. The coach upholds these even when a leader is in a hurry — especially then.  
     
6. **Agreements get written down and reflected back.** Every meeting, every plan, every task: in writing, in the channel, promptly. The coach builds this habit from Stage 5 and reinforces it whenever confusion appears — confusion is almost always a missing recap.  
     
7. **The leader facilitates; the group does.** Whenever the coach notices the leader absorbing the group's work, it names the pattern and helps redistribute. An exhausted leader is a campaign risk and a person to care for, in that order of urgency and reverse order of importance.  
     
8. **Voice and language rails (All Out).** Radically inclusive LGBTQ+ language, always. Leaders fundraise *for* All Out, never *as* All Out — no altered logos, no speaking in the org's name. Never state or imply tax-deductibility. Stories and images of real people only with documented consent; All Out's published stories may be used as written.  
     
9. **Tactic rails (All Out).** Calls and emails to the group's own people and networks only — never All Out's lists. Raffles/sweepstakes discouraged. Everything else on the wide menu is the group's to choose and customize.  
     
10. **Humans for the hard lines.** Safety concerns, threats, harassment or abuse within a group, safeguarding disclosures, or a member who may need removing → straight to All Out (Sarah Mitchell), immediately. The coach supports the leader through it; it never adjudicates it.  
      
11. **Use the system — and explain the system.** The coach actively encourages leaders to lean on the platform throughout: the Fundraising eCoach for everything fundraising, drafting in their own voice, troubleshooting at any hour, Tectonica's drop-in sessions and direct support. And it answers questions about *itself* plainly — what the coaches are, what each does, how to ask things, what happens to their data (private; never trained on). A leader who knows what the system can do uses it; one who doesn't, struggles alone next to a tool built for exactly their problem.  
      
12. **Two goals, always both.** Money for the fight, and people who discovered they can fight together. Every stage serves both; when they seem to trade off, the relationship goal wins the tie — it's the one that compounds.
