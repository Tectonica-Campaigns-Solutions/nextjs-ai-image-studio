## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

The load-bearing claims the book rests on — the baselines a coach returns to across a whole class of problems. Each is individually retrievable and citable; Layer 2 entries point here by name via `anchors to:` (deliberately *not* coded as a taxonomy axis, pending testing — see design-plan Q13).

**Structure is king.** A group's structure — its composition, goals, tasks, and norms — shapes outcomes far more than its leader's effort or personality, and the leader's job is to cultivate conditions rather than force results. Fisher draws this from his mentor Richard Hackman's insight that groups are open social systems best managed through "conditions, rather than causes": like a gardener you can prepare the soil, water, and feed, "but you can't cause them to grow." Because structure is set *before* anyone interacts, it shapes effort and coordination from the outset, which is why fixing structure pays off where pep talks and advice don't. Hackman and Wageman's rough split, which Fisher adopts: about 60% of a group's effectiveness is determined by structure, 30% by how it is launched, and only 10% by ongoing coaching. As Fisher puts it bluntly, "structure is king."
flagship example: At Xerox, regional managers tried to improve struggling photocopier-repair teams. Leaders who intervened on structure (goals, tasks, who was on the team, norms) produced large gains — structure explained roughly 37% of the gap between strong and weak teams — while even good coaching barely moved teams whose structure was broken, and bad coaching hurt poorly structured teams about three times as much.
source:  [Fisher](https://colinmfisher.com/) — *The Collective Edge*, ch.3, ch.4, ch.12governs: [A5.1] [A5.2] [A5.3] [A5.4] [A5.5] [A5.6] [A5.7] [A6.4]

**Effectiveness is task performance plus member satisfaction.** A group is effective only if it does two things at once — accomplishes its task *and* leaves members satisfied enough to keep showing up — and the two feed a virtuous cycle in which doing well feels good, which fuels effort, which improves performance. Every group carries both an instrumental dimension (goal attainment) and a relational one (belonging, liking), and a group that wins but burns everyone out is not effective. For volunteers especially, with no paycheck holding anyone in place, satisfaction *is* retention: people disengage from anything that stops meeting a need.
flagship example: The Rolling Stones lasted 60+ years partly because members, in Keith Richards's words, never got "to the point where they don't want to be involved in it" — a bar most one-hit-wonder bands (The Knack, Mungo Jerry) never cleared, splitting right after success. A meta-analysis of 169 studies covering 11,000+ teams found that staying together improves task performance, because long-tenured members develop shared ways of working and coordinate more smoothly.
source:  [Fisher](https://colinmfisher.com/) — *The Collective Edge*, ch.3governs: [A10.6] [A6.3]

**Leadership is functional and shared.** Leadership is not a throne occupied by one heroic individual; it is any proactive act that fulfills a group need — providing direction, structuring work, protecting members' voice — and the healthiest groups distribute those acts across many people rather than resting them on one. We systematically over-credit and over-blame the person on top (a cognitive shortcut for complex outcomes), and the evidence shows replacing a single struggling leader rarely rescues a group. Leadership acts can be loud (a launch speech) or quiet (a clarifying question, a defusing joke, fixing an unnoticed problem); most studies find shared leadership improves effectiveness.
flagship example: The 2008 "Redeem Team" had several leaders operating at once — Coach K set direction at launch, director Jerry Colangelo built the structural conditions (a three-year commitment, better selection), veteran Kobe Bryant modeled a relentless work ethic, and LeBron James got teammates to follow Kobe's lead. Bryant's example only became leadership *because* James amplified it.
source:  [Fisher](https://colinmfisher.com/) — *The Collective Edge*, ch.13governs: [A9.5] [A1.2] [A9.1] [A9.3] [A9.4]

**Conformity must be actively countered.** Groups naturally drift toward suppressing dissent and narrowing into a shared reality, which quietly forfeits the value of having diverse people in the room; left unchecked this produces self-censorship, groupthink, and decisions that ignore the very information a group exists to surface. The countermeasures are psychological safety (a shared sense that it's safe to admit mistakes, ask questions, and disagree) and running decisions as collective problem-solving rather than advocacy. This is ongoing maintenance, not a one-time fix — the pull toward agreement returns every meeting.
flagship example: Amy Edmondson found the *best*-structured hospital nursing teams reported *more* drug errors — not because they made more, but because it was safe to surface them. A nurse on a strong team explained, "Mistakes are serious because of the toxicity of the drugs"; a nurse on a weak team in the same hospital said the opposite culture meant "You get put on trial."
source:  [Fisher](https://colinmfisher.com/) — *The Collective Edge*, ch.8governs: [A6.5] [A8.2] [A7.2] [A6.1]

**Learning together is the engine.** Sustained effectiveness comes from cycles of trial and error run deliberately at the natural seams of a group's life — beginnings, midpoints, and endings — where members reflect on an experience, draw a generalizable lesson, and experiment with a change. Fisher leans on Kolb's learning cycle (experience → reflect → theorize → experiment) and argues that every project, not just every new technology, is an opportunity to learn a better way of working together. Groups that institutionalize this — debriefs, midpoint checks — compound small improvements and outlast their founders.
flagship example: Adopting minimally invasive cardiac surgery, "Hospital B" treated it as a team-learning challenge: it composed a learning team for skill and teachability, set a learning goal (not just a technology goal), and debriefed after each of roughly ten operations, making small changes each time. It adopted the technique far faster than "Hospital A," which "plugged in" the new tools without changing how the team worked. As one Hospital B nurse put it, it became "a model, not just for this hospital but for cardiac surgery."
