# People Power Change — ## Layer 2 — Concept Entries


### Leadership is a practice, not a position
concepts:    [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is enabling, not doing
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat leadership as something you *do*, not a title you hold. Ganz distinguishes leadership from authority: authority is the legitimacy to enforce rules (by position, ownership, or delegation), while leadership is enabling others to achieve shared purpose. In a volunteer group your authority is non-positional — granted by members' commitment, exercisable only through persuasion and legitimacy, and revocable — so leading "requires more skill than in other settings because it depends more on persuasion than on command." Do not wait for a title or wait to feel qualified.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because no one is paid to obey you and anyone can walk, you cannot compel; you can only earn standing by engaging people's own motives. Ganz notes we usually only ask "who's in charge here?" when we hit trouble — leadership's value shows up under *uncertainty*, the unexpected, the challenging, not the routine. So the leader's job is less having answers than knowing how to find them with others.
- **Example in context:** Ganz contrasts people who occupy formal authority but lead badly with people practicing real leadership "at kitchen tables, in workplaces, neighborhoods" with no title, office, or CV. The point is the practice, visible in deeds, not the seat.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For the first-timer who feels like an imposter, this relocates legitimacy from credentials to practice — you become a leader by accepting responsibility and enabling others, which you can start doing now. It also warns against the opposite error: trying to compel cooperation through whatever positional power you do have, which a volunteer group will simply leave.
- **Related concepts:** enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (what the practice consists of); developing-leaders [A9.1] (leadership grows by making more leaders); building-and-using-power [A7.1] (authority here is relational and earned).

> verbatim: leadership "depends more on persuasion than on command." — Ganz

### The catalyst who enables rather than does
concepts:    [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is enabling, not doing · Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Your job is to *enable others to act*, not to do the work yourself or perform for an audience. Ganz's whole definition turns on "enabling others," and he sharpens it: enabling people to choose action "requires entering into a relationship with them, not performing for them." The leader sets conditions and develops capacity; doing it all yourself is the failure mode, not the job.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** When a leader tries to fix the problem alone, they put on a performance — and performance alone can't solve a problem that depends on many people's commitment. Enabling works because it engages others' agency and resources; performing exhausts the one person and leaves everyone else as spectators. This is the same impulse Ganz diagnoses in Moses doing all the judging himself.
- **Example in context:** the U.S. mayors during the 2020 protests (see Layer 0). The ones who tried to solve it themselves failed; the ones who drew on relationships with police, protesters, and public — inviting them to become part of the solution — practiced leadership. (Large-scale office; the enabling-vs-performing distinction scales to any group.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Doing everything myself" is one of the most common first-timer traps and the seed of burnout. Reframing the role as enabling — and noticing when you've slipped into performing or doing — is the corrective. It directly sets up delegation and developmental leadership.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (the mechanism of enabling); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (what happens when you don't); volunteer-burnout [A10.1] (the cost of doing it all).

### Why me? Stepping into the role
concepts:    [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Expect to feel unqualified, and step in anyway. Ganz roots his whole approach in Hillel's three questions — *If I am not for myself, who will be for me? When I am for myself alone, what am I? If not now, when?* — which frame leadership as the interaction of self, others, and action. Claiming the role starts with being clear about your own values and resources (self-regarding, not selfish) and resisting the "snare of preparation."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** "Why me?" is the universal first response — Ganz points to Moses at the burning bush ("you've got the wrong guy, I can't even give a speech") as the archetype. You answer it not by acquiring more confidence first but by acting, because understanding flows from action; and you ground your standing to lead by telling a story of self that shows where your values came from.
- **Example in context:** Moses' objections to the call — wrong person, can't speak, who are you, can't this wait — are met not with reassurance but with help (his brother Aaron) and a next step. Ganz reads this as the pattern: the doubt is normal, the move is to begin.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Imposter feeling is near-universal for first-timers and stops people before they start. The remedy Ganz offers is concrete: clarify your own values, author your story of self (or others will author it for you), and start acting rather than waiting to feel ready.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (why starting-before-ready is safe); public-narrative [A2.2] (the story of self is where you claim the role); fear-of-asking-or-delegating [A1.5] (the adjacent block).

> verbatim: "If I am not for myself, who will be for me?" — Hillel (quoted in Ganz)

### The fear of the ask — and of delegating
concepts:    [A1.5 fear-of-asking-or-delegating]
situations:  [S:scared-to-ask-people] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Name and push through two linked fears: asking people for a real commitment, and handing real responsibility to others. Ganz is blunt that failing to ask "shows a lack of respect for the agency of our constituents," and that fear of delegating "will stifle growth in the power of your organization." The move is to ask clearly and to delegate despite the risk.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** We avoid the ask because we fear rejection — "or maybe even acceptance, which binds us to the person of whom we have made the ask." We avoid delegating because we fear someone else will get it wrong (Ganz asks of Moses: was it that he cared so much about getting it right he couldn't risk someone else getting it wrong?). Both fears protect the leader and shrink the group; both are overcome by treating the discomfort as the price of others' agency.
- **Example in context:** the "I'll try" that you record as a yes but isn't — Ganz urges following up with "can I really count on you? Yes or no?" On delegation, Jethro frees Moses to let go only after restoring Moses's other identities (father, husband), the sources of courage to risk it.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Scared to ask" and "I can't let go" are distinct symptoms with one root — the fear of binding yourself to others and risking their failure. Both keep a group small and the leader overloaded.
- **Related concepts:** the-ask / escalating-commitment [A3.5] (asking, done well); ceding-control [A9.4] (delegating, done well); enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (the fears are what keep you doing it all).

### Fall off the bike: failure as learning
concepts:    [A1.6 failure-as-learning]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.7, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat setbacks as information, and deliberately risk *small* failures early so you avoid big ones later. Ganz frames action as inherently risky because the future is unknown, so leadership "is therefore more about learning than about knowing." Build the expectation of falling — and getting back on — into how the group works.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Two mechanisms. First, you only develop judgment about whom to trust by delegating, watching what happens, and learning — "if you fear making bad choices, you cannot learn to make good choices." Second, how you *interpret* a setback determines its effect: narrative psychologist Dan McAdams's distinction between a *contaminative* reading (this proves things always go bad) and a *redemptive* one (this is painful but a source of learning and growth).
- **Example in context:** learning to ride a bike — you fall off first, and the moment of truth is finding the courage to get back on knowing you'll keep falling. Ganz pairs this with risking a small meeting rather than a "monster rally" so a failure becomes a cheap lesson.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For an anxious first-timer this license to fail — and to debrief the failure rather than hide it — is what makes a brave space possible and what lets the leader start delegating before they feel certain.
- **Related concepts:** learning-together [A6.6] (the debrief that turns failure into a lesson); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (the redemptive reading at group scale); stepping-into-the-role [A1.4] (why you start before you're ready).

### Start with "who are my people," not the issue
concepts:    [A2.1 shared-purpose]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing] [S:group-lost-its-why]
handoff:
anchors to:  Narrative is how a leader moves people
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Lead with *who* and *why* before *what*. Ganz insists the first organizing question is "who are my people?" — not "what is my issue?" — because "issue-based identities can fragment people; values-based identities can unite people." Start by learning your people's values, interests, resources, and lived experience, and let the issues surface from there.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People are both the source of the change and the resources that create it, so a group anchored in shared values and a common purpose holds together where one anchored in an issue splinters when the issue shifts. Without a shared purpose, Ganz notes, "every decision relitigates what our purpose really is." Purpose is the *why* that the rest of the work serves.
- **Example in context:** in the UFW, the first question wasn't "what's the issue" but who the farmworker families were and what they experienced — security, dignity, a future for their children — which is what a union could deliver; the wage figure ($1.40/hour) was downstream of that. (Large constituency; the start-with-people move is general.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "We're not sure what we're doing" and "we've lost our why" are purpose problems, not tactics problems. Ganz's diagnostic is to go back to the people and the shared values, not to pick a sharper issue.
- **Related concepts:** values-as-glue [A2.3] (what the shared purpose is made of); the power theory of change [A7.1] (what-change-they-want feeds strategy); group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (a values-based "us").

### Public narrative: a story of self, us, and now
concepts:    [A2.2 public-narrative]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:unclear-what-were-doing]
handoff:
anchors to:  Narrative is how a leader moves people
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Learn public narrative as a leadership practice: a *story of self* (why I care), a *story of us* (why we care), and a *story of now* (the urgent challenge to our shared values and the choice we face). It is a framework, not a formula — order can vary — and it is not manipulation; it offers people a *choice* to act on values they already hold. The single most common failure is letting one of the three stories drop out; the story of self is foundational because it roots the values that run through all three ("values coherence").
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Stories work through *story moments* — a challenge, a choice, an outcome, and a moral felt rather than stated. We identify with the protagonist and experience their values as our own, which is why story teaches the heart, not just the head; Ganz draws on Jerome Bruner's distinction between the *paradigmatic* mode (how, facts) and the *narrative* mode (why, what is good), and calls story "agency training." Emotionally, a story of now turns a disruption from a threat we flee in fear into a challenge we engage with hope, enabling a mindful response instead of a reaction. The three stories are interdependent like a three-legged stool: a weak *us* and people won't feel connected enough to join; a weak *self* and they've no reason to trust *you*; a weak *now* and it's a nice gathering but not leadership.
- **Example in context:** Obama's 2004 keynote (Layer 0) is the linked exemplar. For the *now*, Ganz uses King's "I Have a Dream" — properly "the fierce urgency of Now" — where King brings alive the *nightmare* (the dynamited church, the assassinated Evers, dogs and hoses on children) before the dream, and the chant shifts from "Freedom" to "Freedom Now!" The applied form is the **empathetic bridge** for the four hardest moments (loss, difference, domination, change): acknowledge the challenge, offer empathy without claiming it ("don't say 'I know just how you feel'"), narrate hope (the possible, not a promise it'll be okay), and offer a choice — restoring agency.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is Ganz's signature contribution and the register's named source for A2.2. For a group that has "lost its why" or feels aimless, public narrative is the practice that re-roots the work in shared values and converts that into a concrete, hopeful ask. It also gives the leader a teachable structure rather than a vague injunction to "be inspiring."
- **Related concepts:** values-as-glue [A2.3] (values coherence binds the three stories); group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (the story of us *is* collective identity); the strategic goal [A7.1] (a story of now is where story and strategy meet).

> verbatim: King named the speech's purpose as "the fierce urgency of Now." — Martin Luther King Jr. (quoted in Ganz)

### The experiential "us": values as glue
concepts:    [A2.3 values-as-glue] [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Narrative is how a leader moves people
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build an *experiential* "us" defined by shared values and shared moments, not a *categorical* "us" defined by ascriptive traits (everyone with brown hair). A story of us celebrates "the values that make 'us' an 'us'" by recalling specific moments the group lived through — and a leader's job is to be the interpreter of that shared experience.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Categorical membership doesn't bind; lived shared values do. You evoke them by recounting concrete story moments ("remember when…") in which the group felt courage, persistence, or solidarity. How widely you "bound" the us is a strategic choice based on the values members actually share and the agency you want to enable — broad bounding is inclusive but thin, narrow bounding is deep but exclusive; neither is good or bad in itself.
- **Example in context:** Ganz uses Shakespeare's Henry V before Agincourt — the king doesn't promise victory or mention the enemy; he offers his outnumbered men a new view of themselves as a "happy few," united, with a chance at immortality. The eve-of-battle speech creates value that's real win or lose. (Grand illustration; the mechanism — recall shared moments to make values felt — works for any group.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that feels "transactional" or has lost its sense of "us" usually has only a categorical or instrumental tie. Ganz's fix is to surface and retell the shared-values moments that make people feel like a we — the felt belonging that commitment grows from.
- **Related concepts:** public-narrative [A2.2] (the story of us is one leg); cohesion-and-belonging [A6.3] (how the felt "we" develops in practice); transformational-vs-transactional [A2.5] (values bind where exchange alone does not).

### Mobilizing vs. organizing; constituency, not clients
concepts:    [A2.5 transformational-vs-transactional]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium · Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.5, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Know the difference between *mobilizing* (aggregating people's resources for an action, often from the center, often with money) and *organizing* (developing people's commitment and leadership so they build their own power). Ganz frames the goal as turning a community into a *constituency* — from Latin *con* (together) + *stare* (to stand) — not into clients, customers, or beneficiaries. Resist the easy mobilize when the goal is durable power.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Resources contributed from *within* a constituency empower the constituency; resources drawn from *outside* (money or outside time) empower others. And digital tools make it "easier to share information and aggregate individual resources than to share commitment to one another." A transactional/mobilized relationship dissolves when the reward or the campaign stops; an organized one builds people who keep going. This is the register's mobilizing/organizing lens.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's student: "I feel overconnected but undercommitted" — the felt result of a mobilized, transactional civic life. He contrasts a mobilizing campaign (control runs to the script the volunteer reads — "a messenger, not an organizer") with the 2008 Obama field campaign, which distributed real authority and so built lasting civic capacity. *(The mobilizing-vs-organizing campaign *architecture* is org-level — routed; the leader-level takeaway is the relational stance.)*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "People come once and don't return" and "the group feels transactional" are often signs you've been mobilizing where you needed to organize — renting attention rather than building commitment. The remedy is relational depth and shared ownership, not a better broadcast.
- **Related concepts:** transactional-vs-relational-ask [A3.3] (the ask-level version); relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (organizing's medium); developing-leaders [A9.1] (organizing's signature output).

> verbatim: "I feel overconnected but undercommitted." — a student (quoted in Ganz)

### Relationships are strategy
concepts:    [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.2

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat relationship-building as the actual work, not a warm-up to it. Relationships are "the threads with which we weave our communities" and the foundation of any civic association. They are *motivated by difference* — an exchange of differing resources — but begin only when both parties commit a scarce resource (time) to giving the relationship a future; commitment "can turn a transactional exchange into a transformational relationship."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Commonality makes a relationship possible; difference makes it beneficial. Trust, shared purpose, and commonality are usually *results* of a relationship, not what starts one — what starts one is exchange plus a leap of commitment rooted in (often unspoken) shared values. Because relationships are open-ended (unlike a contract), they can surface new interests and resources you didn't know you had, which is why relational communities are capable of collective action.
- **Example in context:** the coffee-shop non-relationship (Layer 0) — great conversation, but no mutual commitment of time, so no relationship forms. Ganz contrasts this with civic relationships built one conversation at a time in Mississippi with E. W. Steptoe, where accompanying a trusted local to talk with people built ties going door-to-door couldn't.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-timer often treats relationships as optional softness and recruitment as a numbers game. Ganz's claim is that the relationship *is* the strategy — low attendance and transactional feel trace back to thin relational ties, and the fix is relational, not promotional.
- **Related concepts:** trust-building [A4.3] (how the tie deepens); transactional-vs-relational-ask [A3.3] (the ask that respects the relationship); recruiting-as-relational [A4.5] (recruitment as relationship).

### "Respect is what you do, not what you have": trust
concepts:    [A4.3 trust-building]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.2, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build trust by *doing* respect — listening, asking, learning — and by being yourself, especially across difference. And know that in public life the aim is to be *respected*, which is "like, but different from, being liked" (Ganz, citing organizer Ed Chambers); chasing being liked in public life leads you to dodge holding people accountable.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Trust is a result of repeated respectful exchange, not a precondition you can assert. There is also a chicken-and-egg "cycle of trust" (Ganz cites Smith & Berg): a group becomes trustworthy only as members extend trust first — "it is only through trusting that trust is built." Distinguishing the private register (being liked) from the public one (being respected) lets a leader hold standards without rupturing the relationship.
- **Example in context:** Ganz, a "Jewish white guy from Harvard" arriving in 1964 Mississippi, asks his mentor Hollis Watkins how to connect with people he'd depend on for his life. Watkins stops the car and says one word — "Respect" — then: first listen and hear; second ask and learn; third show respect and you'll get it; fourth, don't try to be anyone but yourself.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Newcomers don't stick" is often a respect-and-trust failure at first contact. Ganz's sequence (listen, ask, learn, be yourself) is a concrete first-meeting discipline, and the respected-not-liked distinction frees a leader to be accountable rather than ingratiating.
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1 relational-meeting [A4.2] (where respect gets practiced — how-to routes out); cross-cutting-ties [A4.6] (trust across difference); peer-accountability [A8.3] (being respected enables accountability).

> verbatim: "Respect is what you do, not what you have." — Hollis Watkins (quoted in Ganz)

### Strong ties and weak ties
concepts:    [A4.6 cross-cutting-ties-before-conflict]
situations:  [S:cant-recruit] [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the medium
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.2

- **Claim / recommendation:** Deliberately build relationships with people *not* like you, not only with people like you. Ganz uses sociologist Mark Granovetter's distinction: *strong ties* are homogeneous (people like us), *weak ties* heterogeneous (people unlike us). Strong ties support but can close you into an echo chamber; weak ties take more work but open the circle to new information, perspectives, and resources.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Strong ties quickly form a closed, limited circle of people and resources; a wide set of weak ties builds the broader networks organizing needs (the same way people find jobs through weak ties). Neither is "good" or "bad" — they do different work — but for broad, inclusive purposes, weak ties are the keys to success.
- **Example in context:** Granovetter's Boston West End — residents fragmented into intense ethnic/religious/familial strong-tie clusters couldn't combine to resist urban renewal, while more diverse, weak-tie communities collaborated and secured outside support. (Community-scale; the principle applies to who a small group reaches toward.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't recruit" and homogeneity often go together — a group that only knows people like itself runs out of reach. Building cross-cutting ties widens recruitment and, per the register, also lays the relationships across lines *before* conflict hardens.
- **Related concepts:** the-relationship-web [A4.4] (growing the network); recruiting-as-relational [A4.5] (reaching beyond your circle); composition-and-skills [A5.1] (diversity as a team asset).

### Motivation comes from how the work is designed
concepts:    [A3.1 intrinsic-motivation] [A5.4 work-and-task-design]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** When volunteers fizzle, redesign the *task*, not just the pep talk. Ganz (via Richard Hackman's motivational task design) argues that well-designed tasks make people more motivated, more willing to take initiative, and more committed. Stop treating routine work as "shit work" handed to the lowest-status people as a test of commitment; design it as an opportunity for leadership.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Intrinsically motivating tasks produce three felt states — *experienced meaningfulness* (it matters), *experienced responsibility* (it's up to me), and *understood results* (I can see how I'm doing). You engineer those by building five characteristics into the work: **task significance, task identity, skill variety, autonomy, and feedback.** Intrinsic motivation outlasts extrinsic reward because it comes from the work itself, not from pay, pats, or medals. *(This overlaps Fisher's structural/task-design material in* The Collective Edge*; the theory is shared — Hackman — so treat Ganz's contribution as the organizing-specific framing and the phone-bank case, and cross-link rather than restate.)*
- **Example in context:** Molly and Ashley at the phone bank (Layer 0). The same four-hour shift scores 5/25 vs. 25/25 across the five criteria purely on design — and the well-designed version produces re-commitment and surfaces who could take on more. Ganz's line: "Any task, properly designed, is an entry point to leadership."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Volunteers are flaky," "no one takes initiative," and "the work's all on a few people" are usually task-design problems, not character problems. The five criteria give a first-timer a concrete diagnostic to redesign work so motivation and initiative rise.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (responsibility is the live wire); work-and-task-design [A5.4] (this *is* that leaf); developing-leaders [A9.1] (a well-designed task is a leadership ladder); composition-and-skills [A5.1] (Hackman's structural lens, see Fisher).

### Responsibility, not tasks: ownership and voice
concepts:    [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand people *responsibility for an outcome*, not a list of tasks. The difference Ganz draws: "Would you make these fifty phone calls?" versus "Would you take responsibility for getting ten people to the meeting?" The first gives a script; the second gives tools and lets the person figure out how. People commit to what they help shape and own.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Delegating tasks turns a person into a "yo-yo" — do this, come back, do that, come back — and they remain helpers fulfilling *your* responsibility. Delegating an outcome makes it *theirs*; "when a person has accepted responsibility, the motivation for the work follows." Ganz borrows Micah Sifry's contrast between "entaskment" and empowerment. Crucially, don't keep making the responsibility easier until nothing's left — the challenge is to motivate people to accept the level needed to get it done.
- **Example in context:** the meeting-turnout ask — assigning fifty calls vs. asking someone to own getting ten people there. Only the second develops a leader and reliably produces the result, because the owner adapts and runs with it.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" is frequently the predictable result of giving tasks instead of responsibility. Ganz's reframe — outcomes not tasks, with voice in how — is the lever that turns passive helpers into people who take ownership.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (the developmental version); intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (responsibility is one of the three felt states); accountability via peer-accountability [A8.3] (responsibility is real only if accountable).

### The valuable ask, not the easy ask
concepts:    [A3.3 transactional-vs-relational-ask]
situations:  [S:scared-to-ask-people] [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Make a *valuable* ask, not an *easy* one. The common instinct — lower the cost so it's easier to say yes — backfires; "the key is not in making an *easy* ask but rather in making a *valuable* ask." In civic mode you are not asking a favor; you are offering people who share your concern an opportunity to do something that matters.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People commit because a contribution feels worth it — "I am more likely to commit if I believe my contribution will really make a difference." Trivializing the ask ("here's my one dollar a year to solve child poverty") signals the whole effort is trivial. And asking creates the tension that forces a clear choice; failing to ask "fail[s] to create the tension required to elicit a clear choice" and disrespects the other person's agency.
- **Example in context:** the half-hearted easy ask versus a specific, meaningful one; Ganz notes most of us "really do take the commitments we make seriously — which is one reason we avoid making them," so a serious ask is met with the seriousness it deserves.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This anchors the mobilizing/organizing lens at ask-level: an extractive, easy, transactional ask produces a transactional relationship; a valuable, relational ask produces commitment. For the leader scared to ask, it reframes the ask as a gift of opportunity, not an imposition.
- **Related concepts:** the-ask / escalating-commitment [A3.5] (the mechanics, routed); transformational-vs-transactional [A2.5] (the same lens, group-level); fear-of-asking [A1.5] (the block this dissolves).

### Commitment compounds — and must be kept
concepts:    [A3.4 commitment-over-time]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:low-attendance] [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Ask committed people for *more*, not less, and build a culture where commitments are kept. Counter to intuition, "commitments generate more commitments": someone who makes a first commitment is more likely to make a bigger one later, so don't hesitate to escalate. And treat broken commitments — even small ones, arriving at 10:15 for a 10:00 — as corrosive, because "when we break commitments, even in small ways, the commitment itself begins to mean less."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Social psychology's *consistency theory*: once we commit (e.g., sign a pledge), we make later choices consistent with it — signing leads to volunteering leads to giving, and vice versa. Underneath sits Albert Hirschman's distinction: *economic* resources (money, equipment) deplete with use; *moral* resources (commitment, skill, relationships) *grow* with use. So commitment is a renewable resource that compounds — unless a culture of broken commitments teaches everyone it means little.
- **Example in context:** the grape boycott ladder — sign a pledge not to buy grapes → volunteer a picket shift → captain a picket line → join a local boycott committee → quit your job and join full-time. Each step made the next more likely. (Movement-scale; the compounding mechanism applies to any volunteer.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Volunteers are flaky" and "we lost momentum" are commitment-culture problems. Ganz's counter is both to *escalate* (ask more of the committed) and to *protect* commitments (follow up, keep your own), since failure to honor them has, he says, "an exponentially negative effect."
- **Related concepts:** the-ask / escalating-commitment [A3.5] (the ladder mechanics); money-and-motivation [A3.6] (moral vs economic resources); peer-accountability [A8.3] (keeping commitments among peers).

### The ladder of escalating commitment
concepts:    [A3.5 the-ask]
situations:  [S:cant-recruit] [S:scared-to-ask-people]
handoff:     [H:recruitment]
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Offer multiple entry points at escalating levels of commitment, and ask for each one specifically, now. Ganz's "four Cs" name the shape of an ask — **connect** (who you are and why), **context** (the urgent challenge and why they matter — a story of now), **commitment** (a specific, dated, joyful ask: "can we count on you?"), and **catapult** (give a yes real responsibility and a concrete plan to get there). *Why-level kept here; the scripts, channels, and recruitment mechanics route to the recruitment coach.*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A campaign that offers many levels of contribution lets everyone find a rung and widens participation — and "the wider the opportunity to contribute, the wider the possible participation, and the more widely distributed the responsibility — and credit." Commitments are made "now, or not at all"; "I'll try" doesn't count, so get the name and number and follow up. People with a concrete plan ("how will you get there?") are much more likely to actually show.
- **Example in context:** at one 1975 point, pollster Lou Harris found 12% of Americans — some 17 million people — were boycotting grapes, precisely because the boycott offered a rung for everyone from a shopper shunning grapes to a full-time volunteer. (Movement-scale; the ladder logic is what a group reuses.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't recruit" and "scared to ask" both improve when you stop making one big scary ask and instead build a ladder of valuable, specific asks. The *understanding* lives here; the recruitment coach owns the how-to.
- **Related concepts:** commitment-over-time [A3.4] (why escalation works); the-valuable-ask [A3.3] (specific and valuable, not easy); recruiting-as-relational [A4.5] (the relational frame for the ask).

### Money, time, and "shit work"
concepts:    [A3.6 money-and-motivation]
situations:  [S:burning-out] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Don't let money quietly replace time-and-commitment, and don't treat routine work as a status test. Ganz warns that relying on outside money rather than inside time "may get a win but fail to empower our people," and that values-based leaders too often dump routine tasks on the lowest-status members "as tests of commitment rather than opportunities for leadership development."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Two reasons time beats money. First, political scientist Sidney Verba's point that *time is far more widely distributed than money*, so relying on time strengthens democracy and broadens who can participate. Second, Hirschman's moral-vs-economic resources: paid-for compliance buys time and obedience but builds none of the commitment, skill, and relationships that grow with use. Where resources come from shapes who holds power: outside money empowers the funder, inside contribution empowers the constituency.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's warning about civil society becoming "the clients of philanthropic patronage," and the funder dynamic where meetings start to revolve around "what the funders will support" — whoever's resources make the thing possible gets the say.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a small volunteer group this is a caution against two shortcuts: paying to make flaky volunteer work reliable (which hollows out commitment) and offloading drudgery onto the few (which burns them out and wastes leadership opportunities). Design the routine work to motivate; build on contributed time.
- **Related concepts:** intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (why money crowds out); commitment-over-time [A3.4] (moral resources grow); volunteer-burnout [A10.1] (the few who do all the "shit work").

### The bounded, stable, diverse leadership team
concepts:    [A5.1 composition-and-skills]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:cant-make-decisions] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build a *real* team, in Hackman's sense, defined by three conditions: **bounded** (it's clear who's on it and how you join or leave), **stable** (regular meetings, terms, and commitment — not a revolving door), and **diverse** (a range of skills, viewpoints, and constituencies). org-check: this is the leader composing *their own* working team, squarely in scope. *(Heavy overlap with Fisher's "real team" in* The Collective Edge *— same Hackman source; keep Ganz's organizing framing and cross-link rather than restate the theory.)*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A bounded team isn't exclusionary; like a basketball team, cabin crew, or string quartet, it just has clear expectations about who serves and why — and you can't build the trust cycle without knowing who's in. Stability matters because half-attendance means every meeting re-briefs absentees, wasting the group's scarcest resource. Diversity is an asset because leadership "is not a one-person-who-is-good-at-everything job"; the most salient diversity for organizing is rooted in the constituency's identity (age, neighborhood, role, background).
- **Example in context:** Ganz lists movement teams against the lone-leader myth — Moses/Aaron/Miriam; King/Abernathy/Parks/Robinson/Nixon in Montgomery — each a leadership team, not a solo hero. And he notes most leaders have only experienced *dysfunctional* teams: unclear who'll step up, missing information when deciding, everyone leaving with a different idea of the outcome.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A team that's unbounded, unstable, or homogeneous produces flaky participation, stalled decisions, and work piling on a few. The three conditions are the leader's design checklist before the group even starts working.
- **Related concepts:** group-size-and-nesting [A5.2] (small teams, nested — see snowflake); goal-design [A5.3] (the team's shared purpose); functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (interdependent roles); composition (Fisher, *Collective Edge*) for the underlying real-team theory.

### Three decisions: the team's shared purpose
concepts:    [A5.3 goal-design]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Start with "who are my people"
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Have the team explicitly decide its **shared purpose** — a *what* (common goal), *who* (our people), and *how* (the activities) — and do the work of agreeing it yourselves rather than having it assigned or assumed. This is one of Ganz's "three decisions for an effective team" (shared purpose, norms, roles). A self-governing team "must accept responsibility for deciding on a purpose themselves, even if their purpose is nested within broader organizational goals."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** "Without a shared purpose, every decision relitigates what our purpose really is." You cannot shortcut it by aggregating preferences (Ganz refuses an algorithm proposal — that produces "the shared purpose of the algorithm, not of their people"); members have to hear each other, debate differences, and find commonality they own. Beginning from convergence, not difference, makes resolving the rest far less arduous.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's one-sentence exercise — each member writes one sentence on the team's goal, constituency, and activities; a facilitator clusters the words into three columns, surfaces what's shared, runs a second round (which converges further), then picks the sentence closest to the whole and asks its author for a proposal. "We may all care for the natural world, but I'm for whales, you're for trees" — start from the shared "save the environment," not the split.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Unclear what we're doing" and "decisions keep getting relitigated" are the direct symptoms of an unset or assumed shared purpose. The one-sentence exercise is a concrete, repeatable method a first-timer can run.
- **Related concepts:** shared-purpose [A2.1] (the why the team serves); norms-and-operating-agreements [A5.5] (the second decision); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (relitigation is a decision-rules gap too).

### Norms only exist in practice
concepts:    [A5.5 norms-and-operating-agreements]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:     [H:comms]
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.1

- **Claim / recommendation:** Set norms explicitly and early, treat norm-setting as a "constitutional moment," and enforce norms with light, shared corrections — because "norms only really exist in practice." Norms cover how you decide, honor commitments, manage conflict, and respect time. *Communication tooling and channel norms route to the comms coach; the why-level discipline stays here.*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Norms left implicit still form — just not the ones you'd choose. Ganz (citing Hackman) notes we default to reacting to whatever grabs attention and to keeping harmony, so explicit norms counter those tendencies. A norm violated without consequence *redefines* the norm: "We agree to meet at 10:00. Someone wanders in at 10:30. No one says anything… A new norm has been created: roll in by 10:30." Norm-setting is the one moment Ganz says consensus is genuinely required.
- **Example in context:** the *norm correction* — late arrivals do a ten-second dance, not a fine — so accountability is shared and humorous, not one person policing. And the sacred meeting: coordinating Pelosi's 1987 campaign, Ganz refused to take a call from the candidate herself during the daily coordinators' meeting ("I'll call her as soon as we're done") — and they never had trouble holding the meeting again.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Flaky reliability and endless re-litigation are often missing-norms problems. Ganz's moves — set norms explicitly up front, make a norm correction, and protect a sacred regular time — are concrete and cheap.
- **Related concepts:** how-norms-form-and-become-culture [A6.1] (norms harden into culture); peer-accountability [A8.3] (corrections shared, not policed); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (the decision norm is the most important).

> verbatim: "Norm violations that have no consequences redefine the norm." — Ganz

### Boundaries: every inclusion also excludes
concepts:    [A5.6 boundaries-and-membership]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Bound your group clearly — "a community… without boundaries is not a community" — while knowing that "every act of inclusion also excludes." The question isn't *whether* to bound but *how*, in a way that gives a shared identity and makes the responsibilities of membership clear. org-check: a leader bounding *their own* group is in scope; designing the org's membership architecture is not.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ganz uses Hirschman's "voice in return for loyalty": members gain rights (choosing direction, deciding policy) in return for obligations (showing up, honoring norms, respecting decisions). Without voice, the only option when things don't go your way is *exit* — how markets work — whereas obligations give you "an interest in making the community work, even when… you disagree with it." Amorphous, unbounded structures breed confusion and resentment about what people are actually being asked to do.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's caution about confusing chosen commitments with imposed bureaucratic obligations — "I have confused the responsibilities of membership… with the burdens of a top-down organization." Clear boundaries let members embrace responsibilities; vague ones produce dysfunction.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Flakiness and newcomers-not-sticking often trace to fuzzy membership — no clear in/out, no clear ask, no voice-for-obligation bargain. Bounding (who's a member, what they owe, what they get) is a leader-level fix. *The broader inclusion/exclusion and federated-structure debate is org-level — routed.*
- **Related concepts:** the-bounded-stable-diverse-team [A5.1] (bounding the working team); collective-agreement-tension [A7.5] (voice-for-loyalty as decision legitimacy); onboarding-newcomers [A9.7] (bringing people across the boundary well).

### Launching with the heart — and relaunching
concepts:    [A5.7 launch-and-relaunch]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:group-too-big-or-chaotic]
handoff:     [H:events]
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat the launch as a real event that turns individuals into a team, launch "with the heart as well as the head and hands," and plan a *relaunch* at the midpoint when a group stalls. *Event mechanics and meeting logistics route to the events coach; the why-level — what a launch is for and when to reset — stays here.*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A launch isn't onboarding paperwork; it introduces the concepts, skills, and values that found the team and gets specific commitments. The "heart" matters because structuring a team is stressful and conflictual, so emotional connection restores harmony after contentious debate. And groups naturally become receptive to fresh analysis at their *midpoint* (Ganz cites organizational-culture research: like a midlife crisis, the realization that less time remains than has elapsed) — so a planned midpoint relaunch rides a natural phenomenon rather than fighting it.
- **Example in context:** Camp Obama (Burbank, July 2007): 200+ volunteers "arrived Friday afternoon as individuals but left Sunday afternoon as members of leadership teams," each committed to recruiting at least twenty people back home. And the launch-with-heart practices: brainstorming a team name (Just Organizers, Justice Jedis, 7 Deer Dancing) and a team chant, which build shared identity and let members who shine less at head/hands work flourish. (Large training; the move — launch to convert individuals into a committed team — is general.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Lost momentum after launch" is precisely the gap Ganz addresses: a weak launch never made a team, and no planned relaunch means the natural midpoint opening is missed. A chaotic, too-big group is a signal to subdivide and relaunch as nested teams.
- **Related concepts:** cohesion-and-belonging [A6.3] (name/chant build the felt "we"); common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (the stall a relaunch resets); group-size-and-nesting [A5.2] (relaunch as nested teams when too big).

### Learning is the work: explain, model, practice, debrief
concepts:    [A6.6 learning-together] [A6.1 how-norms-form-and-become-culture]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1

- **Claim / recommendation:** Make candid, structured learning a standing practice, not an afterthought. Ganz's four-step pedagogy — **explain, model, practice, debrief** — applies to learning any practice, and the debrief is where lessons "stick." Build a "brave space" with member-set norms and close every session with **takeaways, pluses, and deltas.**
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** "The way we learn is what we learn." Debriefing forces articulation, which is what converts experience into a transferable lesson; doing this routinely "builds in continual learning as a sustained — and expected — practice," and over time that practice hardens into the group's culture. A holding/brave space (mutual respect, assume good intent, a time norm, a norm correction) is what makes giving and receiving critical feedback safe.
- **Example in context:** the bike-learning frame and the session-close ritual of takeaways/pluses/deltas, introduced when launching the 2008 Obama grassroots program and since adapted in countries from Jordan to China. Civic leader Samar Dudin called the approach "a road map, not a blueprint."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Groups that don't debrief lose momentum because they never learn from what happened. The pluses/deltas/takeaways close is a tiny, repeatable habit that builds a learning culture and keeps the group adapting.
- **Related concepts:** measurement-as-learning [A6.6] (the evidence side of learning); psychological-safety [A6.5] (the brave space); norms-and-operating-agreements [A5.5] (the norms that hold the space).

### Measurement as learning: evidence-based organizing
concepts:    [A6.6 learning-together]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Tie action to specific, measurable outcomes with deadlines, and use the numbers to *learn*, not to control. "If you can't count it, it didn't happen." Run post-action evaluations as a routine (the military's After Action Report; Ganz's pluses/deltas/takeaways), and keep metrics fully transparent so they read as shared learning, not surveillance.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Without clear outcomes you can evaluate neither success nor failure, so you can't improve — and the absence of metrics is part of why volunteer work gets dismissed as unreliable ("you can't fire volunteers… except you can"). Measurement makes visible who's succeeding and why, so the whole group can learn from the best. But punitive metrics (fire the bottom 10%, count calls as a proxy for power) measure what's *countable* rather than what's *valuable* and breed suspicion — transparency and using the numbers in front of the constituency is the antidote.
- **Example in context:** in the grape boycott they counted grape carloads arriving in a city and customers turned away from a store, so local captains could feel their impact and learn. Ganz's coaching-by-comparison: "Joe, describe what you're doing… Sam, you haven't gotten the training on commits? Joe, would you coach Sam?" — confronting reality while distancing it from blame makes everyone a learner and a teacher.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't make decisions" and "volunteers seem flaky" both improve with concrete, shared metrics: decisions get an evidence base, and reliability becomes visible and coachable. The key discipline is metrics-for-learning, never metrics-for-punishment.
- **Related concepts:** learning-together [A6.6] (this is its evidence arm); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (comparison as coaching); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (evidence informs decisions).

### Why people stop showing up: stall patterns
concepts:    [A6.4 common-stall-patterns]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5, ch.7, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize the recurring ways a group loses momentum and treat each as a fixable design problem, not as proof people "just don't care." Ganz opens the Action chapter with three: volunteers who phone-bank once or twice and vanish; one person who always runs an operation (and a couple of friends) while no one else shows; and the leader who must tell everyone exactly how to do every job.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Each pattern has a structural cause: the vanishing volunteer met a badly designed, un-motivating task; the lone operator was never asked to *develop* others (and "could this be an opportunity?"); the tell-everyone-everything leader delegated tasks, not responsibility. A further stall: campaigns that "don't take time to reflect, adjust, and retrain end up burning through their human resources." And revolving-door teams where "half of the meeting is used to bring those who didn't show up… up to date" stall on instability.
- **Example in context:** the three opening scenarios, plus the diagnosis that the lone tabling operator is a missed leadership-development opportunity, and the warning against skipping rest/retraining between peaks ("We don't have time for that!" — "This is a mistake").
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Low attendance" and "lost momentum after launch" are this domain's core symptoms. Ganz's move is to map the symptom to its structural cause — task design, missing development, task-not-responsibility, no relaunch, instability — rather than blaming the volunteers.

### Power is relational: "power with" and "power over," and three faces
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.1, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Stop thinking of power as a thing you possess and start seeing it as an interdependent relationship based on the balance of needs and resources: "If you need my resources more than I need yours, who's got the power? I do." Distinguish creating *power with* others (collaboration when interests converge — a credit union, a co-op, a carpool) from challenging *power over* you (when interests diverge, find the resources you hold that the other side needs).
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because power is relational, you build it by changing the balance of needs and resources — "how can I make it more costly for you to resist the change than to accept it?" (Mary Parker Follett's with/over distinction). Power also has *three faces* (Ganz via Lukes): the first is visible (who wins/loses), the second is who decides and sets the rules, the third is the structural question of who benefits and why "things just are this way." The strategic challenge is to engage the urgent first face while building capacity to reach the third.
- **Example in context:** the prickly-pear saying — "no one comes to visit the prickly pear except when it's bearing fruit" — for why politicians appear only at election time (a needs/resources reading of power). And the farmworkers: workers need work, growers need labor; the strike, then the boycott, shifted the balance.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time group leader often experiences powerlessness as fixed. Ganz's relational frame is a tool: locate what your people uniquely hold that others need, and you find leverage. It's also the foundation the whole strategy chapter builds on.
- **Related concepts:** strategy-is-resourcefulness [A7.1] (turning resources into power); the-power-theory-of-change [A7.1] (the four-question analysis); building relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (relationships are a primary resource).

### Strategy is resourcefulness, not resources
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing] [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Strategy is "how you turn what you have into what you need to get what you want" — a verb you already practice (you strategized the morning you overslept and missed the bus). It's not big-as-opposed-to-small tactics; it's a *theory of change* (an if-then hypothesis) that guides targeting, timing, and tactics. Treat it as motivated, intentional, proactive, creative, and *nested*, and expect to adapt rather than execute a fixed plan. *Register-gap: tagged A7.1 for want of a strategy leaf.*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Strategizing is creative adaptation, not planning-then-implementing — Mintzberg's potter shaping clay on the wheel; "plans are useless, but planning is indispensable" (Eisenhower, in Ganz). Its central engine is *resourcefulness*: "Power is thus not simply a matter of resources, but also of the resourcefulness with which those resources are used." Because strategy exists only in time, the value of a resource shifts with context (a full granary in a famine), and the opposition's reactions keep changing the field.
- **Example in context:** David and Goliath as a strategy story (not a nonviolence one). David can't move in the king's armor — the conventional resources Goliath masters — so he sees the fight "through different eyes," reframes Goliath as "just another bear, just another lion," and uses the resources he *has*: five stones, a sling, skill, and his opponent's arrogance. Resourcefulness beats resources.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Group leaders set local strategy and theories of change; Ganz's claim is that being outgunned is not decisive — moving the fulcrum (resourcefulness) is. This also names the most common failure: "tactics in search of a strategy," activities done because "that's what we always do," with no theory of change behind them.
- **Related concepts:** the-power-theory-of-change [A7.1] (the hypothesis itself); building-and-using-power [A7.1] (resources→power); who-strategizes [A5.1] (resourcefulness is generated by particular people).

### The power theory of change, the power map, and the strategic goal
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A2.1 shared-purpose]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing] [S:group-lost-its-why]
handoff:
anchors to:  Start with "who are my people" · Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** Base local strategy on a *power* theory of change and a *power map*, then commit to one specific *strategic goal*. The power theory of change asks four questions: (1) what are our needs? (2) who holds the resources to meet them? (3) what are *their* needs? (4) what do we have that they need? A power map visualizes every relevant actor by their interests and resources and their interdependencies. *Register-gap: tagged A7.1 + A2.1; the campaign-operations detail of running this is compressed.*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Most popular theories of change ignore power (better education, more data, more storytelling, impact philanthropy) and so leave a logic gap — "then a miracle occurs." A power theory of change closes it: if we deploy our resources here, we affect the needs of those whose resources we need, such that resisting becomes costlier than conceding, so we get what we need. A good *strategic goal* then (1) focuses resources on one outcome that unlocks bigger ones, (2) converts the constituency's resources into power and capacity, (3) leverages your strength against the opposition's weakness, (4) is visible/significant enough to motivate, and (5) is contagious/emulable. Capture it in one sentence: "We are organizing [who] to [what outcome] through [how] by [when]."
- **Example in context:** the Montgomery Bus Boycott didn't claim to assault institutionalized racism; it focused on the *segregated bus* — a plausible target where everyone held a resource (their feet), turning riders' dependence on the bus company into the company's dependence on the community. A lawsuit might have benefited people but wouldn't have *empowered* them. (Movement-scale; the four questions and the five goal-features are the reusable tools.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group "unclear what it's doing" usually lacks a power analysis and a single committed goal. Ganz's tools — the four questions, the power map, the five features, the one-sentence goal — are exactly what a local leader uses to make strategy concrete, and the strategic goal becomes the core of a story of now.
- **Related concepts:** building-and-using-power [A7.1] (the relational base); start-with-who-are-my-people [A2.1] (questions 1–2 are about your people); public-narrative [A2.2] (the goal lives in the story of now).

### Who strategizes: strategic capacity from a diverse, embedded team
concepts:    [A5.1 composition-and-skills] [A7.2 decisions-as-shared-process]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.4

- **Claim / recommendation:** *Who* strategizes matters as much as what the strategy is — so assemble a strategy team that is diverse and embedded in the constituency, combining insider and outsider perspectives, in a brave space with a clear decision process. Good strategy is "more often the work of a good strategic team a good leader has assembled" than of a lone genius.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** This is Ganz's *strategic capacity* thesis (from his research, *Why David Sometimes Wins*): innovation is facilitated by diverse, salient knowledge — multiple viewpoints reveal that multiple solutions exist. Insiders hold needed information; outsiders see possibilities insiders can't (David, the shepherd-outsider, saw what the warriors couldn't). And the strategists must be the people *accountable* for making it work — which is also a coaching lesson Ganz learned painfully.
- **Example in context:** the Montgomery Bus Boycott leadership team — a seamstress/organizer who rode the buses, a respected preacher, a Pullman porter, a Black women's-group leader, the only Black lawyer in town, and a very new young minister (King). And Ganz coaching a union leader: they devised "interesting, exciting, creative strategies" that went nowhere until Ganz realized he was imagining strategy *he* could make work — but the leader was the one accountable for executing it. "Strategizing… is a creature of the creator or creators."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that "can't make decisions" on direction may have the wrong people (or one person) doing the strategizing. Ganz's prescription — a diverse, embedded team that owns the strategy and a clear way to decide — reframes the problem as composition and process, and links strategy to who's accountable.
- **Related concepts:** the-bounded-stable-diverse-team [A5.1] (the same composition logic); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (how the team decides); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (the leader coaches but doesn't own the coachee's strategy).

### Forest and trees: distributing strategic authority
concepts:    [A7.3 autonomy-vs-direction] [A7.6 hierarchy-just-enough]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.4, ch.6, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Good strategy needs both the overview "from the top of the mountain" and the intimate "view in the valley" — so push strategic authority down to where the local knowledge is, while keeping enough central direction to coordinate. Hold only as much hierarchy as coordination requires: neither the "I'm the leader" hub nor the leaderless "we're all leaders" works.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The Greek *strategos* on the hill and the *taktikos* in the valley each imagine they know the whole truth; "a fog may settle between" them, and strategizing requires *both* truths. So local leaders need the responsibility *and the authority* to organize their own turf and adapt to local nuance. Ganz frames the underlying tension: "We may all participate in robust debate about whether to take the hill, but we structure ourselves differently when it comes to actually taking the hill" — direction on the *what*, autonomy on the *how*. *The org-design version (federated structure, where authority sits across an organization) is org-level — routed.*
- **Example in context:** the 2008 Obama campaign nested authority "all the way up — at the precinct, region, district, city, state, and national levels — with coaching, training, accountability, and support available at every level," distributing real decision authority rather than reducing volunteers to messengers reading a script. (Large campaign; the principle — distribute the authority to where the local knowledge is — applies to any multi-part effort.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" can be an authority problem: people given tasks but no authority can't act. And "can't make decisions" can mean direction and local autonomy aren't sorted. Ganz's split — direct the what, delegate the how, with authority to match responsibility — is the fix.
- **Related concepts:** autonomy-vs-direction [A7.3] (this *is* that leaf); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (authority must accompany responsibility); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (deciding whether to take the hill).

### Decisions as a shared process
concepts:    [A7.2 decisions-as-shared-process]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Decide *how you'll decide* before you need to, and run decisions as a shared, stepwise process. A team's "most important norm is how the team will make decisions"; agree a method (vote, consensus, or delegating to an individual) up front, because without decision rules "every substantive debate can easily turn into a process debate, leading to irresolution, anxiety, and frustration."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ganz offers a sequence: define the problem, establish criteria, brainstorm possibilities, synthesize, formulate options, evaluate against the criteria, decide. Decision *rules* let the group move and get work done "even if everyone doesn't agree" — they depersonalize disagreement and prevent endless re-litigation. This is the constitutional decision among the team's three (purpose, norms, roles), and the one Ganz says genuinely requires consensus to set.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's stepwise decision process and his insistence that norm-setting around decisions is "a constitutional moment." Paired with the structure chapter's point that formal procedures (even *Robert's Rules*) help by depersonalizing disagreement.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't make decisions" and "decisions keep getting relitigated" are the exact symptoms of absent decision rules. The fix is to set the method early and follow a shared process, so disagreement stays on the issue and the group can actually move.
- **Related concepts:** norms-and-operating-agreements [A5.5] (the decision norm is the key one); collective-agreement-tension [A7.5] (why decision rules legitimate action); task-vs-relationship-conflict [A8.1] (process depersonalizes disagreement).

### Dissent is not disloyalty
concepts:    [A8.2 productive-dissent]
situations:  [S:everyone-just-agrees] [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Protect dissent as a source of learning and accountability, not a threat to loyalty. A "loyal opposition" holds leaders accountable and surfaces alternatives that drive adaptation — but only if the group has "agreed-upon ways we can continue collaborating even when we disagree" (decision rules). A leader should actively "affirm the single voice that the rest of the group tries to drown out, rather than joining the chorus."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Diversity and unity trade off predictably: "the more homogeneous a group, the better it can be at taking the hill, but the more diverse a group, the better the decisions it will make about whether to take the hill in the first place." So dissent buys *better decisions* (at some cost to performance), and unanimity buys *better performance* (at some cost to decisions). The danger is that a group "may choose to reject the carrier of the message so that it does not have to deal with the message" — scapegoating the dissenter instead of hearing the dissent.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's structure chapter formalizes this as one of three ways to manage tensions, and he suggests practical scaffolds — a devil's-advocate role, structured debate, *Robert's Rules* — to keep dissent legitimate. (The examples are organizational; the practice scales to any team meeting.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Everyone just agrees" is a groupthink warning, not a sign of health. Ganz's move is to build dissent in deliberately — affirm the minority voice, give dissent a structured channel, and pair it with decision rules so the group can still act.
- **Related concepts:** psychological-safety [A6.5] (safety to speak is the precondition); task-vs-relationship-conflict [A8.1] (keep dissent on the issue); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (rules that let you move despite disagreement).

> verbatim: dissent on its own "can be divisive and lead to scapegoating." — Ganz

### Keep the conflict on the issue, not the person
concepts:    [A8.1 task-vs-relationship-conflict]
situations:  [S:two-members-clashing] [S:conflict-got-personal]
handoff:
anchors to:  Narrative is how a leader moves people
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** When a group disagrees, keep the disagreement on the task and off the person — and use formal procedure to do it. Ganz notes that "formal procedures for debate, discussion, voting and evaluation can help by depersonalizing disagreements." The danger to guard against is a group treating concerns as "belonging" to the person expressing them rather than as the group's own.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Disagreement over substance is generative; disagreement that becomes personal ruptures the relationships the group runs on. Depersonalizing devices — agreed decision rules, structured debate, even *Robert's Rules* — let people contest the issue without it reading as an attack. Ganz also draws a moral line via Baldwin: disagreement is livable until it denies someone's humanity and right to exist.
- **Example in context:** James Baldwin (quoted in Ganz): "We can disagree and still love each other" — unless the disagreement is rooted in denying the other's humanity. The line marks where productive task conflict ends.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Two members clashing" and "the conflict got personal" are this leaf's symptoms. Ganz's prescription is structural (procedures that depersonalize) plus a clear moral boundary, rather than suppressing the disagreement — which would forfeit its value.
- **Related concepts:** productive-dissent [A8.2] (why the disagreement is worth keeping); repair-and-mutual-accountability [A8.5] (restoring the relationship after a rupture); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (rules that depersonalize).

### Peer accountability as learning, not policing
concepts:    [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Make accountability shared, regular, specific, and timely — and aimed at learning, not catching people out. "The point is not to catch someone doing something wrong and punish them"; it's to learn what results people are getting so everyone can learn from them. Responsibility is only real if the person is clearly accountable for it, and given the *authority* to do what they accepted.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Among peers with no boss, accountability can't be one person policing everyone (which makes them "take all the heat"); a shared *norm correction* spreads it and keeps it light. And accountability is the precondition for the group's most important learning — "systematic reflection on our own experience" — so without it, the after-action learning is impossible. If you see someone make a mistake, "go directly to them, not around them. Do not take care of it for them. This is really a matter of basic respect."
- **Example in context:** the humorous norm correction (a ten-second dance for lateness) so the group, not one enforcer, holds the line; and Jethro's three-thousand-year-old advice echoed by Ganz — choose people of good character, train them, give them real responsibility (with the authority to match).
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Flakiness and re-litigation persist when accountability is either absent or dumped on one policing leader. Ganz's frame — shared, regular, specific, timely, learning-oriented, with authority matching responsibility — lets peers hold each other without a boss and without resentment.
- **Related concepts:** norms-and-operating-agreements [A5.5] (the norm correction); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (responsibility needs authority); measurement-as-learning [A6.6] (accountability feeds learning).

### Identify, recruit, and develop leadership
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.2

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat finding and growing leaders as a core, ongoing job, and identify them by *deeds, not words.* An organizer's task is "to identify, recruit, and develop leadership who can identify, recruit, and develop more leadership… in a cascade." Look for the person with "the sparkle in their eyes" — who asks good questions, learns, has *people* around them as collaborators, and takes pride when a mentee shines.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Leaders are "people committed to change, open to learning, in relationship with others, and able to accept responsibility" — and you confirm leadership by evidence: do others look to them, do they turn people out, do they get the signatures? Beware cultural bias about who "looks like" a leader, and beware self-promoters. Distinguish leaders from *activists*: hardworking activists staff the phone bank, but "effective leaders turn activists into leaders who can in turn activate others." You only develop judgment about whom to recruit by risking choices and learning from them.
- **Example in context:** the farmworkers' phrase for false leaders — *de los dientes pa'fuera* ("from the teeth outward"): three men claimed to lead a community, were asked to bring twenty or thirty members to a meeting that night, and *zero* showed — not even the three. Identify by who actually turns people out, not who claims the title.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" and "I'm doing everything myself" are, at root, leadership-development gaps. Ganz's discipline — look for evidence of leadership, distinguish leaders from activists, and make developing them a standing practice — is how a group stops depending on one person.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (how you grow them); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (the developmental method); recruiting-as-relational [A4.5] (recruitment as relationship); the-dominating-or-toxic-member [A8.4] (the false-leader who shows up first).

### Delegate outcomes, not tasks
concepts:    [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand over ownership of *results*, not chores. "Developing leadership is not about assigning tasks. It is about offering opportunities to accept responsibility." Give people the tools and the authority to figure out *how*, and resist the urge to keep shrinking the responsibility until nothing is left.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Delegated tasks make people "yo-yos" fulfilling *your* responsibility; delegated outcomes make the work theirs, so "the person takes it and runs with it" and your role becomes helping them meet *their* responsibility (Sifry's "entaskment vs. empowerment"). But delegation is genuinely risky — "you may delegate to the wrong people, and they may let you down" — which is why you start with low-stakes responsibilities and learn judgment, rather than not delegating at all (which "will stifle growth").
- **Example in context:** "Would you make these fifty phone calls?" (a script, a task) versus "Would you take responsibility for getting ten people to the meeting?" (tools, authority, ownership). Only the second develops a leader and reliably gets the result.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the concrete mechanism that breaks the "doing everything myself / work's all on a few people / burning out" cluster. Outcomes-not-tasks both distributes the load and develops people, which is the only durable exit from founder overload.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (the motivational version); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (what delegation prevents); peer-accountability [A8.3] (responsibility needs accountability and authority).

### Letting go: the founder bottleneck and ceding control
concepts:    [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck] [A9.4 succession-and-ceding-control]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:i-cant-let-go] [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small · Leadership is enabling, not doing
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Refuse to be the hub everyone routes through, and find the courage to give up enough control for others to lead. Ganz poses it directly: "Are you willing to give up enough control to let others lead so that you can develop the power you need to achieve your goals? Developing a leadership-rich organization depends on your answer." The leadership test is "how many people you can enable to wear hats," not how many you wear.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The "dot model" — all arrows pointing at one leader (or at no one) — can't scale and collapses if the hub drops out. Leadership development, "like any form of trust, involves risk," so ceding control means accepting that delegates may fail; but refusing to delegate guarantees the group stays small and the leader burns out. Notably, what frees a leader to let go is often restoring the *other* sources of identity and worth that don't depend on the work.
- **Example in context:** Moses and Jethro (Layer 0). Crucially, Jethro first reminds Moses he is also a father, a husband, and blessed — "sources of identity that transcend his work" — and *that* restoration is what gives Moses the courage to delegate. The bottleneck wasn't only structural; it was identity.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "I can't let go," "I'm doing everything myself," and "burning out" are the same problem seen from three angles. Ganz's answer pairs a structural move (snowflake the work outward) with an inner one (ground your worth outside the role so you can risk letting others lead).
- **Related concepts:** enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (the role definition); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (the mechanism of letting go); leader-self-care [A10.2] (identities beyond the work); fear-of-asking-or-delegating [A1.5] (the block).

> verbatim: the test of leadership "is how many people you can enable to wear hats." — a student (quoted in Ganz)

### Functional shared leadership: interdependent roles
concepts:    [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership]
situations:  [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Spread leadership across *functions met by interdependent roles*, not a single all-rounder. Like a soccer team or string quartet, each member can lead with a particular practice (strategy, storytelling), function (data, comms), or constituency (downtown, the eastside) — even if someone is the captain or coordinator. Set roles in the first meeting, match needs to abilities (not popularity), and make the roles interdependent so "everyone has a stake in everyone else's success."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Leadership "is not a one-person-who-is-good-at-everything job"; interdependence is what turns diverse individuals into more than the sum of their parts — "we can't lift a table if we all lift the same corner." When roles are interdependent, people speak up when they need help and the team "swarms to the need," no silos. For organizing specifically, Ganz advises defining roles by *constituency* (each member owns their turf — building relationships, developing leaders there) supplemented by functional roles, because a purely functional structure "will dilute the focus on organizing."
- **Example in context:** Ganz's California statewide team — coordinators by region (three for LA alone, because three times the voters lived there), plus functional roles for admin and press — structured to represent the constituency while covering functions. (Large campaign; the role-design principle scales to a small team.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Work's all on a few people" often means roles were never designed, or were assigned by popularity rather than fit, or weren't interdependent. Ganz's design — constituency-based, interdependent, matched to ability, set early — distributes real leadership rather than just chores.
- **Related concepts:** the-bounded-stable-diverse-team [A5.1] (roles fill the diverse seats); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (a role is an owned outcome); hierarchy-just-enough [A7.6] (a coordinator role, not a boss).

### Coaching: the five-step developmental process
concepts:    [A9.6 coaching-group-members]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small · Leadership is enabling, not doing
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Coach people developmentally — draw out their own capacity rather than telling them what to do — on a *regular schedule*, built into the work. Coaching "is not about giving advice, preaching, making judgments, or telling someone what to do"; it uses three methods (ask questions, listen with head and heart, support by affirming *and* challenging) across five steps. Schedule it routinely so asking for help is normal, not a summons to the principal's office.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ganz's mother taught him *educare* — to "bring out, not put in." So the coach assumes "the coachee most often has all the information needed within," not yet articulated. The pivotal move is **diagnosis**: is the challenge **motivational** (heart — effort, fear, confidence), **educational** (hands — missing skill or information), or **strategic** (head — has skill and motivation but doesn't know how to apply it here)? Getting this wrong wastes everyone's effort (pushing someone to "try harder" when the real gap is strategy). The five steps: **observe** (what do I see/hear?), **diagnose** (which of the three challenges?), **intervene** (matched to the diagnosis — exhort for motivational, model for educational, work an example for strategic; but first ask what *they* think they should do), **debrief** (what did the coachee learn? next steps), **monitor** (periodic check-ins; celebrate success). Coaching beats both micromanagement and sink-or-swim.
- **Example in context:** Fred Ross "lovingly" coaching organizer Larry Tramutola — making him chart every task, every reminder call, every callback ("Reminding is the essence of organizing"), then debriefing each day with relentless probing questions ("Why did you do that? What did you say when he said that?"). It was exhausting, but it taught follow-through and built critical reflection into Larry's own practice — and Ganz learned to coach others the same way (prep before, observe without intervening during, debrief after).
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Coaching is how "enabling others" becomes concrete and how new and stuck people grow. The three-way diagnosis is the single most useful tool here — it stops a leader from applying a motivational fix to a skill problem (or vice versa) — and scheduling it routinely is what makes asking for help safe.
- **Related concepts:** developing-leaders [A9.1] (coaching is the developmental engine); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (coaching supports owned responsibility); psychological-safety [A6.5] (routine coaching de-stigmatizes asking for help).

> verbatim: *educare* "refers to bringing out, not putting in." — Ganz

### Onboarding newcomers
concepts:    [A9.7 onboarding-newcomers]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:     [H:events]
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.7

- **Claim / recommendation:** Onboard new people into the group's *culture and relationships*, not just its forms and tasks. An initial training should "enroll them into an organizing culture," let them build relationships with each other, introduce the core practices and values, and send them out with clear, owned commitments. "Launching leadership with an initial training is… not just about learning everyone's name, filling out forms, reviewing a manual, and giving out assignments." *(Training logistics and event mechanics route to the events coach.)*
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People stick when their first experience connects them to a *we* and to a real responsibility, not when they're handed a manual and a task. Ongoing periodic training then lets earlier learners become teachers, supports adaptation, and introduces newly relevant practices — folding onboarding into a continuous developmental rhythm rather than a one-time intake.
- **Example in context:** Camp Obama (2007–08) as onboarding: volunteers arrived as individuals and left as members of leadership teams, each committed to recruiting at least twenty people back home, having met the five practices and built peer relationships. (Large training; the onboarding logic — culture and relationships and a real commitment, not forms — applies to any newcomer.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Newcomers don't stick" is frequently an onboarding failure: they were processed, not enrolled. Ganz's frame — first contact builds belonging, relationships, and an owned commitment — is the retention lever, and a group can also *learn* from newcomers' fresh perspective.

### Burnout: spread the sacrifice
concepts:    [A10.1 volunteer-burnout]
situations:  [S:burning-out] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Distribute leadership or stay small
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.6, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Distribute the work and the sacrifice, and build rest into the rhythm, so a few people don't carry — and burn out under — the whole load. "If one or two people do all the sacrificing, they quickly burn out, while everyone else blames them for whatever goes wrong." Conversely, the wider the chance to contribute, the wider the share in success — "it is their victory."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Concentrating effort on a few isn't just unfair, it's structurally fragile and demotivating; widely shared responsibility both reduces individual load and increases everyone's stake. Ganz also warns against the pace trap: campaigns that "don't take time to reflect, adjust, and retrain end up burning through their human resources — treating them more like 'resources' than like 'humans.'" Rest and retraining between peaks is not a luxury.
- **Example in context:** Ganz's repeated contrast — "it may be more efficient to get five people to spend the whole day… but it may be more effective to get ten teams of five to spend one hour each in their own neighborhoods" — distributing both the work and the ownership.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Burning out" and "work's all on a few people" are the same failure. The fixes are structural (delegate outcomes, widen participation) and rhythmic (plan rest/retraining), not exhortations to the exhausted to try harder.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (spread the load); leader-self-care [A10.2] (the leader's own sustainability); launch-and-relaunch [A5.7] (rest/retrain between peaks).

### Celebrate: the heart work
concepts:    [A10.3 celebration-and-recognition]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  Motivation comes from how the work is designed
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.6, ch.7, ch.5

- **Claim / recommendation:** Celebrate deliberately — it's not a frill but one of the only three things groups actually do. Ganz's frame: organizations *meet* (the head work — deliberate, decide, learn), *act* (the hands work), and *celebrate* (the heart work — "sustaining commitment, honoring values, recognizing contributions, and affirming relationships"). Recognize contribution specifically and use celebration to restore harmony after hard debate.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Celebration does emotional work the other two activities don't: it renews commitment and rebuilds connection, which is why Ganz urges "launching with the heart" — a team name, a chant — and recognition in the moment (Molly publicly noting Ashley got the most commitments and asking how). Done collaboratively (a potluck, a talent show, members rather than hired entertainers), the celebration *itself* distributes responsibility and deepens belonging.
- **Example in context:** the end of Molly's phone-bank shift — "a big round of applause… Ashley, you got ten, three more than anyone else, how'd you do it?" — recognition that's also learning; and the team chant in a Beijing workshop that produced "laughter, loss of self-consciousness… and creativity."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Momentum and morale decay without marked wins and felt recognition. Ganz's contribution is to make celebration a *structural* practice (meet/act/celebrate) and to tie recognition to learning and to repair after conflict, not just to morale.
- **Related concepts:** cohesion-and-belonging [A6.3] (celebration builds the "we"); repair-and-mutual-accountability [A8.5] (celebrate to restore harmony after debate); intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (recognition as a felt result).

### Handling losing: narrating a setback
concepts:    [A10.5 resilience-through-setbacks]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  Practice over theory; understanding flows from action
source:      Ganz — *People, Power, Change*, ch.5, ch.7, ch.3

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat handling a loss as a core leadership skill, and interpret the setback in a way that keeps the group whole. "One of the most important leadership challenges is learning to handle losing. A campaign loss requires narrative interpretation: Does the setback contaminate the enterprise, or does it provide an opportunity for redemption?" Choose — and help the group choose — the redemptive reading.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Dan McAdams's *contaminative* vs. *redemptive* framings (also used in the narrative chapter for loss): a contaminative reading makes the loss predictive ("things always go bad"); a redemptive reading holds the loss as real and painful *and* a source of learning, growth, and resilience. Because a group can come out of a *failed* goal with more capacity, more power, and the ability to try again, the loss need not be fatal — the interpretation is a leadership act.
- **Example in context:** the 1988 California Democratic GOTV campaign — three hundred organizers, 14,000 precincts, a multitiered leadership structure — *lost* the state, yet "resulted in almost a refounding of the California Democratic Party": "a prime example of how even a losing campaign can be used to develop a more powerful organization." (Large campaign; the redemptive-reading principle scales down — a small group can lose a goal and still come out stronger.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A loss is where momentum and morale most easily collapse. Ganz gives the leader both a diagnosis (which interpretation is taking hold?) and a move (narrate the redemptive reading; measure the capacity and leadership gained, not only the goal lost) to hold the group together.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (the individual-scale version); public-narrative [A2.2] (the story that reframes the loss); celebration-and-recognition [A10.3] (mark what was built, even in a loss).
