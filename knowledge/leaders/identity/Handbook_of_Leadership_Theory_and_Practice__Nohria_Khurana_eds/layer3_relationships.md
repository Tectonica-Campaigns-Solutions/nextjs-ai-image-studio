# Handbook of Leadership Theory — Relationships, Flags & Coverage

## Layer 3 — Relationship Map

- **Conditions over causes ↔ Functional, shared leadership** — [A5.1] × [A9.5]: distributing leadership *is* a structural act — you build the conditions (purpose, composition, norms) that let others step up; it does not emerge from stepping back. (Entries: "Conditions over causes", "Functional, shared leadership — and why it won't emerge on its own")
- **From doer to enabler ↔ Delegate outcomes, not tasks** — [A1.2] × [A9.2]: the inner shift in what the leader values is enacted by the outer behaviour of handing over ownership and not retaking it. (Entries: "From doer to enabler", "Delegate outcomes, not tasks — and don't take it back")
- **Identity transition ↔ Made, not born** — [A1.4] × [A1.6]: believing leadership is learnable (incremental view) is what lets a first-timer endure the awkward identity-transition period instead of concluding they're unfit. (Entries: "Becoming a leader is an identity shift", "Made, not born — and believing it is half the battle")
- **Belonging ↔ Psychological safety** — [A2.4] × [A6.5]: people only contribute their best when they feel safe, and they only feel safe inside a community whose purpose and values they share — belonging and safety are mutually reinforcing. (Entries: "Create a world people want to belong to", "Psychological safety")
- **Shared fate ↔ Cooperative norms** — [A6.3] × [A6.1]: making shared fate salient is *how* a leader installs cooperation, especially in a diverse group. (Entries: "Shared fate turns difference into strength", "How set norms harden into culture")
- **Ownership and voice ↔ Diagnose, then flex** — [A3.2] × [A7.3]: a participative style is what generates ownership on uncertain work; a directive style on creative work suppresses the very initiative the leader wants. (Entries: "People commit to what they help create", "Diagnose, then flex")
- **Earned authority ↔ Lead by example** — [A1.1] × [A1.3]: with no positional power, a volunteer leader's authority rests on credibility, so a single visible inconsistency (hypocrisy) withdraws the consent the role depends on. (Entries: "Where a volunteer leader's power actually comes from", "Lead by example")
- **Bounded membership ↔ The meeting that decays** — [A5.6] × [A6.4]: an overlarge, role-composed group can't define what it's interdependent for, so it slides into information-sharing — the size problem causes the meeting problem. (Entries: "Bounded membership", "The common stall")
- **Psychological safety ↔ Productive dissent** — [A6.5] × [A8.2]: safety is the precondition for challenge; without it a group looks harmonious but is actually conformist ("everyone just agrees"). (Entries: "Psychological safety", "Support and confrontation")
- **The undiscussables ↔ The dominating member** — [A8.4] × [A8.2]: a dominating member persists largely because the authority dynamic around them is the thing no one will name; surfacing it is the lever. (Entries: "The undiscussables", "Support and confrontation")
- **Decisions / 70% solution ↔ Inner and outer circle** — [A7.2] × [A4.4]: timely deciding depends on consulting a trusted inner circle *and* a diverse outer one — the circles are how a leader gets to "70% then decide" without blind spots. (Entries: "Decisions as a learnable function", "The inner and outer circle")
- **Hierarchy just enough ↔ Founder bottleneck** — [A7.6] × [A9.3]: over-intervention (too much standing hierarchy) trains members to delegate problems back up, producing the bottleneck; situational hierarchy prevents it. (Entries: "Hierarchy, just enough", "Delegate outcomes, not tasks")
- **System viability ↔ Burnout** — [A10.6] × [A10.1]: because member fulfilment is part of the definition of effective leadership, running people into the ground is a leadership failure, not a side effect of success. (Entries: "System viability", "Burnout and the overloaded leader")
- **Create more leaders ↔ Coaching members** — [A9.1] × [A9.6]: the goal of developing leaders is realised through concrete coaching — timed to the group's phase and including publicly naming members' strengths. (Entries: "The most essential work is to create more leaders", "Coaching members")
- **Integrative problem-solving ↔ Keep conflict on the issue** — [A8.1] × [A8.1]: a third-way solution becomes possible only once members' real interests are named and discussable rather than disguised as pure big-picture argument. (Entries: "Integrative problem-solving", "Keep conflict on the issue")

## Excluded from this summary

**Chapters dropped (Gate 1 — leader, not organisation; or already in corpus):**
- Ch.1 (editors' introduction) — used as the Layer 1 map, not summarised as entries.
- Ch.2 *When Does Leadership Matter?* (Wasserman, Anand & Nohria) — variance-partitioning of CEO impact on firm performance; org/measurement altitude.
- Ch.5 *Leadership Through an OB Lens* (Glynn & DeJordy) — a "definitional quagmire" review of the academic field; no bearing on leading a small group.
- Ch.8 *Classical Sociological Approaches* (Guillén) — authority, charisma, and elites at the level of bureaucracy and society.
- Ch.9–10 *Economists' Perspectives* (Bolton et al.; Zupan) — principal-agent and prisoner's-dilemma models of the firm.
- Ch.11 *Leadership and History* (Friedman) — historiography of leadership; out of scope.
- Ch.13 *Leadership and Cultural Context* (GLOBE; Javidan et al.) — cross-national/global-organisation altitude.
- Ch.16 *What Is Leadership? The CEO's Role* (Porter & Nohria) — explicitly the CEO of a large, complex organisation → `H:org-level`.
- Ch.20 *Leadership in a Globalizing World* (Kanter) — global-firm leadership tasks → org.
- Ch.24 *Leadership Development Interventions* — organisational programme design → `H:org-level`.
- Ch.19 *Leading Change / Social Movements* (Ganz) — **not re-summarised**; Ganz's distributed leadership and public narrative are already in the corpus as *People, Power, Change*. Noted only as a reinforcing overlap.

**Chapters deferred (in-scope but not yet close-read — recommended next adds):**
- Ch.12 *Power and Leadership* (Nye) — hard/soft/"smart" power; flagged as the next add for **A7.1**, though it overlaps Moray's *How to Hold Power* (corpus), so it may yield only one or two distinct entries.
- Ch.14 *Women and Leadership* (Ely & Rhode) — the competence/likability "double bind"; the strongest distinct not-yet-covered material, mapping to **A1.4** (how a first-time leader from an under-expected group experiences the role) and **A9** (who gets seen as a leader). Recommended as the first deferred chapter to add.
- Ch.3 *Revisiting the Meaning of Leadership* (Podolny, Khurana & Hill-Popper) — meaning-making; substantially covered by Burns and Ganz in the corpus, so low marginal yield.
- Ch.7 *A Clinical Approach* (Kets de Vries & Engellau) — the unconscious/intrapsychic life of the leader; niche, partial overlap with the inner-game material already drawn from ch.6 and ch.22.
- Ch.23 *The Experience Conundrum* and Ch.26 *Adult Development* — both feed A1/A9 development, but their core idea (developmental readiness; growth through experience and life-stage) is already surfaced via ch.22 and ch.25; add only if A1 still reads thin after testing.

**Concepts cut within kept chapters (org/research altitude):** founder "blueprints" and org-culture-as-control, signalling *across hierarchical levels*, and the board "monitoring vs partnering" cases (Boeing/Lenovo) — all firm-level; the innovation-economy framing in ch.21 (BRICs, corporate universities, intranets/ValuesJam scale); and the science-of-development apparatus in ch.25 (RODI, micro-interventions, fMRI/simulation futures, the full transformational/transactional model — the last routed to Burns in the corpus).
