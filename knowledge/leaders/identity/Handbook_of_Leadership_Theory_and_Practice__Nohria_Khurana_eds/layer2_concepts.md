# Handbook of Leadership Theory — ## Layer 2 — Concept Entries


### —— A1 · Stepping into Leadership ——

### Becoming a leader is an identity shift, not just a skill upgrade
concepts: [A1.4]
situations: [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
anchors to: Made, not born
source: Ibarra, Snook & Guillén Ramo — *Handbook*, ch.22

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat the discomfort of a new leadership role as an identity transition to be worked through, not a sign you are unqualified. People become leaders by trying on "provisional selves" — tentative ways of acting the part — and keeping what proves effective and authentic while discarding what does not. Expect a "liminal" in-between stretch where the old self (the doer) and the new one (the enabler) coexist; that ambiguity is the process, not a failure of it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ibarra's research on role transitions shows that new roles require new skills *and* a revised self-concept; the two develop together. A formal title is not required — people take on leadership, and the attendant identity wobble, well before any promotion. Because the new behaviours mean spending less time on the work that made you good, the transition involves real "unlearning" and a felt loss of competence [A9.3].
- **Example in context:** The chapter follows managers and engineers moving into organisational change-agent roles: separated from their old functions, they felt disconnected and unsure of their standing, then gradually rebuilt an identity around the new work — some no longer wanting to return to their old jobs.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a first-time volunteer leader, this reframes impostor feelings as predictable transition states rather than evidence of unfitness. It also explains why early leading feels "fake": acting before the identity has caught up is normal, and the feeling fades as the self-concept settles [A4.1].
- **Related concepts:** from doer to enabler [A1.2] (the specific shift underway); failure as learning [A1.6] (relapse and awkwardness are expected); coaching members [A9.6] (guides and peers steady the transition).

### From doer to enabler
concepts: [A1.2]
situations: [S:doing-everything-myself]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Ibarra, Snook & Guillén Ramo — *Handbook*, ch.22 (citing Hill, *Becoming a Manager*); Chatman & Kennedy — ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** The central move of stepping up is shifting from valuing your own production to valuing making others more productive. New leaders must learn to "value making others more productive", not just to add output themselves. Do not keep doing the work you are good at simply because it is comfortable and rewarded; that is the most common way the transition stalls.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Chatman & Kennedy frame this as the leadership paradox: the achievement orientation that earns someone the role is precisely what they must set aside to perform it, because a serious role is too wide for a soloist [A9.3]. Hill's work shows the hurdle is as much about *values* as skills — people keep allocating time to tasks they were previously praised for.
- **Example in context:** Hill's new managers had to come to see planning for and coaching others as mission-critical rather than a distraction from "real work" — a change in what they counted as their job.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer leader who keeps absorbing every task starves the group of ownership and burns themselves out [A10.1]. Naming the shift as a values change, not a time-management tweak, is what makes it stick.
- **Related concepts:** delegating outcomes not tasks [A9.2] (the behaviour that enacts the shift); founder bottleneck [A9.3] (what doing-it-all produces); enabling-vs-doing is the A1 face of the functional view of leadership [A9.5].

### Made, not born — and believing it is half the battle
concepts: [A1.4] [A1.6]
situations: [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
anchors to: Made, not born
source: Avolio — *Handbook*, ch.25

- **Claim / recommendation:** Leadership is far more developed than inherited, and holding that belief is itself a precondition for growing. Adopt an "incremental" view (ability is elastic and trainable) rather than an "entity" view (you either have it or you don't), because people who believe leadership is fixed disengage from their own development.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Avolio's twin studies estimate leadership emergence at roughly 30% heritable and 70% environmental — lower heritability than intelligence or personality. Drawing on Dweck, he notes the entity/incremental belief is itself malleable: prime someone toward "I can grow at this" and development accelerates; leave them thinking it is fixed and it stalls. Bandura's efficacy — the felt probability that you can succeed at a task — is "statelike" and can be built, and it predicts performance.
- **Example in context:** Avolio recounts a workshop exercise in which teams, given a "morally challenged" character, designed elaborate controls — then admitted the *least* developed character was the one their organisations usually promoted, because raw individual performance, not leadership, was being rewarded.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a first-timer, "leaders are made" is not a pep-talk but an evidence-based stance that licenses practice and patience. It pairs with the identity view [A1.4]: growth is expected, relapse is normal, and confidence (efficacy) is something you can deliberately build.
- **Related concepts:** identity transition [A1.4] (what is being made); failure as learning [A1.6] (the incremental mindset in action); developing leaders [A9.1] (the same logic applied to others).

### Lead by example: people read your behaviour, and punish hypocrisy
concepts: [A1.3]
situations: [S:group-feels-transactional]
anchors to: Diagnose, then flex
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6 (with Avolio — ch.25)

- **Claim / recommendation:** Members infer what actually matters from mundane leader behaviours — what you put on the agenda, what you ask about, what you follow up on, what you celebrate — far more than from any stated values. So model the behaviour you want, and be aware that espousing values raises the bar you will be judged against.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Chatman & Kennedy describe the "hypocrisy-attribution dynamic" (Cha & Edmondson): naming values makes everyone's conduct — especially the leader's — newly salient, and the actor-observer bias makes people judge a leader's lapse harshly, swapping hard-won commitment for cynicism. Avolio's "pseudo-transformational" leader is the same failure: the appearance of inspiring leadership without the moral substance behind it [A4.3].
- **Example in context:** Avolio describes Bill George, who repeatedly lost his fraternity-presidency bids until a friend told him he would never win while running to win for *himself*; once he showed he cared about the group, he won — and went on to a celebrated CEO career built on consistency between word and act.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer group watches whether the leader does what they ask of others; a single visible inconsistency can poison trust faster than any speech can build it. The defence is consistency, not charisma.
- **Related concepts:** trust-building [A4.3] (what consistency underwrites); how norms become culture [A6.1] (your behaviour is the loudest norm); earned authority [A1.1] (legitimacy rests on credibility).

### Hubris and humility: see your own standing accurately
concepts: [A1.4]
anchors to: Made, not born
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Effective leaders hold a paradoxical mix of some confidence and substantial humility, and — crucially — they perceive their own status in the group accurately rather than overestimating it. If you are driven by achievement, watch for a deficit of humility; if driven by status, watch for a deficit of accurate self-perception.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Research the chapter cites finds that people who perceive their own status accurately — and who avoid overestimating it — are more influential, not less. The two common derailers are the high-achiever who cannot let others contribute and the high-power-motive person who misreads how much standing they actually have.
- **Example in context:** *No single in-context case given; the chapter states this as a synthesis of status-perception studies.*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer leader with no positional power depends entirely on accurate self-reading: over-claim authority you haven't earned and the group quietly withdraws consent [A1.1].
- **Related concepts:** earned authority [A1.1] (humility is how non-positional authority is kept); diagnose-then-flex [A7.3] (self-perception is part of diagnosis); the leadership paradox feeds enabling-vs-doing [A1.2].
- **Source reach:** The chapter is firm that accurate self-perception correlates with influence; it is thinner on *how* to calibrate it. Attribute the correlation confidently; treat the "how" as the coach's own practical addition.

### —— A2 · Purpose, Story & Shared Identity ——

### Create a world people want to belong to
concepts: [A2.4] [A2.1]
situations: [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:low-attendance]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21

- **Claim / recommendation:** The leader's primary task is to build a world people *want* to belong to — one that affirms each person's identity and talents *and* lets them contribute to something larger than themselves. The sharpest framing for a volunteer leader: treat every member as someone who shows up "show up by choice, not obligation", because that is literally true.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People only share their best ("their slice of genius") when they feel safe and part of a community whose purpose and values they embrace [A6.5]. Belonging plus shared purpose is what converts a collection of individuals into a group that holds together under stress; a purpose that speaks only to instrumental ends ("maximise returns") does not energise people for long.
- **Example in context:** At Pixar, Ed Catmull rejects hiring outside scripts in favour of a "community of artists"; leaders cannot mandate trust and respect, so they instead build an environment that nurtures them, which then makes the place a magnet for talent.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Volunteers can leave at any time; the only durable hold is meaning and belonging, not obligation. If attendance is fading, the question is usually whether people still feel they belong to something worth their evening.
- **Related concepts:** identification and shared fate [A6.3] (the mechanism of "we"); psychological safety [A6.5] (the precondition for contributing); purpose people help shape [A3.2] (belonging deepens when people co-author the why).

### Shared fate turns difference into strength
concepts: [A2.4] [A6.3]
situations: [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build a felt sense of "we" by making members' shared fate salient — the fact that they succeed or fail together. When group identity is strong, people cooperate more, commit more, and treat differences as resources rather than fault lines.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Social identity theory: when membership in a group becomes a salient part of the self, people see themselves as part of the in-group, cooperate with it, and align to its goals. Diverse groups normally cohere more slowly and produce less — but when a leader emphasises shared fate over individual difference, diverse teams can become *more* productive and creative than homogeneous ones (Chatman et al., 1998).
- **Example in context:** The chapter reports that emphasising employees' "shared fate" reversed the usual diversity penalty, with mixed teams outperforming homogeneous ones on creative work.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer group is often demographically mixed and loosely bound; deliberately naming the common stake is what stops it fragmenting into parallel individuals who happen to share a room.
- **Related concepts:** belonging [A2.4] (the felt result); cohesion [A6.3] (how groups bind); promoting cooperative norms [A6.1] (how shared fate is institutionalised).

### —— A3 · Motivation & Commitment ——

### People commit to what they help create
concepts: [A3.2]
situations: [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:group-feels-transactional]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21 (citing Block); with Lorsch — ch.15

- **Claim / recommendation:** Give members a real hand in shaping the work — the purpose, the decisions, the design — because people are accountable and committed to what they have helped create, and merely informed of. Freedom and voice are not soft perks; they are what generate willingness to be held responsible for the whole.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Block's premise (quoted by Hill) is that the people most involved usually hold the collective wisdom to solve the problem, and that participation converts compliance into ownership. Lorsch adds that involving followers in a decision does three things at once: the leader learns their views, the decision is jointly owned, and expectations get communicated — so participation is also coordination [A7.2].
- **Example in context:** Hill cites IBM's "ValuesJam," where ~50,000 employees co-wrote the company's values over three days; having helped define them, people were motivated to pursue them — a CEO could not simply have announced them.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" is often a design symptom: people withhold ownership of work they had no hand in shaping. The fix is upstream — bring them into the shaping — not more exhortation.
- **Related concepts:** decisions as shared process [A7.2] (the mechanism of co-creation); autonomy vs direction [A7.3] (give voice on the how); ownership feeds belonging [A2.4].

### The volunteer-commitment trade-off
concepts: [A3.4]
situations: [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff: H:recruitment
source: Lorsch — *Handbook*, ch.15

- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognise the real tension between reliability and commitment: leaning on a few dependable people is easier in the short run, but recruiting and trusting more volunteers is what builds membership commitment over time. Neither pole is "correct" — the choice is a deliberate trade-off the leader must make consciously.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** More volunteer involvement raises commitment because people invest in what they participate in [A3.2]; but it also raises coordination cost and unpredictability, which is why leaders are tempted to keep work in a trusted few [A5.4]. Lorsch frames this as one of the interrelated choices a leader makes when defining the group's mission and how it staffs it.
- **Example in context:** Lorsch describes a non-profit helping elders stay in their homes, whose board pushed for more volunteer involvement to build commitment, while the paid leader worried that volunteers were "unreliable and inexperienced" and would complicate her job.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Volunteers are flaky" often reflects a leader who, to protect reliability, never gave them real ownership — which is exactly what would have built their commitment. (Recruitment tactics and the ask itself → `H:recruitment`.)
- **Related concepts:** commitment over time [A3.4] (what's at stake); ownership and voice [A3.2] (the commitment mechanism); work and task design [A5.4] (the coordination cost).

### —— A4 · Relationships as Strategy ——

### The inner and outer circle
concepts: [A4.4]
situations: [S:cant-make-decisions]
source: Useem — *Handbook*, ch.18

- **Claim / recommendation:** Cultivate both a small inner circle of trusted, candid advisers and a wider, more diverse outer circle — and use both. The inner circle gives you fast, unbiased counsel; the outer circle keeps you from the blind spots that come from only ever consulting people like you.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Useem cites research that faster, better-deciding firms had managers who consulted a few experienced, *unbiased* advisers before deciding — advisers with no stake in the outcome. But over-relying on a tight inner circle alone, especially under uncertainty, produced worse decisions: the bank study found that the more deal-makers leaned only on close ties, the less they learned what they needed [A8.2].
- **Example in context:** Cisco's John Chambers credited his acquisition track record to two advisers — a former CEO and the CFO — who needed no briefing on strategy, gave unbiased advice, and notably had no interest in his job.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer leader who only ever checks decisions with their two closest allies will reliably miss things; deliberately widening the circle is a cheap guard against groupthink [A8.2].
- **Related concepts:** decisions as shared process [A7.2] (where the circles feed in); productive dissent [A8.2] (the diverse circle as a dissent source); the relationship web [A4.4] (mapping who you draw on).

### —— A5 · Group Design & Structure ——

### Conditions over causes: set the stage, then manage at the margin
concepts: [A5.1] [A5.3]
situations: [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Spend the larger share of your energy establishing the conditions that make good outcomes likely — clear purpose, the right people, sound norms — and the smaller share on real-time intervention. Trying to drive a group's behaviour moment to moment is both exhausting and less effective than getting it "stabilised on approach" early.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hackman argues social systems can't be reliably steered by cause-effect commands; what works is putting structural conditions in place and then letting the group chart its own course, intervening only as needed. He likens it to a pilot who stabilises the aircraft far out rather than wrestling it down at the last second — the prudent move, if you're fighting it at the threshold, is to go around and re-establish the approach [A5.7].
- **Example in context:** Temirkanov prepared his orchestra fully in rehearsal, then at the performance cued the start and lowered his hands, "managing at the margin" — the conditions did the work.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the coach's first diagnostic question: when a group stalls, look at its structure (purpose, composition, norms) before blaming effort or personalities. It is the primary statement of the structure-before-process lens that the corpus also reaches via Fisher.
- **Related concepts:** clear purpose [A5.3] and composition [A5.1] (the conditions that matter most); relaunch [A5.7] (re-establishing conditions when stalled); generative rules [A5.5] (light conditions, not heavy control).

### Underdesigned despite all the power
concepts: [A5.3] [A5.6]
situations: [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:cant-make-decisions]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** A core team that has the authority to set itself up well will often fail to use it — drifting into vague purpose, poor task design, and thin support. Treat your own group's design as a deliberate job, not something that takes care of itself because the members are capable.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman's studies of top-management *and* volunteer activist teams found both were "underdesigned, underled, and underresourced" despite having full authority to fix that — partly because such teams feel they should guard resources rather than claim them, and partly because articulating what a group does *together* (beyond the sum of individuals) is genuinely hard. A team's effectiveness is a joint function of effort, sound strategy, and talent applied — and poor design depresses all three.
- **Example in context:** In a grassroots environmental association, elected committees with full latitude to choose their strategic focus nonetheless showed *less* compelling shared purpose than far more constrained frontline teams.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the activist-team finding that makes the chapter land for a volunteer core team: capability does not substitute for design. A drifting committee usually has a design problem, not a motivation problem [A6.4].
- **Related concepts:** clear purpose as keystone [A5.3]; bounded membership [A5.6]; the stall that follows poor design [A6.4].

### Bounded membership: compose for the task, not the title
concepts: [A5.6] [A5.2]
situations: [S:group-too-big-or-chaotic] [S:cant-make-decisions]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** Keep the working team clearly bounded and sized to its task, and choose members for what they contribute to the shared work — not because their role or seniority "entitles" them to a seat. Resist the pull toward inclusiveness that quietly bloats the group until it can no longer do real work.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** In the study, of 120 top teams only 9% agreed on who was even *on* the team; teams composed by role rather than task became overlarge and porous, and an overlarge group cannot answer "what are we interdependent *for*?" — so it defaults to mere information-sharing [A6.4]. Leaders err toward inclusion because disinviting someone risks loss of face and, in volunteer settings, the fear of losing an active participant altogether.
- **Example in context:** A "five o'clock meeting" national-security team grew so large through repeat "guests" that the senior executive abandoned it for smaller informal groups to make real decisions.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer committee that lets everyone who is keen join the core team will find it cannot decide anything; the kindness of inclusion at the boundary becomes a tax on the whole group's effectiveness.
- **Related concepts:** group size and nesting [A5.2]; the info-sharing stall [A6.4]; decisions as shared process [A7.2] (which oversized groups cannot do well).
- **Source reach:** The chapter studies leadership teams specifically; the composition-and-boundary findings generalise well to a volunteer core team but were not tested on small task groups, so attribute the pattern, not a precise size rule (for sizing, lean on Fisher's 3–7 in the corpus).

### Generative rules, not a rule book
concepts: [A5.5] [A7.6]
situations: [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21

- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold a few clear, generative principles rather than trying to write a rule for every situation. A small set of "this is how we treat each other / these are the lines we don't cross" rules lets members generate the right behaviour in cases you never anticipated; an exhaustive rulebook makes them wait to be told.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill draws the agile-software distinction between *inclusive* rules (specify everything in advance) and *generative* rules (a minimum set that lets people improvise appropriately) — generative rules suit groups doing novel, uncertain work because no one can pre-script it [A7.3]. This is "structuring" (an ongoing, adaptive activity) rather than fixed "structure."
- **Example in context:** W. L. Gore runs an 8,000-person company on four principles — fairness, freedom, commitment, and "waterline" (make your own calls, as long as the downside can't sink the ship) — from which its practices emerge.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a volunteer group, a short charter of operating principles travels further than a long procedures document, and it stops decisions being relitigated because the principles, not a missing rule, settle most cases. (Communication tooling and channels → `H:comms`.)
- **Related concepts:** norms and operating agreements [A5.5]; hierarchy just enough [A7.6]; how norms become culture [A6.1].

### Clear purpose is the keystone — and constraints sharpen it
concepts: [A5.3]
situations: [S:unclear-what-were-doing]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17 (with Hill et al. — ch.21)
- **Claim / recommendation:** Define a circumscribed, compelling shared purpose for the group, and re-state it as conditions change — it is the single condition most tied to whether a team functions. Don't fear hard constraints (deadlines, limited resources); used well, they focus a group rather than crushing it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman found that teams whose leaders explicitly defined the team's overall purpose made better decisions and ran better processes than those whose leaders were laissez-faire about it; an amorphous "provide leadership for the organisation" purpose leaves members unable to orient or engage. Hill adds that leaders often deliberately introduce a "hard stop" — a deadline or budget reality — to force evaluation and reframing.
- **Example in context:** Hill quotes a leader: the creative process "will go on forever unless there is a hard stop," and constraints "force the team to find ways to get around them."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "We're not sure what we're doing" is the most diagnosable stall there is; the leader's job is conceptual work — articulating the purpose — that comes best from one person, then is owned by the group.
- **Related concepts:** underdesign [A5.3/A5.6] (purpose is the first casualty); decisions [A7.2] (a clear purpose makes decisions decidable); shared purpose [A2.1].

### Launch and relaunch: there is no recipe
concepts: [A5.7]
situations: [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff: H:events
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat establishing a group's conditions as an art you repeat, not a one-time setup. The specific conditions a given group needs are idiosyncratic and shift over time, and membership turns over — so expect to re-launch (re-purpose, re-compose, re-norm) periodically rather than assuming the original setup holds.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The authors call this "the art of structuring": there is no formula, because the right purpose, norms, and roles depend on the group's history, relationships, and the moment's challenge. Volunteer teams in particular face "chronic patterns of team dissolution and relaunch" — election cycles and turnover reset the team repeatedly, each time requiring a fresh structuring.
- **Example in context:** In the activist association, committee membership changed predictably with the cycle of elections, so each new committee had in effect to be structured all over again.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer group that loses momentum after launch usually needs a deliberate *relaunch* — re-establishing the conditions — not a motivational push. (The mechanics of running that launch session → `H:events`.)
- **Related concepts:** conditions over causes [A5.1/A5.3] (what you re-establish); onboarding newcomers [A9.7] (turnover triggers relaunch); the stall [A6.4].

### —— A6 · Group Dynamics & Lifecycle ——

### Psychological safety: the precondition for people sharing their best
concepts: [A6.5]
situations: [S:everyone-just-agrees] [S:one-person-dominates]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21 (citing Edmondson); with Ibarra et al. — ch.22

- **Claim / recommendation:** Deliberately build the shared sense that it is safe to speak, question, and be wrong, because people only take the risk of contributing their real ideas when they feel safe and part of a like-minded community. Safety is not the absence of challenge; it is the security that lets challenge happen.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill draws on Edmondson: the vulnerability of putting forward a half-formed idea or a dissenting view is only bearable inside a trusting group, so safety is what unlocks the "slices of genius." Ibarra et al. add that learning a new role likewise depends on a protected space to practise and make mistakes — a "holding environment" with relief from constant judgement.
- **Example in context:** Hill's innovative teams treat missteps as information: one leader notes that if someone "makes a really big mistake, we assume they are working on a really tough problem."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When "everyone just agrees," the group usually lacks safety, not opinions; and a dominating member destroys safety for everyone else [A8.4]. Building safety is upstream of both dissent and learning.
- **Related concepts:** productive dissent [A8.2] (what safety enables); failure as learning [A1.6] (mistakes as information); belonging [A2.4] (safety rests on feeling part of the group).

### How set norms harden into culture — and how to set cooperative ones
concepts: [A6.1] [A5.5]
situations: [S:group-feels-transactional]
anchors to: Conditions over causes
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Setting and monitoring group norms is a core leadership task, and a few norms — above all, cooperation — repay deliberate attention. Don't assume cooperation will emerge on its own; install it, especially when the group is diverse.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Norms are shared standards against which behaviour is judged; once set, they become "how we do things." Cooperation faces real headwinds — self-interest, reward systems, and the fact that diverse groups develop cooperative norms less readily. Leaders instil cooperation by rewarding and celebrating *team* success rather than individual stars, by sharing credit, and by lengthening the group's time horizon so reciprocity pays [A10.3].
- **Example in context:** The chapter notes that rewarding collective rather than individual achievement, and framing success in terms of "we," makes cooperating more attractive and defection less so.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that "feels transactional" often has individualistic norms the leader never noticed setting; the lever is what gets celebrated and rewarded, which the group reads as the real rule. (Communication norms and tooling → `H:comms`.)
- **Related concepts:** generative rules [A5.5] (the form norms should take); cohesion [A6.3] (cooperation builds it); recognition [A10.3] (celebrating the team as a norm-setter).

### The common stall: the meeting that decays into updates
concepts: [A6.4]
situations: [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:burning-out]
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** Watch for the most common failure mode of a core team: meetings that drift into round-the-table updates and trivial decisions while real, interdependent work goes undone. Counter it by curating, each time the group meets, a short list of genuinely shared, consequential tasks.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman found leadership teams waste time three ways — trivial matters, unresolved conflict, and agreeing to disagree then disaggregating the work into solo efforts. The result is "many things done poorly versus a few things done superbly," with power dissipated and members both overloaded *and* bored. The fix is the leader actively crafting an agenda of meaningful interdependent work, repeatedly, not once.
- **Example in context:** Activist committees that defaulted to information-sharing dissipated their power across many thin activities; ones that focused on a few shared decisions mobilised the whole group.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "We lost momentum" and "I'm burning out in meetings" are often the same problem — the meeting stopped being about shared work. Curate the agenda toward interdependence and the energy returns.
- **Related concepts:** decisions as shared process [A7.2]; bounded membership [A5.6] (oversize causes the decay); burnout [A10.1].

### Learning together: coaching timed to the group's phase
concepts: [A6.6] [A9.6]
situations: [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6 (citing [Hackman & Wageman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf))
- **Claim / recommendation:** Coach the group on task-relevant matters, and time the *kind* of coaching to where the group is in its cycle — motivational coaching at the start, strategic coaching at the midpoint, and reflection toward the end. Generic encouragement at the wrong moment wastes the intervention.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hackman & Wageman's team-coaching theory holds that coaching only produces performance gains when it focuses on the task (not personalities) and lands at the predictable phase when the group is ready for it — energy at launch, strategy when the work is underway. Learning is the engine of a group becoming *more capable over time*, the second test of viability.
- **Example in context:** *The chapter states the timing rule in the abstract (motivational at the beginning, strategic at the midpoint) rather than via a single case.*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer leader's "pep talk" lands at launch but falls flat mid-project, when the group needs help with strategy; matching coaching to phase is what makes it useful.
- **Related concepts:** coaching members [A9.6]; stages and development [A6.2]; becoming more capable feeds system viability [A10.6].

### —— A7 · Power, Decision-Making & Autonomy ——

### Diagnose, then flex: directive or participative by the task
concepts: [A7.3]
situations: [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
anchors to: Diagnose, then flex
source: Lorsch — *Handbook*, ch.15

- **Claim / recommendation:** Match your style to the certainty of the work. When the task is routine and well-understood, a directive approach (tell people what and how) works fine; when the task is novel and uncertain, a participative approach is needed — and people doing creative, self-directed work expect a real say.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Lorsch revives contingency theory: certain tasks let a leader rely on simple direction, while uncertain tasks require motivating people to solve problems together, which reduces their anxiety and draws on their ideas. Educated, autonomous people will not engage uncertain work well without power-sharing — without it, leader and follower expectations don't align. This is the situational half of *diagnose, then flex* [A1.4].
- **Example in context:** Lorsch contrasts the leader of an assembly line (certain task, directive) with the leader of consultants or research scientists (uncertain task, participative) — the appropriate style follows the work, not the leader's preference.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Most volunteer work is uncertain and creative, so a directive default ("here's what to do") suppresses the initiative the leader wants; "no one takes initiative" can be a sign the style is too directive for the task [A3.2].
- **Related concepts:** autonomy vs direction [A7.3]; ownership and voice [A3.2] (participation's payoff); diagnostic ability [A1.4].

### Where a volunteer leader's power actually comes from
concepts: [A7.1] [A1.1]
situations: [S:scared-to-ask-people]
source: Lorsch — *Handbook*, ch.15 (Weber's three sources)

- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand that a volunteer leader has almost none of the positional power (reward, promotion, dismissal) that bosses rely on — so you must lead through *influence*: perceived competence and personal/relational pull, grounded in shared goals and values. Plan to earn followership, not to command it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Lorsch separates Weber's three sources: positional power (a property of the office) versus charisma and competence (properties of the person, which he calls influence). People follow competent leaders because they "seem to know what they are doing," and they follow leaders whose goals and values they share; leaders also reduce followers' anxiety. Strip out positional power — as volunteering does — and competence, relationship, and value-congruence are all that remain [A2.3].
- **Example in context:** Lorsch notes professional-service firms, where leaders are chosen by peers and cannot succeed without demonstrated competence — the closest analogue to a volunteer leader who holds authority only by members' consent.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Being "scared to ask people" eases once you see that your authority is consent-based and relational; the ask works through legitimacy and shared purpose, not leverage. (Recruitment asks and scripts → `H:recruitment`.)
- **Related concepts:** earned authority and consent [A1.1]; building and using power [A7.1]; values as glue [A2.3].

### Decisions as a learnable function: the 70 percent solution
concepts: [A7.2]
situations: [S:cant-make-decisions]
source: Useem — *Handbook*, ch.18

- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat making good, timely decisions as a core leadership skill you can improve — not a gift you either have or lack. Guard against both extremes: overconfidence on unfamiliar ground, and "decidophobia," the endless gathering of data to reach impossible certainty.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Useem argues decision errors are predictable (overoptimism, escalating commitment to past choices, assigning zero probability to the unlikely) — and predictable errors are preventable through training and experience. The remedies he names: prepare (under-preparation plus stress degrades decisions); build a cohesive team that raises the "panic point" under stress [A6.3]; consult an inner *and* outer circle [A4.4]; and apply the U.S. Marine Corps "70% solution" — with ~70% of the information, analysis, and confidence, decide.
- **Example in context:** The Marines instruct officers to "analyse, but do not overanalyse," on the logic that "The worst decision is no decision at all" — a flawed action, swiftly taken, at least lets you learn whether you were right.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader stuck in analysis is the common case; "70% then decide" is a concrete, repeatable rule that breaks the freeze without licensing recklessness.
- **Related concepts:** integrative problem-solving [A8.1] (a better third option, not just faster choice); inner/outer circle [A4.4]; cohesion buffers decision stress [A6.3].

### Hierarchy, just enough: first among equals
concepts: [A7.6]
situations: [S:i-cant-let-go]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21

- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold only as much hierarchy as coordination genuinely requires. Encourage bottom-up initiative as the default and reserve top-down intervention for when it is truly needed; conceive of yourself as "first among equals" who accepts final accountability but lets influence flow to whoever can contribute.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill's innovative leaders keep hierarchy "on an as-needed basis," letting "natural hierarchies" form around contribution rather than title; they intervene sparingly — to procure resources, to reframe a conflict heading toward dysfunction, or to bring in a fresh perspective. The discipline is knowing when to step in and when to let the group keep grappling, because people delegate up (hand the problem back) the moment they feel stuck [A9.3].
- **Example in context:** In these teams, status and influence are "determined more by contribution than by title"; some even elect their own leaders, with the formal leader serving as integrator rather than commander.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "I can't let go" often expresses too much standing hierarchy; the cure is to make hierarchy situational — present when coordination needs it, absent otherwise — so members carry real responsibility.
- **Related concepts:** functional shared leadership [A9.5]; founder bottleneck [A9.3] (over-intervention's result); autonomy vs direction [A7.3].

### Integrative problem-solving: the third way
concepts: [A8.1] [A7.2]
situations: [S:two-members-clashing] [S:cant-make-decisions]
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21 (citing Follett)

- **Claim / recommendation:** When two positions clash, resist the reflex to pick a winner or split the difference; look for an integrative third option that honours the real demands of both sides. Treat conflict as a source of value to be mined, not a malfunction to be suppressed.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill draws on Mary Parker Follett's three ways to resolve conflict — domination, compromise, and integration — arguing that only integration produces genuinely novel solutions, because it reframes the problem rather than dividing the spoils. Roger Martin's "opposable mind" names the underlying capacity: holding two opposing models at once and finding in the tension a superior third, instead of collapsing prematurely to one.
- **Example in context:** Follett held that conflict can be "a normal process by which socially valuable differences register themselves" — difference as raw material for a better answer, provided the group seeks integration over domination.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Two clashing members usually present an either/or; the leader's most valuable move is often to reframe the question so a third answer becomes visible — which also models that disagreement is welcome [A8.2].
- **Related concepts:** task vs relationship conflict [A8.1]; productive dissent [A8.2]; decisions as shared process [A7.2].

### Is "leader vs. manager" a false dichotomy?
concepts: [A1.2] [A7.3]
source: Lorsch — *Handbook*, ch.15 (with Hill et al. — ch.21; Avolio — ch.25)

- **Claim / recommendation:** Be wary of the popular split that glorifies "leaders" (who drive change and inspire) over "managers" (who keep things running). Lorsch argues this is a "false dichotomy": most effective leaders are also good managers, and treating the two as opposed flatters the leader while obscuring real work. Hold the distinction loosely — as two modes most leaders move between — rather than as two kinds of person.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Lorsch's objection is that the leader/manager split (from Zaleznik, Kotter, and others) makes change the only "real" leadership and demotes the everyday work of sustaining a group, when in practice the same person does both. He instead defines a leader simply as someone who influences others to follow — leaving "directive vs participative" as a style choice within that, governed by the task [A7.3].
- **Example in context:** *No single case; Lorsch argues the point conceptually, using the range from an army lieutenant to a CEO to a law-firm partner to show one model can't fit all.*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For the coach's "leadership vs management" remit, this is the honest framing: the work of holding a volunteer group together (coordination, follow-through, care) is leadership too, not a lesser "management" task — so a first-timer needn't feel they're failing to "lead" when much of the job is steady tending.
- **Related concepts:** enabling vs doing [A1.2]; diagnose-then-flex [A7.3]; the functional view that dissolves the split [A9.5].
- **Source reach:** This is contested. Lorsch dissents from a widely held distinction; Kotter and Bennis (cited by Hill, ch.21) hold the opposite — that organisations are often "overmanaged and underled," and the distinction is real and useful. Present both poles; the coach should name the disagreement rather than declare a winner. (Burns's transformational/transactional framing, in the corpus, is the deeper treatment of the change-leadership pole.)

### —— A8 · Conflict, Dissent & Accountability ——

### Support and confrontation: guarding against agreeable groups
concepts: [A8.2] [A6.5]
situations: [S:everyone-just-agrees]
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21

- **Claim / recommendation:** Build enough mutual support that people will challenge each other, then make sure they actually do. Too much harmony tips into groupthink, where members stop critiquing and suppress doubts; so cultivate the skill of "being comfortable making others uncomfortable," with critique aimed at ideas, never people.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill frames support and confrontation as a paradox to keep in balance: support is what makes confrontation bearable, but unbroken support breeds the conformity that kills adaptation. When pent-up disagreement finally erupts it tends to polarise — people want to win rather than solve — so the discipline is steady, low-stakes dissent rather than rare blow-ups. The norm "I'd rather be successful than right" keeps critique about the work.
- **Example in context:** Hill's teams keep critique on "the quality of the ideas, not the person who generated them," precisely so that frequent challenge doesn't curdle into personal conflict.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Everyone just agrees" is a warning sign, not comfort: it usually means safety is high but challenge is absent. The leader's job is to make dissent normal and ideas-focused before suppressed disagreement erupts as personal conflict [A8.1].
- **Related concepts:** psychological safety [A6.5] (what makes confrontation possible); task vs relationship conflict [A8.1]; productive dissent [A8.2].

### The undiscussables: authority dynamics no one will name
concepts: [A8.4] [A8.2]
situations: [S:one-person-dominates] [S:conflict-got-personal]
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** Notice that the most damaging group dynamics are often the ones members won't put on the table — deference to "the boss," resentment of a dominating member, simmering conflict everyone avoids. Part of the leader's job is to make the undiscussable discussable, because a group can't fix what it won't look at.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman found leadership teams ruled by authority dynamics members wouldn't discuss: people treated the group as "the leader's responsibility" and stayed passive, or fell into Bion's unconscious patterns (depend on the leader to fix everything, fight/flee, or hope a deputed pair will rescue them). The norms of such groups actively discourage raising exactly the issues that matter most — so problems persist not because they're invisible but because they're unspeakable.
- **Example in context:** Asked why he stayed silent while a committee chair dominated and harmed the team, one activist member just laughed and said, in effect, why start a fight — a vivid case of an undiscussable left to fester.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When one person dominates or a conflict has gone personal, the leader who names the dynamic plainly (and safely) does more than any amount of working around it. Avoidance is the default; surfacing is the skill.
- **Related concepts:** the dominating member [A8.4]; psychological safety [A6.5] (what allows surfacing); repair and mutual accountability [A8.5].

### Keep conflict on the issue: let people own their interests
concepts: [A8.1]
situations: [S:two-members-clashing] [S:conflict-got-personal]
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17 (citing Berg)
- **Claim / recommendation:** When members represent different constituencies or commitments, make those interests explicit and discussable rather than expecting everyone to argue only "for the good of the whole." Pretending interests don't exist backfires; naming them keeps disagreement about the issue instead of curdling into suspicion about motives.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman cite Berg's counter-intuitive advice to "let people have their groups": a norm of speaking only big-picture makes others assume any position is secretly self-serving, so trust erodes. Making one's stake explicit converts a hidden agenda into a shared topic — and the best teams in the study both put individual concerns on the table and showed high empathy for each other's.
- **Example in context:** Activist teams that built explicit norms acknowledging members' particular concerns were better able to mobilise the whole group and less likely to fracture into competing efforts.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** In a volunteer group where people care about different things, suppressing those differences breeds personal conflict; surfacing them as legitimate keeps the fight on the merits and makes integrative solutions possible [A8.1].
- **Related concepts:** integrative problem-solving [A7.2]; the undiscussables [A8.4]; repair and mutual accountability [A8.5].

### —— A9 · Developing Others & Distributing Leadership ——

### Functional, shared leadership — and why it won't emerge on its own
concepts: [A9.5]
situations: [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17 (with Hackman — ch.4)
- **Claim / recommendation:** Think of leadership as a set of functions to be filled by whoever can — the leader's job is to "do, or get done, whatever is not being adequately handled" for the group (McGrath). But don't expect shared leadership to arise spontaneously; in a group of capable people it is *least* likely to emerge on its own and must be deliberately set up early.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The functional view (Barnard, McGrath, Hackman) holds that anyone who fulfils a needed function is leading, freeing the leader from being the sole source. Yet Wageman & Hackman's "fifth irony" is that the very conditions that distribute leadership — clear purpose, good composition, working norms, coaching — are best *initiated* by one authority, especially at the start; capable people default to their individual roles rather than to shared responsibility unless someone structures for it.
- **Example in context:** Across the activist and corporate teams studied, shared leadership "took" only where a single leader first established the conditions and modelled the norms, then handed responsibility on.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This resolves a common volunteer-leader confusion: "distributing leadership" is not stepping back and hoping — it is actively building the structure that lets others step up [A5.3]. Founder-as-sole-doer and "work all on a few people" are the symptoms of skipping that setup.
- **Related concepts:** delegating outcomes [A9.2]; clear purpose and design [A5.3]; the functional view anchors enabling-vs-doing [A1.2].

### The most essential work is to create more leaders
concepts: [A9.1]
situations: [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Avolio — *Handbook*, ch.25 (citing Follett); with Ibarra et al. — ch.22

- **Claim / recommendation:** Make developing other leaders an explicit goal of the role, not a nice-to-have. In Follett's formulation, "The most essential work of the leader is to create more leaders" — leadership measured by the capacity to increase others' sense of power, not to accumulate your own.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Avolio identifies "developing followers into leaders" as the signature of genuinely transformational (as opposed to self-serving) leadership, and links it to moral perspective-taking: leaders stuck in "what's in it for me?" can't invest in growing others, because doing so makes them more dispensable [A9.4]. Ibarra et al. add that development happens through real experience plus guides who believe in a person's still-forming "dream" and peers who validate the new identity [A4.3].
- **Example in context:** Avolio's prison-shop supervisor "Sam" treated each inmate as a unique individual with a six-week path to a real skill — and posted the lowest recidivism and highest productivity in the facility, an authentic developer of people at the front line.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" is often a development gap: the leader has been performing leadership rather than growing it in others. Reframing the job as "create more leaders" changes where the leader spends their attention.
- **Related concepts:** developing others through guides and reference groups [A9.6]; succession and ceding control [A9.4]; ownership and voice [A3.2].

### Coaching members: time it, and publicise their strengths
concepts: [A9.6] [A9.7]
situations: [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
source: Chatman & Kennedy — *Handbook*, ch.6

- **Claim / recommendation:** Coach individuals on task-relevant matters, and actively name a member's strengths to the rest of the group — especially a newcomer's, and especially when they belong to a group others might underestimate. Don't leave it to the member to prove themselves against a low expectation.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Chatman & Kennedy show that coworkers expect members of historically underrepresented groups to perform poorly regardless of actual skill, and the expectation is self-fulfilling. When the *leader* explicitly articulates a member's task-relevant capabilities — "sponsorship" — it lifts not just that person's performance but the whole group's, because others are relieved of the fear of carrying a weak link.
- **Example in context:** The chapter reports that leader sponsorship of a new member's capabilities had a strong positive effect on the member *and* on overall group performance (Chatman et al., 2008).
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Newcomers don't stick" often traces to a chilly first reception in which they had to earn standing alone; a leader who vouches for a newcomer's strengths up front changes their odds of staying [A6.3].
- **Related concepts:** onboarding newcomers [A9.7]; coaching timed to phases [A6.6]; identification and belonging [A6.3].

### Delegate outcomes, not tasks — and don't take it back
concepts: [A9.2] [A9.3]
situations: [S:i-cant-let-go] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17 (with Ibarra et al. — ch.22)
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand over ownership of a *result*, with latitude on how to achieve it — and then resist the strong pull to retake control the moment it looks shaky. Specifying the details of execution, or snatching the work back at the first wobble, teaches members they are not really responsible.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman found leaders who articulated a purpose but then "specified the details of execution" reinforced members' sense that their job was only to do as told; and leaders who delegated but then lost patience and reclaimed the work taught the group not to feel collectively responsible. Ibarra et al. add the personal cost: letting go means the leader's own "unlearning" — giving up the doer identity that delegating threatens [A1.2].
- **Example in context:** An activist committee chair turned over a fundraising event to her team; when she found a week later that no venue was booked, she made her own plan and issued directives — and so unintentionally taught the team they needn't feel responsible for it.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the precise mechanism behind "I can't let go" and "work is all on a few people": delegating tasks but not outcomes, or taking outcomes back under stress, keeps everything routed through the leader [A9.5].
- **Related concepts:** founder bottleneck [A9.3]; enabling vs doing [A1.2]; functional shared leadership [A9.5].

### Leading from behind
concepts: [A9.5] [A1.2]
situations: [S:doing-everything-myself]
anchors to: Functional, distributed, and ultimately dispensable
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21

- **Claim / recommendation:** Conceive of your role as creating the conditions in which others can come forward, rather than always being out front setting direction and being followed. Leading from behind is not abdication — like a shepherd, you still keep the flock together and nudge it from danger — but the nimble are allowed to lead the way.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill uses Mandela's shepherd image: the leader stays behind, letting the most able go ahead, so the group is directed without realising it is being directed. This fits the functional, fluid view of leadership — different people lead at different moments depending on their strengths — and depends on the leader's generosity with power and credit, which itself builds the safety others need to step up [A6.5].
- **Example in context:** Hill quotes a team member on a generous leader: it's as though "she enjoys getting the credit just so she can give it away" — generosity that frees others to contribute and take ownership [A10.3].
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a leader doing everything themselves, "leading from behind" is the concrete alternative stance: shape the conditions, share the credit, and let members lead in turn — without mistaking it for stepping away entirely.
- **Related concepts:** functional shared leadership [A9.5]; enabling vs doing [A1.2]; recognition and sharing credit [A10.3].

### —— A10 · Sustaining People & Yourself ——

### System viability: satisfaction and growth are half of effectiveness
concepts: [A10.6] [A6.2]
situations: [S:burning-out]
anchors to: System viability is the measure
source: [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Judge the group's health on three things, not just output: whether those it serves are satisfied, whether the group is becoming *more capable* over time, and whether members get at least as much learning and fulfilment as frustration from the work. Member wellbeing and growth are not soft extras — they are part of the definition of effective leadership.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hackman's "system viability" reframes success away from one good result toward the conditions that let a group keep performing: a burnt-out, stagnant, or resentful group is failing on viability even if it hit this month's goal. Because viability includes members' fulfilment, the leader who runs people into the ground is, by this measure, leading badly regardless of short-term output.
- **Example in context:** *Stated as a general criterion rather than via one case (see the Layer 0 note); Hackman contrasts it with the reflex of crowning whoever was present when good numbers appeared.*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This gives a volunteer leader permission — and a reason — to treat sustainability and member fulfilment as core success, not as competing with "getting things done." Burnout is a viability failure, full stop.
- **Related concepts:** burnout [A10.1]; learning together [A6.6]; satisfaction and retention [A10.6].

### Burnout and the overloaded leader (and team)
concepts: [A10.1] [A10.2]
situations: [S:burning-out] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
source: Wageman & [Hackman](https://26225792.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/26225792/Hackman%20%26%20Wageman%20(2005).%20A%20Theory%20of%20Team%20Coaching.pdf) — *Handbook*, ch.17
- **Claim / recommendation:** Notice that the people carrying leadership in a volunteer group are often at real risk of burnout — and that the cure is genuine sharing of the load, not just adding meetings. Sharing leadership functions among peers reduces the cognitive, emotional, and effort strain on any one person; meetings that don't share real work add to it.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Wageman & Hackman observed members of both corporate and activist leadership teams "at serious risk of burnout," and noted that the whole point of a leadership team — spreading the burden — is defeated when the team wastes time and members leave more drained, not less [A6.4]. Research they cite (Andrews et al.) found civic associations whose leaders worked as interdependent teams sustained their energy better and engaged others more.
- **Example in context:** *The chapter reports burnout risk and the energy benefit of interdependent teams from its study data rather than a single named case.*
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "I'm burning out" and "the work is all on a few people" are the same problem; the answer is structural — real shared ownership [A9.2] and meetings about shared work [A6.4] — not the leader simply trying harder.
- **Related concepts:** delegate outcomes [A9.2]; the meeting that decays [A6.4]; leader self-care [A10.2].

### Recognition: share the credit
concepts: [A10.3]
source: Hill, Travaglini, Brandeau & Stecker — *Handbook*, ch.21 (with Chatman & Kennedy — ch.6)

- **Claim / recommendation:** Mark contribution generously and publicly, and route credit toward members and the team rather than yourself. Sharing credit is not just gracious — it builds the psychological safety and commitment that make people willing to contribute again [A6.5].
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Hill found that the most effective leaders are "secure enough to share credit," and that this generosity enhances members' commitment to the collective. Chatman & Kennedy add the structural form: celebrating *team* success rather than individual stars, and explicitly sharing credit for outcomes, is a way to set and reinforce cooperative norms [A6.1].
- **Example in context:** A team member described a generous leader as someone who "enjoys getting the credit just so she can give it away" — credit treated as something to redistribute.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** In a volunteer group with no pay or promotion to offer, recognition is one of the few real currencies; a leader who hoards credit quietly drains the motivation they depend on.
- **Related concepts:** psychological safety [A6.5]; cooperative norms [A6.1]; belonging [A2.4].
