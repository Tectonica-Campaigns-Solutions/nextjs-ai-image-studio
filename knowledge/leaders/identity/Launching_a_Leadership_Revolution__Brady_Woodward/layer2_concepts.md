# Launching a Leadership Revolution — ## Layer 2 — Concept Entries


### Leadership is influence, earned — not positional
concepts:    [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent] [A7.1 building-and-using-power]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is influence, earned not positional
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.1; ch.8
- **Claim / recommendation:** Stop equating leadership with a title or an appointment. The book adopts Maxwell's definition — leadership is influence — and insists influence "cannot be awarded, appointed, or assigned… it must be earned." For a volunteer group leader this means your delegated role gets you the chair, but not the followership; that you build, person by person, by who you are and what you do.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Followers, the book argues, "buy in to the leader before anything else" — before the vision, the rewards, or the plan — asking subconsciously whether the leader can be trusted, cares, and knows what they're doing. People have become "astute customers of leadership" who spot a phony, so positional authority unbacked by trust and competence simply fails to move them. Influence is therefore earned *continually*, not banked once [A4.3].
- **Example in context:** Out of government for six years and, in the book's words, "powerless" in terms of office, Churchill nonetheless worked to "inform, persuade, and convince his countrymen" of the Nazi danger — influence with no position. The contrast case is the titled-but-uninfluential figure the book repeatedly warns against.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the load-bearing reframe for a first-time leader who feels they have no authority: the book's whole point is that you don't need positional authority to lead, and that even those who *have* it must still earn followership. Evidence is illustrative (Churchill, Maxwell's framework) rather than empirical.
- **Related concepts:** lead-by-example [A1.3] (how the influence is actually transmitted); trust-building [A4.3] (the substrate of buy-in); building-and-using-power [A7.1] (influence as the leader's power source).

### Lead by example — model the way
concepts:    [A1.3 lead-by-example]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is influence, earned not positional
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.3; ch.8
- **Claim / recommendation:** Set the example first; it is, in the book's framing, "not the main thing in influencing other people; it's the only thing." A leader's daily actions — not their words or position — are what the group copies. The practical test the book offers: would you want a whole group of people who behave exactly like you?
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The book claims members tend to become "carbon copies" of the leader over time, so "the simplest way to get high-level performance is to be sure the master-copy is worth duplicating." Because everything a leader does is amplified through the people watching, modeling is the highest-leverage behavior available, and no technique compensates for a poor example. This is why character is treated as a precondition, not a bonus [A1.1].
- **Example in context:** At Valley Forge, Washington refused the farmhouse offered him until his men were housed, ate the same "fire cake" rations, and stayed on the scene under personal attack rather than leave to defend his reputation — sharing the hardship he asked of others. His army re-formed around that example.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a volunteer leader with no carrots or sticks, example is often the *only* lever — you can't pay or fire anyone, so what you model is what propagates. Evidence is anecdotal (Washington, "managers push, leaders pull"), not measured.
- **Related concepts:** leading-by-earned-authority [A1.1] (example is how earned authority is shown); enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (model effort without doing others' jobs); collective-care [A10.4] (sharing hardship as modeled care).

### Teachability and the roadblocks to learning (hone-able)
concepts:    [A1.6 failure-as-learning]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  The Three Hs
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2; ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Stay teachable — "hone-able," in the book's term — treating learning as never finished and remaining humble toward anyone with something to teach, including peers and the people you lead. The book names specific roadblocks to watch for in yourself: arrogance, disinterest, wrong assumptions, entrenched habits, "not-invented-here" pride, wrong priorities, and cynicism.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The argument is epistemic humility as a discipline: "you don't know what you don't know," and you forget what you do know, so continual learning is the only way to keep pace with growing responsibility. Arrogance produces ignorance; the "not-invented-here" reflex sacrifices good ideas on the altar of pride; cynicism is skepticism let run to contempt, which closes the learner's mind. Humility is the through-line that keeps all of these in check.
- **Example in context:** The book praises Ray Kroc's enthusiasm for *other people's* ideas: when a franchisee invented something like the Big Mac, Kroc tested it, learned from it, and rolled it out company-wide — so a vast company ran on the ideas of thousands of individuals.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader is most tempted by "that's just how we've always done it" and by defensiveness toward feedback; the book frames teachability as the thing that keeps a leader improving instead of crashing. The roadblocks are a usable self-checklist. Support is illustrative, not empirical.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (the same humility applied to mistakes); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (being coachable as a protégé); onboarding-newcomers [A9.7] (learning from a newcomer's fresh eyes).
- **Source reach:** The book treats teachability as individual self-development; the leaf A1.6 and the situation tags reach toward *group* learning, which LLR does not develop. Attribute to LLR for the leader's own learning stance, not for team learning practices.

### Honor holds hunger in check — character as the foundation
concepts:    [A1.3 lead-by-example] [A4.3 trust-building]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  The Three Hs
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2; ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Build character deliberately, because it is the precondition for being followed at all. The book defines "honor" as integrity (not doing wrong) plus character (doing right because it's right, even when hard and unpopular), and argues that honor is "the force that holds a leader's hunger in check" so ambition serves others rather than the self. Who you become, it claims, matters more than what you accomplish.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People follow a leader only as far as they trust them, and "people will not follow a leader they can't trust" — so character is the substrate of all influence [A1.1]. Without honor, hunger "runs rampant and ultimately serves only selfish interests"; with it, drive becomes productive for others. The book cites the recurring pattern of high-profile leaders who "crash and burn" not from incompetence but from a character failure — a time bomb that eventually goes off.
- **Example in context:** The book leans on the corporate-scandal era (Enron, Tyco) as evidence that no degree of charisma or results buys lasting influence once character is missing — a direct illustration of the Ledger's "zero" effect (see Trilateral Ledger entry).
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a volunteer group, where the leader has no contractual hold, trust *is* the binding force; a single integrity breach can collapse it. The claim is argued by maxim and example, not study.
- **Related concepts:** trust-building [A4.3] (honor is what makes trust possible); Trilateral Ledger / self-mastery [A4.1] (character as one of the three multiplied dimensions); lead-by-example [A1.3] (character is shown, not announced).

### Self-mastery first — the Trilateral Ledger and confronting brutal reality
concepts:    [A1.6 failure-as-learning] [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Character × Tasks × Relationships multiplies
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Grow yourself before you try to grow your influence, and assess yourself honestly across three dimensions — Character, Tasks (getting things done), and Relationships (getting along with and bonding to people). The book's Trilateral Leadership Ledger multiplies the three (each scored 0–10), so the rule for improvement is to lift your *weakest* dimension, not coast on your strongest. Cited via Beliles & McDowell: "You must rule yourself before you rule others."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because the three multiply rather than add, a near-zero in any one collapses overall effectiveness regardless of the others — "anything times zero is still zero." The book also warns that the chief obstacle to growth is self-deception: people overrate themselves and can't see their own "blind spots," so honest self-assessment (Jim Collins's "confronting brutal reality," ideally checked by a mentor) is the starting move.
- **Example in context:** "Mister B" — Tasks 6, Character 3, Relationships 0 — has zero leadership effectiveness; raising his strongest area (Tasks) to 8 still leaves him near zero, because the relationship gap caps him. The lesson: weaknesses undo strengths.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader who is task-capable but neglects relationships (or vice versa) will hit an invisible wall the Ledger makes visible; it reframes "I'm doing everything myself" as possibly a relationship- or delegation-deficit, not an effort deficit. The model is a heuristic the authors propose, not a validated instrument — the specific scores are illustrative.
- **Related concepts:** relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (why a relationship "zero" is fatal); failure-as-learning [A1.6] (honest self-assessment as growth); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (a Tasks-heavy, Relationships-light leader hoards work).
- **Source reach:** The Ledger is the authors' own framework with no cited evidence; present it as a useful self-diagnostic ("Brady and Woodward suggest…"), not as established science.

### Stepping into the role — champions don't start that way
concepts:    [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter] [S:scared-to-ask-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  The Three Hs
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Expect to feel inadequate, and lead anyway. The book is blunt that there will be "many opportunities to feel second best," that champions are made not born ("they do not start out that way"), and that a leader must "succeed despite these feelings, not in the absence of them." Feeling unqualified is treated as normal raw material, not a disqualification.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The gap between your current ability and your potential is *supposed* to feel uncomfortable — "because our potential outreaches our current reality, it is natural to feel a tinge of inadequacy," and deliberate growth is what closes the gap. Framing the feeling as a measurement of potential (rather than evidence of unfitness) converts imposter anxiety into a growth signal. Belief plays a role too: the book argues people largely get what they expect, so a leader's self-talk shapes outcomes.
- **Example in context:** Tiger Woods looked like an overnight phenomenon but had, in fact, planned and trained toward beating Jack Nicklaus from age five — the "overnight success" that "took twenty years."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This directly addresses the first-timer's "I feel like an imposter," normalizing it rather than soothing it away — the leader is told to act through the feeling. Evidence is motivational anecdote and the "power of belief" literature the authors cite, not research.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (inadequacy resolved by trying and learning); fear-of-asking-or-delegating [A1.5] (the same fear blocks asks); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (persisting through the gap).

### Failure as tuition
concepts:    [A1.6 failure-as-learning]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch] [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  The Three Hs
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.6; ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat setbacks as information, not verdicts. The book's formulation: "Failure is not failure if something is learned from it" — failure that teaches "is simply tuition toward a future success." Great leaders, it argues, are not those who never err but those who "learn quickly and most effectively from their mistakes," extracting "every ounce of lesson" because "the lesson continues until the lesson is learned."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Mistakes are the fastest teacher precisely because they're concrete and consequential; the book recommends deliberate "crash test analyses" of what didn't work and why, so the failure converts into transferable knowledge. The cost of *not* learning is repetition — Einstein's line about doing the same thing and expecting different results is invoked. Struggle, the book argues elsewhere, is what "seasons" a leader and builds the instincts good judgment requires.
- **Example in context:** Mel Fisher searched for the sunken *Atocha* for years through lawsuits, government seizures, bankruptcy, and his son's drowning, repeating "Today's the day," continually improving his methods after each setback until he found the treasure — failure metabolized into iteration.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader whose first event flops needs the reframe that the flop is data, not a sentence — it keeps them in the game and turns the post-mortem into a method. Evidence is anecdote (Fisher) and maxim.
- **Related concepts:** stepping-into-the-role [A1.4] (failure normalized for the beginner); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (the emotional side of persisting); allows-struggle-to-instruct in mentoring [A9.6] (the leader later does this *for* others).

### Willing to do any job, but don't do their work — enabling vs doing
concepts:    [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing] [A1.3 lead-by-example]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.3
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold a precise line: there is "no job below the leader," so be *willing* to do any task — but don't actually take over your members' work, because that is reverting from leading to doing. The book frames a leader who delegates only because they don't want to do something as having "reverted to managing instead of leading," while a leader who hogs the doing has stopped leading too. The leader "pulls" by their own performance rather than "pushing" the group.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The tension is real: a leader must demonstrate work ethic and be in the trenches (so the example is credible), yet must not absorb the work that belongs to others (or the group can't grow and the leader becomes the limit). The resolution the book offers is that the leader's *own specific duties* — vision, judgment, the "big rocks" only they can carry — are the work; doing subordinates' tasks is a misallocation. Eisenhower's string image captures the "pull, don't push" mechanism: pull a string and it follows; push it and it buckles.
- **Example in context:** The book cites Peter Drucker — "No leader is worth his salt who won't set up chairs" — to make the *willingness* point, immediately qualifying that the leader's job is not to tackle subordinates' responsibilities.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the precise concept behind "I'm doing everything myself": the fix isn't to stop caring or stop modeling effort, it's to stop *absorbing* work that belongs to others. The distinction is argued, not measured.
- **Related concepts:** avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (where "doing it all" leads); delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (the constructive alternative); lead-by-example [A1.3] (why willingness still matters).

### Casting vision — lead with why, and let people own it
concepts:    [A2.1 shared-purpose] [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing] [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is influence, earned not positional
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.3
- **Claim / recommendation:** Make casting and re-casting the vision a core, repeated act of leadership, and shape it so people see how their own effort fits — and feel ownership of it. The book (citing Barna) argues effective leaders "enable people to own the vision as if they had thought it up by themselves" and clarify how each person's gifts contribute. The leader is "the amplifier and consistent reminder" of the why.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The book's claim — "people are much more motivated by purpose and cause than anything else" — means vision is the renewable fuel that sustains effort "over the long haul" where tasks and rewards run dry [A3.1]. Crucially, the gripe that stalls groups is usually not disagreement with the vision but "no sense of personal participation in its pursuit," so the leader's job is to connect each person's role to the whole, converting a told vision into an owned one [A3.2]. Repetition matters because a vision stated once fades.
- **Example in context:** The book offers Walt Disney: when someone lamented he'd died before seeing Disneyland, his wife replied that if he hadn't *already* seen it in his mind, it would never have been built — the leader sees the future first, then casts it for others.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Unclear what we're doing" and "the group lost its why" are vision-casting failures; the entry tells the coach to treat them as such, and to check whether people have a *role* in the vision, not just knowledge of it. Evidence is illustrative; the ownership claim aligns with the broader canon (Ganz, Pink, Han).
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (owned vision motivates); intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (purpose as durable fuel); a-vision-big-enough-for-many-leaders [A9.4] (vision that scales beyond the leader).

### The Three Levels of Motivation
concepts:    [A3.1 intrinsic-motivation] [A3.6 money-and-motivation] [A2.1 shared-purpose]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  The deepest motivation is purpose
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2
- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand that motivation runs in three ascending levels — (1) material success/money, (2) recognition and respect, (3) purpose, destiny, and legacy — and that the deepest is the most sustaining. The book tells a leader to cultivate the deeper levels in themselves "on a regular basis," and the implication for leading a group is to reach for purpose, not just rewards or applause, when motivation flags. Money and recognition are real motivators but shallow; they're satisfiable and external.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The mechanism is durability: material rewards, once attained, stop pulling, and recognition depends on others' approval (which the book warns against over-needing); purpose is internal and inexhaustible, so it "sustains it over the long haul." The book explicitly ranks respect/recognition as a *stronger* pull than money ("people will do more for respect and admiration than they will ever do for money"), and purpose stronger still. This is a near-exact analog of Pink's hierarchy (autonomy/mastery/purpose over carrots), reached by a different route [A3.3].
- **Example in context:** The book reads Wilberforce's 47-year fight against the slave trade — at the cost of health, career, and reputation — as Level-3 motivation: a calling no material or reputational reward could explain or sustain.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When volunteers drift or stop showing up, the coach can use this to diagnose *which* level the group has been running on — and whether it's leaning on shallow fuel (a transactional ask) that's now spent, versus a purpose that's gone unspoken. The framework is the authors' own, supported by maxim and citation rather than data; for the empirical case, route to Pink's Drive.
- **Related concepts:** money-and-motivation [A3.6] (the shallow level and its limits); shared-purpose [A2.1] (the deepest level in practice); celebration-and-recognition [A10.3] (the middle level, used well).
- **Source reach:** LLR offers no studies; the three-level model is asserted. Where the coach needs evidence (e.g., that money can *crowd out* intrinsic motivation), attribute to Pink, not LLR.

### Self-motivation is the leader's own job
concepts:    [A3.1 intrinsic-motivation] [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]
situations:  [S:burning-out] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  The deepest motivation is purpose
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2; ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Own your own motivation; don't outsource it. The book insists "it is the sole responsibility of a leader to motivate herself," that there is "no source of external circumstances that can be relied upon to provide consistent motivation," and that staying hungry is "actually a discipline" requiring regular care and feeding. A wise mentor, it notes, will decline the job of keeping a protégé motivated, because that has to be internal.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The book frames ambition as fragile and decaying: comfort and complacency pull a leader off the road, frustration risks discouragement, so motivation must be actively renewed by returning to vision and the deeper levels of motivation. Because a leader "takes people somewhere," the moment they stop moving they stop leading — making self-motivation a precondition of leading at all. Initiative (being a self-starter) is treated as a non-negotiable marker of a maturing leader.
- **Example in context:** No single flagship case; the book uses the "Road of Success" image — comfort on one shoulder, frustration on the other — and Anthony Robbins's "if you get a big enough why, you can always figure out the how" to make the point that the leader manages their own fuel.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A leader who waits to *feel* motivated will stall; this entry tells the coach to treat motivation as a practice the leader maintains, and connects "no one takes initiative" partly to a leader who hasn't modeled self-starting. Support is exhortation, not evidence.
- **Related concepts:** intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (the deep fuel being managed); stepping-into-the-role [A1.4] (acting before feeling ready); leader-self-care [A10.2] (sustaining the fuel without burning out).
- **Source reach:** The book's "motivate yourself, always" stance can tip into ignoring real burnout; pair with A10 (self-care) so the coach doesn't push a depleted volunteer to simply "stay hungry."

### People don't follow expertise until they feel you care
concepts:    [A4.3 trust-building] [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:newcomers-dont-stick]
handoff:
anchors to:  Character × Tasks × Relationships multiplies
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.6; ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Lead with care before competence. The book repeats the maxim that people don't care how much a leader knows until they know how much the leader cares, and tells leaders to take "an active, sincere interest" in each person — learning names and life details, finding genuine points of commonality. Relationship comes before the ask, the correction, or the task.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** People-skills sit *on top of* a caring heart, not instead of one — "there is nothing worse than a would-be leader pumped full of people skills but operating from a devious heart," so character and genuine care are what make the skills land [A1.3]. The book pictures connection like a boat finding a harbor: the leader circles until they find a real point of commonality where a relationship can dock. Until that's done, instruction and influence have nothing to land on.
- **Example in context:** The book invokes Dale Carnegie's image of everyone wearing an invisible sign reading "Make me feel important," and treats remembering names and personal details as the concrete practice of showing care.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "The group feels transactional" and "newcomers don't stick" often trace to a leader who related to people as task-units; this entry names care-before-competence as the corrective. Evidence is the people-skills tradition (Carnegie), not study.
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1-relational-meeting [A4.2] (the practice of getting to know people); relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (care does strategic work); transactional-vs-relational-ask [A3.3] (the contrast this avoids).

### The emotional bank account — goodwill before correction
concepts:    [A4.3 trust-building]
situations:  [S:two-members-clashing] [S:conflict-got-personal]
handoff:
anchors to:  Character × Tasks × Relationships multiplies
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Build up goodwill *before* you need to spend it. Borrowing Covey's "emotional bank account," the book tells leaders to make regular "deposits" of goodwill into their relationships so that when a withdrawal comes — a hard correction, a slight, a misunderstanding — the account doesn't go bankrupt. Especially when you'll later need to give uncomfortable feedback, "there had better be a relationship the mentor can draw upon."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Trust is reliance on someone's character and intent; it accrues only through time and shared experience, so a relationship with a surplus can absorb a hurtful moment ("I'm sure she didn't mean anything by it") that would bankrupt a thin one. Correction without a prior balance reads as attack; with a balance, it reads as care. The mechanism is sequencing again: invest first, withdraw later.
- **Example in context:** The book uses the account metaphor itself as the illustration — surplus goodwill lets a relationship survive an inevitable hurt, where an empty account turns the same comment into a rupture.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Two members clashing, or a conflict that "got personal," often reflects relationships with no goodwill reserve; this entry tells the coach that the prevention is earlier deposits, and that a leader can't correct effectively into a deficit. The model is Covey's, used illustratively.
- **Related concepts:** trust-building [A4.3] (what the account stores); course-correction in mentoring [A8.5] (the withdrawal that needs a balance); repair-and-mutual-accountability [A8.5] (rebuilding after a withdrawal).

### Know your people — ask, listen, seek first to understand
concepts:    [A4.2 the-1:1-relational-meeting] [A9.6 coaching-group-members]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Before leading or developing someone, get to know them — what makes them tick, what they dream about, where their thinking is, what challenges and victories shaped them. The book frames this as Covey's "seek first to understand," accomplished by asking questions and *listening* far more than talking ("two ears and one mouth… use them proportionately"). The leader cannot lead, it argues, "those we do not know."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Drawing someone out reveals their motivation, perspective, blind spots, and commitment level — the raw material for tailoring how you support them. The book insists each person needs *unique*, not equal, treatment, attending to their temperament, learning style, and "love language," because spot-on support requires knowing how a person is wired. Listening is the diagnostic instrument; questions are the probe.
- **Example in context:** The book gives a 13-question listening agenda a mentor works through over time (what motivates him, what victories has he had, what's the basis of his character, where is his thinking) — a structured way to understand before instructing.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Newcomers don't stick" frequently means no one took the time to understand them as individuals; this entry tells the coach the foundational practice is a genuine one-to-one of curiosity, not an onboarding script. Support is the Covey tradition, not data.
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1-relational-meeting [A4.2] (the practice itself); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (understanding precedes coaching); onboarding-newcomers [A9.7] (understanding the newcomer well).
- **Source reach:** LLR's listening agenda is mentor→protégé, asymmetric; the register's A4.2 (the relational one-to-one) is more mutual. Keep LLR for the "understand before you lead" principle; route practice depth elsewhere.

### Autonomy vs micromanagement — guided, not governed
concepts:    [A7.3 autonomy-vs-direction] [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck

- **Claim / recommendation:** As your people grow into leaders, let them actually lead — set direction but leave them room to decide *how*, and resist meddling. The book is emphatic that strong leaders "refuse to be 'governed'; they will only agree, at the most, to be 'guided,'" so a leader who micromanages "will never attract or retain the highest leadership talent." Give them "the freedom to fail or fly," and let them "live and learn from their own results."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Capable people are drained, not helped, by a leader's need to be involved in every detail; the book cites Eisenhower — a leader who can't decentralize gets "weighed down with details" and loses the big issues. The cost of getting this wrong is twofold: the leader becomes the bottleneck, and the best people leave or stay small.
- **Example in context:** The book contrasts Jimmy Carter — who mastered every detail himself and whose administration suffered for it — with Ronald Reagan, who "left the details to his subordinates and focused on the big picture." Same job, different leadership level.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "No one takes initiative" and "I can't let go" are two faces of the same micromanagement trap; this entry tells the coach to check whether the leader is governing where they should be guiding. The contrast is illustrative and politically framed — present the *principle*, not the partisan read.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (autonomy is over outcomes); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (micromanagement *is* the bottleneck); hierarchy-just-enough [A7.6] (only as much control as coordination needs).

### The Five Levels of Influence (overview)
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  The five levels of influence are progressive
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Map your leadership against five ascending levels — Learning, Performing, Leading, Developing Leaders, Developing Developers-of-Leaders — and understand that growth means *climbing*, while keeping every level below you. A leader can be at different levels in different parts of life, and so can a group; the model is a diagnostic for "where am I, and what's the next move." The book builds on Maxwell's and Collins's level schemes and refines them into a developmental route.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Each level expands the leader's *scope*, and influence compounds as you climb: a Performer adds (own output), a Leader multiplies (a team), a Developer-of-leaders grows exponentially (leaders who each multiply), and a Developer-of-developers endures (leaders who reproduce). Climbing doesn't shed lower levels — a head coach still learns and performs — so the levels accumulate rather than replace. The higher you go, the more it is about who you *are* (the "art") and the less about discrete actions (the "science").
- **Example in context:** The book tracks one figure — a novice soccer player → scorer → captain → head coach → sport-reshaping legend — through all five levels, and reads Queen Elizabeth I's reign (survival/learning → performing → gathering talent → enduring tradition) as the same ascent.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the book's central contribution and the spine the coach should reach for when a leader is stuck at "everything routes through me": the diagnosis is usually "you're operating at Level 3 and the next move is Level 4." The scheme is the authors' synthesis of Maxwell/Collins, not an evidenced model.
- **Related concepts:** developing-leaders [A9.1] (Level 4's core work); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (the Level-3 ceiling the model exposes); succession-and-ceding-control [A9.4] (Level 5's core work).

### Leading (Level 3) — team results, leverage, and the present-leader ceiling
concepts:    [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing] [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.8
- **Claim / recommendation:** At Level 3 the unit of work shifts from "what I can do" to "what a team can do" — the leader gets results through others by modeling the way, serving, coaching, orchestrating, and communicating. But the book names the built-in ceiling plainly: at this level "the group grows when the leader is present," and effective Level-3 leaders are spotted precisely because their groups thrive only when they "take the field." Recognizing that ceiling is the whole point of the entry.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The book calls the team a *lever*: coordinating people produces "the mechanical advantage" of multiplication — "individuals can flourish but teams explode" — so Level 3 is a genuine leap beyond personal performance [A4.1]. But because the leader is the coordinating node, results scale only with the leader's own presence and attention; "the speed of the group is the speed of the leader." That dependency is exactly what Level 4 is built to break (see Developing Leaders).
- **Example in context:** The mule fable: a heavy truck is pulled from a ditch not by what each mule can pull alone but by "what they can pull together" — the leader's job is to make the team's combined pull exceed the sum of parts.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "I'm doing everything myself" / "work is all on a few people" is the felt symptom of a leader stuck at the Level-3 ceiling; this entry tells the coach to *name* the ceiling and point toward developing other leaders, not toward the leader working harder. Argued by analogy, not data.
- **Related concepts:** enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (the leader's role at Level 3); developing-leaders [A9.1] (the move that breaks the ceiling); servant-leadership [A1.1] (how a Level-3 leader holds the role).

### Servant leadership — leadership as sacrifice and service
concepts:    [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent] [A10.4 collective-care]
situations:  [S:burning-out] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Leadership is influence, earned not positional
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.8
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat leadership as service, not perks. The book argues "leadership is about service to others," that "the single most effective way for a leader to get the most out of his organization is to serve its people," and that the role demands sacrifice — the higher you rise, the more your own conduct is scrutinized and the less tolerance there is for error. Perks are side benefits, not the purpose.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because everything a leader does is amplified through the people they're responsible for, self-interest and pettiness "stop progress cold," whereas serving people's ability to perform is what actually produces results. The book offers a seven-question test of servant leadership (do your people believe you'll sacrifice your own interest for the team's good; that you want and value their ideas; that you understand their lives; that you're committed to their growth) — a usable mirror. The deepest rewards, it claims, become "the satisfaction of seeing other people's lives enriched."
- **Example in context:** Washington at Valley Forge again serves as the model — living in his tent until his men were housed, eating their rations, staying on the scene — leadership as shared sacrifice rather than privilege.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a volunteer leader, service framing keeps the role from curdling into status-seeking and reorients "doing everything myself" toward "serving people's capacity" rather than doing their work. The seven questions are a concrete self-check. Support is the servant-leadership tradition (Blanchard), not study.
- **Related concepts:** leading-by-earned-authority [A1.1] (service is how authority stays legitimate); collective-care [A10.4] (serving the group's people); leader-self-care [A10.2] (the boundary — service is not self-erasure).
- **Source reach:** The book's "the higher you rise, the more your freedoms shrink" can read as self-sacrifice without limit; balance with A10.2 so the coach doesn't valorize burnout.

### Developing Leaders (Level 4) — duplication and replacing yourself
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership] [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Break the Level-3 ceiling by developing *other* leaders, not just followers — a Level-4 leader is "basically in the business of replacing himself." The defining shift: where a Level-3 leader's group grows only when they're present, a Level-4 leader's group "gets results even when they are not around." The book's blunt distinction: "average leaders lead followers while great leaders lead leaders."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** This is duplication — once another person can lead a team to results, the original leader's reach multiplies through people who each multiply, producing exponential rather than linear growth. The leader stops being the single coordinating node, so the group's capacity is no longer capped by the leader's attention or presence. The internal habit changes: a Level-4 leader meets a challenge by asking "whom have I developed who can handle this?" rather than "how do I handle this?"
- **Example in context:** The book reads Ray Kroc's McDonald's as duplication at scale — Kroc succeeded by "helping its franchisees succeed," marshaling hundreds of independent operators rather than doing it himself — and Nelson's captains as duplication in the field (each able to win without the admiral's signal).
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the prescription the coach reaches for whenever the leader is the bottleneck: the answer to "everything routes through me" is to develop people who can lead in your absence. It converges directly with the canon's "leadership develops leadership" (Han) and the snowflake/distributed model (Ganz). Argued by analogy, not data.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (how duplication is enacted); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (the problem it solves); succession-and-ceding-control [A9.4] (Level 5 extends it).

### Talent-scouting — who is worth developing
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A5.1 composition-and-skills]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  The five levels of influence are progressive
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Be deliberate about whom you invest development time in, because the time is finite. The book surveys many leaders' criteria lists, then settles on the Three Hs (hungry, hone-able, honorable) plus four signals: activity, respect (for you as mentor), a connected relationship, and a positive attitude with basic "relatability." The single strongest tell is *activity* — look for someone already in motion.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** "It is impossible to steer a parked car, but a car in motion turns with ease" — activity signals the ambition, courage, and initiative that can't be installed from outside, while almost everything else can be taught along the way. Respect is load-bearing because if the protégé doesn't believe the mentor can help, the process breaks before it begins. - **Why it matters / evidence:** When a leader complains "no one takes initiative," part of the coach's job is to ask whom they're trying to develop — and whether they're picking by potential signals (activity, attitude) rather than current skill. The lists are gathered from named practitioners, not validated; "hire attitude, train skill" (Schultz) captures the spirit.

- **Source reach:** This sits at the A5.1 `org-check` line: the leader choosing whom to develop *inside* their group is in scope; broad recruitment/selection mechanics route to H:recruitment. Keep the why-level "who's worth developing," not hiring tactics.

### Empowering leaders — the freedom to fail or fly

situations:  [S:i-cant-let-go] [S:no-one-takes-initiative] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:

source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Empowering means handing over real control and decision authority — letting people "lead their own teams and make their own mistakes," giving them "the freedom to fail or fly." The book warns that "true leaders will not stick with the Level 4 Leader unless given a chance to spread their wings," because without that chance "they will never reach their potential." This is the difference between developing followers and developing leaders.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Leaders grow only by exercising judgment and owning outcomes, including failures; a leader who withholds authority keeps people as followers and stunts them. The fear that blocks this is the leader's own — the reluctance to hand off and risk a mistake — which the book treats as a growth edge to push through [A1.5]. Empowerment is therefore as much about the leader's fear as the protégé's readiness.
- **Example in context:** Nelson's "Band of Brothers" embodies empowerment under pressure: having taught his captains his thinking, he delegated "crucial tactical decisions to them in the heat of battle" rather than clinging to signals — and that delegation is what won the Nile.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "I can't let go" is the leader-side block; "no one takes initiative" is its downstream effect. This entry tells the coach that empowerment requires giving away authority *and* tolerating others' mistakes, and that the obstacle is often the leader's fear, not the people's capacity. Argued by example.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (what's handed over); fear-of-asking-or-delegating [A1.5] (the leader-side block); autonomy-vs-direction [A7.3] (the room being given).

### The mentoring relationship — between Friendship and Dictatorship
concepts:    [A9.6 coaching-group-members] [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:newcomers-dont-stick] [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff:
anchors to:  The five levels of influence are progressive
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Mentor from a position that is neither buddy nor boss. The book frames the mentoring relationship as bordered by "the Ditches of Friendship and Dictatorship": too much friendship erodes the respect and accountability the relationship needs, while drifting toward dictator breeds resentment and lost motivation. A mentor "cares about the performance of the protégé enough to say what a friend would not" — going *beyond* friendship — while respecting them too much to become bossy.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The mentor's leverage is voluntary respect, not authority, so the relationship has to hold a careful middle: explicit framing ("Is it okay for me to rise above mere friendship and speak as a mentor?"; "I'm not your boss — I only want what's best for you") keeps both parties clear on the footing. This matters because much of mentoring is delivering hard truths, which a pure friend won't and a dictator can't do without rupture. The footing is what makes correction land as care.
- **Example in context:** The book offers the framing statements themselves as the technique — a mentor naming the relationship out loud before hard advice, so the protégé hears the coming truth as care rather than command.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A volunteer leader has *no* boss-authority over peers, so this "beyond friendship, short of boss" stance is exactly the register's "accountability without authority" problem; the entry gives the coach a workable posture. Support is the authors' framing, not data.
- **Related concepts:** peer-accountability-without-authority [A8.3] (the no-authority condition); course-correction [A8.5] (what the footing enables); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (the broader practice).

### Course correction — "humble truth and aggressive love"
concepts:    [A9.6 coaching-group-members] [A8.5 repair-and-mutual-accountability]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:conflict-got-personal] [S:two-members-clashing]
handoff:
anchors to:  The five levels of influence are progressive
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Give hard feedback as a blend the book (citing McDowell & Hostetler) calls "humble truth and aggressive love" — correction delivered gently enough that the person isn't demoralized, but on a foundation of care strong enough to permit real directness. Address the behavior, not the person; hold up "a mirror of truth" so the protégé can see what they can't, and confront issues while they're small. Use a "sandwich": affirm a real strength, address the problem boldly and warmly, then rebuild the person.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Most people instinctively do the reverse — "aggressive truth and humble love," lobbing "grenades of truth" — which wounds without helping. The book argues the *stronger the love, the stronger the allowable correction*, because care is what lets truth be received; without an established relationship, correction bankrupts the bond [A4.3]. The reframing skill matters too: a mentor takes the problem as the protégé defined it and shows it in a more workable light.
- **Example in context:** The "paper apples" image (from Tripp): a gardener staples paper apples onto a tree that grows bad fruit — pointless, because surface change isn't real change. The book uses it to insist correction must reach the heart, not just behavior.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Conflict got personal" and "volunteers flaky" both need a leader who can correct without rupturing; this entry gives the coach the posture (care-backed directness) and the small-and-early rule. Support is illustrative; route any deeper conflict-mediation practice to the relevant sibling coach.
- **Related concepts:** repair-and-mutual-accountability [A8.5] (correction as repair, not punishment); the-emotional-bank-account [A4.3] (the balance correction draws on); task-vs-relationship-conflict [A8.1] (behavior-not-person).

### Get the protégé to take responsibility — and hold them accountable
concepts:    [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks] [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand over not just tasks but ownership of *results*, and then hold the person to them. The book describes the mentor "passing the baton" — "now it is the protégé's war" — with the explicit stance: "I won't take the credit when you win. I won't take the blame when you don't." The mentor holds the developing leader accountable for results, and "forces" them to measure up to their potential.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Accountability works partly through relationship: "people will often do for the approval of someone else what they won't accomplish for themselves," so a respected mentor's expectation pulls performance that self-discipline alone wouldn't. But the responsibility genuinely transfers — the protégé owns implementation and its outcomes — which is what distinguishes developing a leader from supervising a worker. If nothing improves in a reasonable time, the book says the mentor should redirect their energy to a more committed person.
- **Example in context:** The "Message to Garcia" story: a lieutenant is told to get a message to a Cuban general whose location is unknown; he takes the letter and delivers it without asking "where is he?" — the book's emblem of someone who takes full responsibility for the result, not just the task.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Volunteers flaky" is partly an accountability-without-authority problem; this entry gives the coach the relational mechanism (people perform for someone whose respect they want) and the credit/blame stance that makes ownership real. Argued by maxim and anecdote.
- **Related concepts:** delegating-outcomes-not-tasks [A9.2] (ownership of results, not chores); peer-accountability-without-authority [A8.3] (holding peers with no formal power); coaching-group-members [A9.6] (accountability as part of coaching).
- **Source reach:** "Find a more worthy student" suits a paid/voluntary-recruit downline; in a volunteer group, dropping people is costly — the coach should keep the *ownership-transfer* principle and soften the "cut them loose" implication.

### Pursue a heart change, not surface compliance
concepts:    [A9.6 coaching-group-members]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  The five levels of influence are progressive
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Aim development at how a person *thinks*, not just what they do — "ninety-five percent of mentoring is about helping the protégé develop proper thinking." The book (citing Tripp) argues that change driven by external pressure or incentive disappears the moment the pressure is removed; only change "way down deep, at the level of the heart" lasts. So the target of coaching is the underlying perspective and beliefs, not the surface behavior.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Behavior is downstream of thinking and motivation; address only the behavior and you get temporary, brittle compliance that reverts under stress. The book's method is to gradually impart a way of thinking — over time the protégé starts asking "what would my mentor think about this?" and eventually reasons the way the mentor would — so the change is internalized rather than imposed. Lasting change is therefore slow and relational, not a one-time instruction.
- **Example in context:** The "paper apples" tree: stapling fake fruit onto a tree that produces bad fruit changes the appearance and nothing else — "lasting change always takes place through the pathway of the heart."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When a fix doesn't stick — momentum lost again, the same volunteer flaking again — the coach can ask whether the leader addressed behavior or thinking; the durable lever is the latter. Support is illustrative (Tripp), not empirical.
- **Related concepts:** coaching-group-members [A9.6] (the practice this governs); failure-as-learning [A1.6] (changing how setbacks are interpreted); how-norms-form-and-become-culture [A6.1] (shared thinking hardening into culture).

### Developing Developers of Leaders (Level 5) — succession and ceding control
concepts:    [A9.4 succession-and-ceding-control] [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck]
situations:  [S:i-cant-let-go] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  A leader who must be present is a bottleneck
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.10
- **Claim / recommendation:** The highest level is developing leaders who in turn develop *other* leaders, so the work outlives any one person — a Level-5 leader is "only as good as the enduring quality and succession of their vision." This requires a vision big enough to carry many leaders, an ego firmly in check, and the willingness to surround yourself with people who could eclipse you. The book's contrast: "Small leaders surround themselves with followers; big leaders surround themselves with other leaders," and the biggest with leaders who could outshine them.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Succession is what converts a leader's influence from something that ends with them (Level 3) or with their direct protégés (Level 4) into something self-propagating across generations. It demands handling unrest — strong, self-directed leaders won't form consensus easily and will speak their minds — and not micromanaging them, because "true leaders refuse to be governed." Ego is the gating constraint: a leader who needs the glory can't develop people who might surpass them.
- **Example in context:** The Apostle Paul "appointed elders in every city," trained protégés (Timothy, Titus) to train others, and built a structure that kept spreading "centuries after he lived" — the emblem of a vision that reproduces leaders far beyond the founder's reach. (The book also names MLK as a vision that outlived the leader and inspired Mandela and Walesa.)
- **Why it matters / evidence:** This is the register's succession concept and the ultimate answer to the founder-bottleneck: build leaders who build leaders, then let go. For a movement context it's the most resonant idea in the book — leadership that doesn't depend on any single person. Argued by historical example, not data; the Paul material is heavily religious (translate to secular succession).
- **Related concepts:** avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (the problem succession finally solves); a-vision-big-enough-for-many-leaders [A2.1] (what carries many leaders); autonomy-vs-direction [A7.3] (ceding control to strong leaders).

### A vision big enough for many leaders — that outlives the leader
concepts:    [A2.1 shared-purpose] [A9.4 succession-and-ceding-control]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why] [S:unclear-what-were-doing]
handoff:
anchors to:  The deepest motivation is purpose
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.9; ch.10
- **Claim / recommendation:** Cast a vision large enough that many leaders can find room in it, and that doesn't depend on you to survive. The book argues top talent is attracted "not solely by high-dollar offers, nor by perks and power… but by a compelling vision" — one they can believe in, that "provides room for them to make their mark." At the highest level the leader and the vision become intertwined, yet the vision must be able to carry on through others.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A small or leader-centric vision can't hold multiple strong leaders — there isn't "enough to do for everybody" and ego competition sets in; a big vision makes the leaders' individual goals "seem petty in comparison," aligning them rather than pitting them against each other [A9.5]. And because purpose is the deepest, most durable motivator, a vision tied to a cause (not to the leader's glory) is what keeps people in it for the long haul and lets the work continue past any individual.
- **Example in context:** The book points to Martin Luther King, Jr. — whose "I have a dream" vision attracted many leaders, changed laws, and inspired later movements (Mandela, Walesa) decades on — as a vision that became inseparable from, yet outlived, the leader.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When a group has "lost its why" or can't hold more than one strong personality, the coach can ask whether the vision is big enough to be shared — a too-small or too-leader-centric vision can't scale leadership. The claim aligns with Ganz/Han; LLR's support is historical example.
- **Related concepts:** shared-purpose [A2.1] (the vision itself); succession-and-ceding-control [A9.4] (vision outliving the leader); intrinsic-motivation [A3.1] (purpose as the durable draw).

### Attitude conquers circumstances — resilience and persistence
concepts:    [A10.5 resilience-through-setbacks] [A1.6 failure-as-learning]

source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold attitude and persistence as deliberate disciplines, especially through setbacks, unfair conditions, and critics. The book argues a leader must "succeed despite" rough conditions by "focusing upon only what they can control," that desire eventually "trumps talent," and that effort "fully releases its reward only after a person refuses to quit." - **Reasoning / mechanism:** The book's claim is that interpretation, not events, drives outcomes — "it's not what happens, but how the leader sees what happens, that counts" — so a leader's controllable variable is their response. Persistence works because many would-be leaders quit just before the payoff; staying in past the point others fold is itself the differentiator. Critics are reframed as a near-inevitable sign of doing something worthwhile, so their noise is discounted rather than obeyed.
- **Example in context:** A SEAL-training instructor's observation that washouts fail on *attitude*, not fitness — "it's not physical toughness, it's mental toughness" — illustrates attitude as the determining variable under extreme stress.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Lost momentum after launch" needs a leader who can hold the group through a low without spiraling; this entry gives the resilience stance — control what you can, persist past the dip, discount the critics. Support is anecdote and maxim, not study.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (setbacks as data); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (holding the group through losses); self-motivation [A3.1] (refueling through the dip).
- **Source reach:** The "attitude conquers everything / never be satisfied" framing can mask real burnout and structural problems; pair with A10.1–A10.2 so the coach doesn't tell a depleted or under-resourced volunteer that they just need a better attitude.

### Recognition and celebration
concepts:    [A10.3 celebration-and-recognition]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff:
anchors to:  The deepest motivation is purpose
source:      [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.3; ch.8; ch.9
- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize contribution actively, specifically, and publicly. The book tells leaders to "specialize in catching people in the act of doing things right," to reward outstanding performance "publicly" so it sets a standard others can reach for, and — at the developing-leaders level — to *bestow* recognition on others rather than collect it ("Level 4 Leaders are focused upon how much recognition they can bestow"). Praise should be specific, not flattery.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Recognition is the book's middle level of motivation — a deep human need to be "noticed and appreciated" that pulls effort harder than money does. "The things that get accomplished are the things that get rewarded," so recognition directs behavior, not just feeling. As a leader develops others, sharing credit ("waving your people past") becomes a development tool, not a loss — the leader isn't competing with their own people.
- **Example in context:** The book cites the adage "Babies cry for it and grown men die for it" for recognition's pull, and praises Charles Schwab's view that appreciation and encouragement, not criticism, "develop the best that is in a person."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Flaky attendance and flagging effort often reflect people who don't feel seen; this entry tells the coach that specific, public recognition is a cheap, powerful lever — and that a leader who hoards credit is starving a core motivator. Support is the people-skills tradition, not data.
- **Related concepts:** the-Three-Levels-of-Motivation [A3.1] (recognition as the middle level); developing-leaders [A9.1] (recognition as a development tool); satisfaction-and-retention [A10.6] (recognition feeds staying).
