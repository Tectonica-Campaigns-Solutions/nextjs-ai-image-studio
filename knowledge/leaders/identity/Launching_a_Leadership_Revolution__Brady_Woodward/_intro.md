# RAGSRC — *Launching a Leadership Revolution* (Chris Brady & Orrin Woodward) — Source Summary

**Product:** Group Leader eCoach · **Doc:** RAGSRC source summary · **Owner:** TBD · **Review milestone:** post-test prune (Instruction Set A) · **Version:** v1.0 · **Date:** 2026-06-07

> **Scope note.** *Launching a Leadership Revolution* (LLR) is a **personal-development leadership book** written by the founders of a network-marketing training organization. Its native frame is the individual leader's heroic ascent — hunger, character, destiny — taught through great-man history (Churchill, Washington, Nelson, the Apostle Paul) and a providential/Christian lens, and its "develop leaders who develop leaders" logic originally describes building a downline. The coach must translate two things throughout: (1) **individual-success / wealth framing → leading *other people* inside a small group**, and (2) **God-given destiny → secular purpose or calling**. The book's strongest, most register-relevant contribution is its back half — the **Five Levels of Influence**, and specifically the claim that a leader whose group only thrives *when present* is a bottleneck, and that the highest leadership "replaces itself." That converges with Han, Ganz, and the collective-power canon; it is where this summary puts its weight. The front-half inner-game material maps cleanly onto **A1** and is core/permanent, but is more derivative. Where the book over-claims or where its evidence is anecdote rather than study, `Source reach:` lines and the closing Coverage gaps mark it. LLR is **light on empirical evidence** — it argues by maxim, story, and authority-citation, not data — so attribution should generally be "Brady and Woodward argue…," not "research shows."

## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

**Leadership is influence, and influence is earned — not positional.** A title, rank, or appointment does not make someone a leader; followers grant leadership by buying in to the *person* — their character, their results, their evident care — before they ever buy the vision, and that permission is earned continually and can be revoked. It holds because people have become, in the book's phrase, astute customers of leadership: they can spot a phony, so authority that isn't backed by trust and performance simply fails to move anyone. *(earned-authority-and-consent lens — A1.1.)*
flagship example: For six years Winston Churchill held no office and "none of the influence of public office," yet his warnings about Hitler moved a nation by the weight of his known character and judgment alone — influence with no position.
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *Launching a Leadership Revolution*, ch.1; ch.8governs: [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent] [A7.1 building-and-using-power] [A1.3 lead-by-example] [A4.3 trust-building]

**The Three Hs gate everyone: Hungry, Hone-able, Honorable.** Before any leadership skill matters, a would-be leader needs drive (hunger), teachability (hone-able), and integrity/character (honorable) — and honor is what holds hunger in check so ambition serves others instead of running rampant in self-interest. The mechanism is sequencing: skills layered on a person without drive go unused, without teachability go stale, and without character eventually detonate — so the three are prerequisites, not nice-to-haves.
flagship example: A sage holds a squire underwater until the boy nearly drowns, then tells him: when you want to lead as badly as you just wanted air, you'll be ready. Hunger is the gate.
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2governs: [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role] [A1.6 failure-as-learning] [A1.3 lead-by-example] [A1.1 leading-by-earned-authority-and-consent]

**Leadership effectiveness multiplies across Character, Tasks, and Relationships — so a zero anywhere zeros you out.** The book scores a leader as Character × Tasks × Relationships (the Trilateral Leadership Ledger): the three combine by multiplication, not addition, so excellence in two cannot rescue a zero in the third. The mechanism is that the three are interdependent in practice — a brilliant performer no one trusts gets no follow-through, and no amount of character substitutes for the ability to get things done.
flagship example: "Mister B" rates Tasks 6, Character 3, Relationships 0; his leadership effectiveness is 0 — and even raising Tasks to 8 leaves him near-zero, because anything times zero is zero.
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.4governs: [A1.6 failure-as-learning] [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy] [A1.3 lead-by-example]

**The deepest, most durable motivation is purpose — not money or recognition.** The book ranks motivation in three ascending levels: material success (shallowest), recognition and respect (deeper), and purpose / destiny / legacy (deepest and most sustaining over the long haul). It holds because shallow motivators are satisfiable and external — once you have the car or the applause, the fuel is spent — whereas purpose is inexhaustible and internal, so it survives the long stretches with no reward in sight. *(This converges with Pink's autonomy/mastery/purpose; see the Drive summary.)*
flagship example: The book contrasts a leader chasing perks with one who, like Wilberforce across 47 years against the slave trade, is carried by a calling that survives ruined health and reputation — the deeper the motive, the longer the endurance.
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.2governs: [A3.1 intrinsic-motivation] [A3.6 money-and-motivation] [A2.1 shared-purpose] [A10.3 celebration-and-recognition]

**The five levels of influence are progressive, and the highest is developing leaders who develop leaders.** A leader ascends Learning → Performing → Leading → Developing Leaders → Developing Developers-of-Leaders; each level keeps the ones below it, and influence compounds at each step — from a performer's *addition* (own output) to a leader's *multiplication* (a coordinated team) to a developer's *exponential* growth (other leaders who each multiply) to an enduring legacy (leaders who reproduce leaders). The mechanism is duplication: once your way of leading lives in other people who can also reproduce it, your reach no longer depends on your presence or your lifespan.
flagship example: A novice soccer player becomes a key scorer (perform), then team captain calling plays (lead), then head coach who develops other captains and assistant coaches (develop leaders), then a figure whose methods reshape the sport through people she never met (develop developers).
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.5; ch.10governs: [A9.1 developing-leaders] [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership] [A9.4 succession-and-ceding-control] [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]

**A leader who must be present is a bottleneck; real leadership replaces itself.** The book's sharpest diagnostic: at Level 3 "the group grows when the leader is present" — and that *is the ceiling*, because the leader's reach stops at the edge of their own attention. The fix is to develop other leaders who get results when you are *not* around (Level 4: "in the business of replacing himself"), and to keep ceding control as they grow rather than meddling — because strong leaders, in the book's view, refuse to be governed and will only be guided.
flagship example: Hunting Napoleon's fleet across the Mediterranean, Nelson schooled his captains so thoroughly over dinners that at the Battle of the Nile — fought at night, too dark for signals — each ship acted on its own initiative; "Each knew his duty," Nelson said. The leader's absence from the decision did not stop the win.
source: [Brady & Woodward](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/624022.Launching_a_Leadership_Revolution) — *LLR*, ch.8; ch.9; ch.10governs: [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck] [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks] [A7.3 autonomy-vs-direction] [A9.4 succession-and-ceding-control]
