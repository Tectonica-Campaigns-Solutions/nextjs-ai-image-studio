# Groundbreakers — ## Layer 2 — Concept Entries


### Enabling, not doing: organize yourself out of a job
concepts:    [A1.2 enabling-vs-doing]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Never do for people what they can do for themselves; Enable, don't do
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** The leader's job is to develop the capacity of others to do the work, not to do it themselves. Borrowing the Industrial Areas Foundation's core principle, organizers "should never do for people what they can do for themselves." In practice this means a team leader spends their time recruiting, training, and supporting members — not personally making the calls and knocking the doors. The thing to avoid is being the most productive worker on your own team.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A leader who does the work is a ceiling; a leader who builds others is a multiplier. OFA defined leadership, via Ganz, as "enabling others to achieve purpose in the face of uncertainty." Capacity that lives in one person disappears when that person tires or leaves; capacity built into other people compounds, because each developed leader can develop more [A9.1].
- **Example in context:** Field organizer Tony Speare described spending the majority of his time building self-sufficient volunteer teams, so that by the end "volunteers were calling other volunteers to recruit them… running all the trainings… and we were able to remove ourselves." The doer-staffer who "made 700 dials in a day" was, to deputy field director Alex Steele, doing it wrong — busy instead of building.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a first-time leader, this is the hardest and most important inversion: success is measured by what the group does without you, not by your own output. OFA's whole bet — winning two national campaigns on volunteer labor — rested on staff resisting the urge to "take shortcuts" and rack up their own numbers.
- **Related concepts:** develop-leaders [A9.1] (what enabling produces); delegate-outcomes [A9.2] (the mechanism of handing off); the-leader-in-the-middle trap [A9.3] (the failure mode this avoids).

### "Everyone can be a leader": stepping into the role
concepts:    [A1.4 stepping-into-the-role]
situations:  [S:i-feel-like-an-imposter]
handoff:
anchors to:  Enable, don't do
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.1, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Leadership in this model is not a credential or a personality type; it is a role anyone can be coached into. A first-timer who feels unqualified should be reassured that the campaign's working assumption, in field organizer Austin Brookley's words, was that "everyone can be a leader in a democracy." What qualifies someone is willingness to take responsibility and learn, not prior experience.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Because the leader's job is to enable rather than personally perform, the disqualifying traits people fear (I'm not the loudest, the most political, the most able-bodied) often don't bind. The relevant capacity — building relationships and developing others — can be taught inside a coaching relationship [A9.6].
- **Example in context:** Alex Watters, paralyzed from the chest down, worried aloud that he couldn't knock doors or even turn on the office lights. His director reframed the job entirely: he needed someone to inspire and organize volunteers "so that *they're* knocking your doors." Watters went on to build one of the biggest teams in the state.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Imposter feeling is the first barrier for a new volunteer leader, and it's usually grounded in a wrong picture of the job. Reframing leadership as a role of enabling — not heroics — is what let OFA turn unlikely people (a quadriplegic newcomer, a "lifelong Republican," a working mom in rural Nevada) into core leaders.
- **Related concepts:** failure-as-learning [A1.6] (resilience once in the role); coaching-members [A9.6] (how someone is grown into it); enabling-vs-doing [A1.2] (what the role actually is).
- **Source reach:** The book is rich on the *reframe* and the stories, but offers no general theory of imposter feeling or identity formation; attribute to the vignettes, not to a model of how confidence develops.

### Develop leaders, don't just recruit helpers
concepts:    [A9.1 developing-leaders]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize; Enable, don't do
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat every promising volunteer as a future leader to be grown, not a pair of hands to be used. The recommended path is a deliberate cycle: build a relationship, test commitment with a small piece of real responsibility, then ask the person to take on a leadership role — and then teach *them* to run the same cycle with others.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** This is what distinguishes organizing from mobilizing [A2.5]. Mitch Stewart contrasted the old model — one staffer at the center recruiting volunteers, so "your growth is limited" — with distributing leadership so volunteers recruit other volunteers. Investing early starts slower but produces self-replicating, compounding growth rather than a plateau.
- **Example in context:** Florida team leader Howard Dolginoff was phone-banking a few times a week when his organizer asked him to run the phone bank; soon he was "comfortable training other people" and repeating the cycle himself. The same staged process — one-on-one, test, ask — was run with eager and reluctant recruits alike.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group where nobody takes initiative is usually a group whose leader is recruiting helpers, not developing leaders. OFA's two best leading indicators of a healthy turf were *not* doors knocked but volunteer-recruitment calls made and one-on-ones held — i.e., development activity, not production.
- **Related concepts:** the-ask / escalating-commitment [A3.5] (the rungs of responsibility); delegate-outcomes [A9.2] (what you hand a new leader); coaching-members [A9.6] (the support around them).

### Delegate outcomes, not tasks
concepts:    [A9.2 delegating-outcomes-not-tasks]
situations:  [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people] [S:doing-everything-myself]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hand people ownership of a *result*, not a list of chores. Tell a member "this is your phone bank / your precinct / your turf to deliver," not "make these specific calls tonight." OFA's training was explicit that developing leaders "requires delegating responsibility (as opposed to tasks)."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ownership of an outcome engages judgment, motivation, and local knowledge in a way that task-assignment never does; it makes the work the person's own. Joy Cushman warned that people who copy only the team structure — "just giving people call goals or door knock goals" — find "it just doesn't work," because the goal without genuine ownership is hollow [A3.2].
- **Example in context:** Regional field director Kate Cummings told her organizers "they were the field director for their turf" — meaning they owned building the operation that produced the results, not the doorknocking itself. Colorado's Gabe Lifton-Zoline grounded it bluntly: "This is your subsection of it. This is your community."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When work piles onto a few people, the cause is usually that the leader has delegated tasks (which keep flowing back) instead of outcomes (which a person can run with). Delegating outcomes is the practical move that breaks the bottleneck and is what made teams able to "run their own operation" in the final days.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (why outcomes motivate); avoiding-founder-bottleneck [A9.3] (the problem this solves); goal-design [A5.3] (what makes an outcome delegable).

### The "leader in the middle" trap
concepts:    [A9.3 avoiding-founder-bottleneck]
situations:  [S:doing-everything-myself] [S:i-cant-let-go]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Don't become the single person everyone routes through. OFA's training showed this as a diagram of one central figure with every arrow pointing inward, and asked pointedly what happens "if the 'leader' in the middle drops out?" The recommendation is to distribute responsibility across interdependent roles so the group does not depend on you alone.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A hub-and-spoke leader excludes other voices, reduces the team's output to one person's capacity, and creates a single point of failure. Interdependence — where each role needs the others — both spreads the load and binds the team together, without requiring the leader to coordinate every interaction [A5.4].
- **Example in context:** The training contrasted two failures — a leaderless ring working at cross-purposes (Figure 5.1) and the overloaded leader-in-the-middle (Figure 5.2) — against the snowflake, where a team leader anchors a small team but core members own distinct, interdependent jobs.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Doing everything myself" and "I can't let go" are the same problem seen from two angles, and both end the same way: burnout and a group that collapses when the leader steps back. The whole neighborhood-team design exists to hold "teams — rather than a single heroic leader — accountable."
- **Related concepts:** delegate-outcomes [A9.2] (how to off-load); functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (the alternative shape); volunteer-burnout [A10.1] (the cost of staying the hub).

### Leadership as a set of functions, not a title
concepts:    [A9.5 functional-shared-leadership]
situations:  [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Think of leadership as a bundle of jobs the team needs done — recruiting, running phone banks, managing data, coordinating canvasses — met by whoever can do them, rather than concentrated in one title. A "developed" team has a leader plus core members each owning a distinct function.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Interdependent roles mean each person has a unique job and "no one person could get her work done without coordinating with others." That interdependence is what produces both coordination and commitment: titles track real contribution, not status. As NTL Cheryl Ellis put it, people don't "have titles just to have titles."
- **Example in context:** A fully developed OFA team had a neighborhood-team leader anchoring a canvass captain, a phone-bank captain, a data captain, and a volunteer coordinator, each with their own volunteers. Mary Pizzariello — a computer-handy working mom — was asked to be the team's data captain, a defined function matched to her actual aptitude.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Distributing named functions is how a leader gets work off their own plate honestly: each function is somebody's, and the team can lose any one person without losing the whole. This functional structure is what let 5,171 volunteer leaders run GOTV staging locations on Election Day without staff.
- **Related concepts:** work-and-task-design [A5.4] (designing the functions); composition-and-skills [A5.1] (matching people to them); the-leader-in-the-middle trap [A9.3] (what functions prevent).

### Public narrative: story of self, us, and now
concepts:    [A2.2 public-narrative]
situations:  [S:group-lost-its-why]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Lead with story, not talking points. Marshall Ganz's "public narrative" practice asks a leader to tell a story of *self* (why I'm called to this), *us* (the shared values that bind this group), and *now* (the urgent choice we face). A first-time leader should learn to share their own story and invite others to share theirs, rather than reciting issues or "quoting commercials."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Story moves people to action by connecting values to a choice; it is how a leader makes the group's purpose felt rather than merely stated [A2.1]. It also does relational work — exchanging stories is how a one-on-one builds a real public relationship [A4.2].
- **Example in context:** At the first Camp Obama in Burbank (2007), volunteer Susan Christopher described arriving afraid "to pick a losing cause — again," then watching strangers across faiths and generations share their stories until "I felt my heart softening again." She left committed not because of a policy argument but because of a shared narrative.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When a group has "lost its why," the repair is usually narrative, not logistics. OFA built public narrative into its core training precisely because, as field organizer Jenni Boyle-Smith put it, the "human element" connects people "on a real level versus just repeating talking points."
- **Related concepts:** shared-purpose [A2.1] (what story carries); the-1:1 [A4.2] (where stories are exchanged); values-as-glue [A2.3] (the "us" the story names).

### "Respect, empower, include": values as the team's glue
concepts:    [A2.3 values-as-glue] [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Name and live a short set of shared values, and let them define how the group treats people. OFA's three-word motto — "respect, empower, include," credited to Iowa's Paul Tewes — was posted in every office and handed to everyone "from the field director on down." A leader should make the group's values explicit and visible, not assumed.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Shared values are the cohesive force that makes a group feel like an "us" rather than a transaction; they tell members they are valued as whole people, which is itself a source of motivation and belonging [A3.3]. Deidre Reynolds contrasted OFA with campaigns where volunteers "never felt like they were part of anything."
- **Example in context:** Steele recalled the words "respect, empower, include" penned on the wall of every OFA office he ever worked in, including one volunteer's converted dining-room "war room." The motto functioned as a constant, portable statement of how the group operated.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that "feels transactional" is usually one whose values are unstated, so people experience it as extraction. Making values concrete and repeated is low-cost and was central to the social-movement *feeling* that distinguished OFA, "especially in 2008."
- **Related concepts:** group-identity-and-belonging [A2.4] (what values produce); transactional-vs-relational-ask [A3.3] (the alternative they guard against); how-norms-become-culture [A6.1] (how a posted value hardens into practice).

### Commitment to each other outlasts commitment to the cause
concepts:    [A3.4 commitment-over-time] [A2.4 group-identity-and-belonging]
situations:  [S:low-attendance] [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Build the bonds *between members*, because that, more than enthusiasm for the cause, is what keeps people coming back. People may join for the issue or the candidate, but they stay — through tedium and hard weeks — because they're committed to the people on their team. So invest deliberately in the team as a set of relationships, not just a unit of production.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Researchers find that organizing people into teams makes them committed "not only to the cause, but also to each other." Affect toward a candidate fades; interpersonal obligation is stickier and shows up exactly when motivation would otherwise flag [A4.1].
- **Example in context:** A third of respondents cited admiration for Obama as why they first showed up, but *none* said his personality sustained them through 90-hour weeks; what carried them was their teammates. Laura Diviney's North Carolina team refused to hold its party until her hospitalized husband came home.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Low and erratic attendance is often a relationship problem dressed as a logistics problem: people skip a phone bank for a cause more easily than they skip teammates who'd notice. The endurance of OFA's volunteers — 95% of NTLs and 75% of volunteers kept volunteering afterward — tracks the depth of these ties.
- **Related concepts:** relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (the underlying claim); cohesion-and-belonging [A6.3] (the felt result); ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (the other pillar of staying).

### Relational self-interest: motivate from interests, don't extract
concepts:    [A3.3 transactional-vs-relational-ask]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:scared-to-ask-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Ground every ask in the person's own interests and relationships, not in what you need from them. The one-on-one exists to get someone's "real interests… on the table" so the ask fits who they are. The thing to avoid is treating people as resources to be extracted — "money, time, whatever."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The IAF teaches that the Latin root of *interest*, *interesse*, means "to be among or between" — so self-interest is relational, formed in the context of others, not selfish. An ask matched to a person's genuine interests "will lead to action"; an extractive ask produces, at best, grudging compliance that doesn't last [A3.4].
- **Example in context:** Ganz taught at a 2007 Camp Obama that the one-on-one is not about extracting resources. In practice, an organizer would ask a minister to host a house meeting at her church, or a member of a Thursday bridge group to run a post-game phone bank — the ask shaped to the life the person already had.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Leaders who feel "scared to ask" usually picture the ask as extraction, which is exactly what makes it feel uncomfortable and what makes groups feel transactional. Reframing the ask as meeting the person's real interest both eases the leader's fear and produces durable commitment.
- **Related concepts:** the-ask / escalating-commitment [A3.5] (the ask itself, routed below); the-1:1 [A4.2] (where interests surface); fear-of-asking [A1.5] (the leader's block).

### Ownership and voice: people commit to what they help shape
concepts:    [A3.2 ownership-and-voice]
situations:  [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Give members a real stake — a piece of the work that is theirs, and a voice in how it's done — and motivation follows. Don't hand out activity for its own sake; hand out ownership of outcomes that matter, and let people feel they own their subset of the larger goal.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Ownership converts external goals into internal motivation: when work is "yours," effort becomes self-directed. NTL Rick Baer, drawing on his union-organizing past, noted people have to "take ownership of the campaign" or "you're not probably going to win." The mechanism is the felt sense of being "part of something bigger," not "an individual cog in a machine" [A7.5].
- **Example in context:** Reflection-chapter leaders repeatedly tied hours to ownership: feeling they "had a stake," that "their voice was heard… and valued." Clinton Thomas's volunteers, given ownership, "were willing to put in 10, 15, 20 hours a week there at the end."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group where "no one takes initiative" usually has no one who *owns* anything — initiative is what ownership produces. Ownership was also, in the authors' account, half of why the campaign was transformative for volunteers, not just effective.
- **Related concepts:** delegate-outcomes [A9.2] (how ownership is conferred); goal-design [A5.3] (the object owned); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (voice in the work).

### The ask that leads to action
concepts:    [A3.5 the-ask / escalating-commitment]
situations:  [S:scared-to-ask-people]
handoff:     [H:recruitment]
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Making a clear ask — and escalating it over time — is the act that converts a sympathizer into a committed leader. The understanding to hold here is *why* the ask works and what makes it land; the tactics, scripts, and channels of recruitment belong to the recruitment coach. (Route how-to → H:recruitment.)
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Commitment builds in rungs: a small, concrete ask that the person can succeed at, then a larger one, each tested by follow-through. The ask must be specific and matched to the person's interests, or it doesn't "lead to action, to… commitments to *do something*." Escalation is how a relationship turns into responsibility [A3.4].
- **Example in context:** The standard OFA cycle was: hold a one-on-one, ask the person to host a house meeting or lead a voter-contact event, watch whether they followed through, then ask them to take a leadership role. Some, like Howard Dolginoff, needed only the softest ask ("What took you so long?"); most needed the staged escalation.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Fear of asking is one of the most common first-timer blocks, and it quietly caps a group's growth, because un-asked people stay passive. OFA treated the *hard ask* as a learnable craft, and made recruitment calls (asks) one of its two key health metrics.
- **Related concepts:** relational-ask [A3.3] (the spirit of it); fear-of-asking [A1.5] (the block); developing-leaders [A9.1] (what escalation builds toward).

### Relationships are the strategy
concepts:    [A4.1 relationships-are-strategy]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional] [S:low-attendance]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat relationship-building as the actual work of leading, not a warm-up to it. In OFA's model, "if [an organizer] can't build relationships, they're not going to be successful no matter how many calls they make." The recommendation: budget real time for getting to know people as people, and resist the pull to skip straight to production.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Relationships were the conduit through which everything else flowed — staff motivated volunteers, volunteers motivated voters — operating at three levels (staff-to-staff, organizer-to-volunteer, volunteer-to-voter). Without the relationship beneath a number, Jeremy Bird observed, "it's just a number in a spreadsheet," and the durable outcome is "not as good" [A3.4].
- **Example in context:** Alex Steele lived in Waukon, Iowa (population ~4,000), ate and worked in the community, and could say on the phone, "I'm right here in Waukon. I live on such-and-such a street." When caucus time came, voters answered not "some Obama staffer" but "Yes, Alex. I'm ready."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A transactional, thinly-attended group is the predictable result of skipping relational investment. The hard evidence: 75% of interviewees credited their sustained hours to a personal relationship or the campaign's human presence — relationships were measurably the load-bearing strategy.
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1 [A4.2] (the core practice); trust-building [A4.3] (what relationships create); commitment-over-time [A3.4] (what they sustain).

### The one-on-one
concepts:    [A4.2 the-1:1-relational-meeting]
situations:  [S:group-feels-transactional]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Make the intentional one-to-one meeting your foundational practice. It is not small talk: it's a "directed and disciplined" conversation with a loose agenda — exchange personal stories, connect them to why this work matters, and make an ask matched to the person's interests and talents. A new leader should be holding these regularly, not waiting for people to volunteer.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The one-on-one is "the most basic tool of organizing" because it builds a *public* relationship — a working bond oriented to shared action — and because it surfaces the person's real interests, strengths, and social connections, which is what lets the leader make an ask that sticks [A3.3].
- **Example in context:** Colorado NTL Pat Bruner grew her team from three to fifteen by holding one-on-ones with "eight to ten people a week"; in an isolated, conservative area they ran one to two hours each — one stretched to four. The campaign's two best capacity indicators were recruitment calls made and how many became one-on-ones.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** For a first-time leader, the one-on-one is the single highest-leverage habit: it is simultaneously recruitment, motivation, leadership development, and relationship-building. It is also nearly free — one of the practices that, the authors note, costs almost nothing after fixed staffing.
- **Related concepts:** public-narrative [A2.2] (the story exchange inside it); the-ask [A3.5] (its action step); developing-leaders [A9.1] (what it sets in motion).

### Trust as the foundation
concepts:    [A4.3 trust-building]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Trust your people with real responsibility — and earn their trust by investing in them first. OFA's foundational learning was that volunteers, taken seriously and well-trained, "would show up and could produce real results." A leader who withholds responsibility because volunteers seem unreliable usually gets the unreliability they expect.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Trust is built inside the relationship and then underwrites delegation: a leader who knows their members as people can confidently hand off outcomes, and members who feel invested in reciprocate with follow-through. OFA's training was explicit that leaders "must be able to trust them to delegate responsibility… and to follow through on commitments" [A9.2].
- **Example in context:** Florida's Howard Dolginoff developed "a relationship of trust and respect" with his organizer Stan, which "facilitated his commitment." The deeper lesson came from Iowa, where, against the cautionary memory of Dean's 2004 collapse, OFA learned that well-trained "nontraditional, local volunteers will deliver."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Flaky" volunteers are often a symptom of low trust and low investment, not fixed character. OFA staked two presidential campaigns on the wager that trusted, developed volunteers could do the work of paid staff — and won twice on it.
- **Related concepts:** peer-accountability [A8.3] (trust plus follow-through); delegate-outcomes [A9.2] (what trust enables); commitment-over-time [A3.4] (its longer arc).

### Recruiting as relationship, not transaction
concepts:    [A4.5 recruiting-as-relational]
situations:  [S:cant-recruit]
handoff:     [H:recruitment]
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.4
- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand recruitment as relationship-building, not list-working. New members come through a one-on-one and an interest-matched ask, not a cold pitch. The principle lives here; the channels, scripts, and outreach tactics belong to the recruitment coach. (Route how-to → H:recruitment.)
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A recruit who joins through a relationship arrives already partly committed and partly known, so the leader can place them well and they're likelier to stay [A3.4]. Recruiting relationally also seeds the next generation: a well-recruited member learns to recruit the same way, producing the self-replicating growth organizing depends on [A9.1].
- **Example in context:** Organizers began with a data-generated list of likely supporters, but the list only opened the door — capacity grew once the organizer "had a network of relationships in the community" and used those ties to find and vet new people the database never surfaced.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't recruit" is often "recruiting transactionally" — cold asks to strangers, which convert poorly and don't retain. The relational approach is slower at first but, as Stewart described, yields "explosive" growth where the transactional model "plateaus really quickly."
- **Related concepts:** the-1:1 [A4.2] (the recruitment medium); the-relational-ask [A3.3] (its spirit); developing-leaders [A9.1] (recruitment's true aim).

### The snowflake: nested small teams
concepts:    [A5.2 group-size-and-nesting]
situations:  [S:group-too-big-or-chaotic]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Organize work into small teams — a leader plus a handful of core members — and nest those small teams into the larger effort, rather than running one big undifferentiated group. The unit of real work is the small team; scale comes from linking many of them, not from enlarging one.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** The "snowflake" — co-developed by Ganz and organizational scholar Ruth Wageman for the Sierra Club — links people in interdependent roles so a team can work toward a shared goal without overburdening anyone [A5.4]. Small size keeps coordination tractable and relationships real; nesting (field organizer → several team leaders → their core members) lets the model reach national scale while staying hyper-local.
- **Example in context:** A field organizer typically anchored two to seven neighborhood teams, each with its own leader as the "nucleus." The whole 2012 operation was ~10,000 such teams and ~30,000 core members — scale built entirely out of small, interlocking units, not big crowds.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A group that feels "too big or chaotic" usually needs subdividing into nested teams, not better crowd-control. OFA's structural innovation was precisely this: the people called it "the team model," and field leaders judged the model's success on whether small teams could run autonomously.
- **Related concepts:** work-and-task-design [A5.4] (the roles inside a team); functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (how the team divides work); stages-and-development [A6.2] (how a team matures).
- **Source reach:** Groundbreakers vividly describes the small-team-and-nesting design but gives no theory of *optimal* size; for the "why small teams" mechanism (coordination cost, decision quality) the coach should lean on a structure source like Fisher and attribute size-numbers lightly here.

### Interdependent roles
concepts:    [A5.4 work-and-task-design]
situations:  [S:work-is-all-on-a-few-people]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Design the team so each member owns a distinct, real job that the others depend on. Don't leave roles vague or let everything default to the leader. A developed team has named functions — someone runs canvasses, someone runs phone banks, someone owns the data, someone coordinates volunteers — and each is genuinely needed by the rest.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Interdependence is the engine: when "no one person could get her work done without coordinating with others," the structure itself produces coordination, clarity, and mutual commitment, without the leader brokering every interaction [A9.5]. It also distributes load so the team isn't one person plus spectators.
- **Example in context:** OFA's fully developed team placed a canvass captain, phone-bank captain, data captain, and volunteer coordinator around the team leader, each with their own volunteers. Mary Pizzariello's role as data captain — entering the team's numbers after every event — was a defined, depended-upon function, not a vague "help out."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** When work lands on a few people, it's usually because roles were never designed; interdependent role design is the structural fix. This design is what let teams "work collectively" and, ultimately, run Election-Day staging locations without staff.
- **Related concepts:** functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (roles as distributed leadership); composition-and-skills [A5.1] (who fills them); goal-design [A5.3] (what the roles serve).

### Goals that are clear, vivid, and grounded
concepts:    [A5.3 goal-design]
situations:  [S:unclear-what-were-doing]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes; Direction on the what, autonomy on the how
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Give the team concrete, consequential goals that members can see themselves in — and explain where the goals come from. Vague aspiration doesn't coordinate or motivate; a clear target the team owns does both. But goals alone aren't magic: "what matters is how they are used."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A well-designed goal does triple duty — it confers ownership [A3.2], coordinates effort, and makes accountability legible. Grounding the goal in real reasoning ("you have to get to 50 plus 1 percent, and you know how many votes you need") turns a number from an arbitrary demand into something members can buy into, especially when paired with evidence it works.
- **Example in context:** Regional field director Staci O'Brien told volunteers the campaign "all going to end at 8:00 p.m. on Election Day" and walked them through the vote math, so the goals "were very grounded in reality." Florida's Austin Brookley found that giving volunteers the evidence behind goals made them "a lot easier to buy into."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Unclear what we're doing" is a goal-design failure, and it quietly drains motivation and coordination alike. OFA's mantra "people-focused but metrics-driven" captured the balance: clear numbers, but in service of people who understand and own them.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (goals as motivators); decisions-as-shared-process [A7.2] (goals as collective); peer-accountability [A8.3] (goals as the basis for holding each other).

### The launch — and Camp Obama as relaunch
concepts:    [A5.7 launch-and-relaunch]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:     [H:events]
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat the first real gathering as a deliberate launch that both teaches skills *and* sets up the structure — not just a kickoff rally. The understanding to keep here is *why* a strong, structure-building launch matters; the mechanics of running the event belong to the events coach. (Route how-to → H:events.)
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A launch that builds relationships and stands up the team structure creates the capacity that later production draws on; one that only generates enthusiasm produces a crowd that dissipates. Buffy Wicks's insight at the first Camp Obama was to do both at once — teach organizing skills *and* "set up a campaign structure, too" [A5.2].
- **Example in context:** Camp Obama (Burbank, July 2007) gathered several hundred supporters for three days of public narrative and team formation — improvised, even "BYOC: Bring your own chair." It became the institutionalized launch point for neighborhood teams and was contrasted with Dean's "Deaniacs," whose enthusiasm "did not translate into results."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Losing momentum after launch is the classic failure of an energy-only kickoff. The fix is a launch that converts energy into structure and relationships; OFA's whole "build slowly, then peak" rhythm depended on it.
- **Related concepts:** the-snowflake [A5.2] (what a launch stands up); public-narrative [A2.2] (the launch's emotional engine); common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (the momentum loss this prevents).

### The five phases of a team's development
concepts:    [A6.2 stages-and-development]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Expect a team to move through recognizable phases, and lead it differently at each. OFA's training (developed by national training director Sara El-Amine) named five: (1) *potential* — a list of supporters, no relationships yet; (2) *forming* — early growth, maybe one confirmed leader; (3) *functioning* — a confirmed leader but incomplete core; (4) *developed* — an experienced leader plus a full core, the most productive phase; (5) *splitting* — a mature team divides to seed new ones.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Naming the phases tells a leader what to work on now (relationships early, role-filling next, mentoring others late) and prevents misreading a normal early phase as failure. A developed team can "help mentor new teams," so the phases also describe how capacity replicates [A9.1].
- **Example in context:** A team in Nashua was so fully developed that its leader traveled the state mentoring new team leaders, "because she understood her role so well." The fifth phase — splitting — is what scaling looked like from inside a single team.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Momentum loss is often a phase being misread — a leader expecting phase-4 production from a phase-2 team, or failing to mentor outward from a phase-4 team. The framework gives a first-timer a map of what "normal" looks like over time.
- **Related concepts:** launch-and-relaunch [A5.7] (the start of the arc); common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (phase mismatches); developing-leaders [A9.1] (the late-phase work).
- **Source reach:** This is OFA's own internal training schema, not a validated developmental model; present it as a practical map ("OFA's teams tended to pass through…"), not as established group-development theory.

### Learn by experimenting; failure is information
concepts:    [A6.6 learning-together] [A1.6 failure-as-learning]
situations:
handoff:
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3
- **Claim / recommendation:** Treat your group as a place to try things, fail, reflect, and try again — and protect that experimental spirit deliberately. The whole OFA model was not designed up front; it "emerged not through premeditated planning, but… by an iterative process of trying new things, learning from mistakes, and trying again."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A culture of learning is what makes adaptation and real innovation possible; the alternative is staff who "would have just been told what to do." 2008 national field director Jon Carson built "a culture of learning and experimenting" — patient, and willing to scale up whatever proved to work. The risk, per Joy Cushman, is that a working model can ossify into "ideology" and "lose a lot of value" [A6.4].
- **Example in context:** A month into the South Carolina pilot, Bird lost his nerve — "It's not working… We've got to blow this up." Cushman counseled patience in what she called a "recommitment one-on-one," reconnecting him to *why* he started; the team held, and the model proved out. Wicks dubbed the early campaign a "petri dish."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader who treats every stumble as proof they're failing will quit or freeze; one who treats setbacks as data keeps adapting. OFA's central innovations all came out of failure and revision — the experimental stance was the precondition for everything else.
- **Related concepts:** the-recommitment-conversation / repair [A8.5] (recovering from a low point); common-stall-patterns [A6.4] (the ossification risk); resilience-through-setbacks [A10.5] (holding on through failure).

### How "respect, empower, include" becomes culture
concepts:    [A6.1 how-norms-form-and-become-culture]
situations:
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Set the norms you want early and repeat them relentlessly, because what a leader models and insists on hardens into "how we do things here." OFA's values weren't left implicit — they were named, posted, and handed to every newcomer, so they became the group's actual operating culture rather than a slogan.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Norms form through repetition and modeling; a value that's stated once is forgotten, while one that's visible and consistently enacted becomes self-enforcing among members [A2.3]. Ellen Gangnon's vow to "never ask people to do more than what I would do myself" — trudging door-to-door in Wisconsin blizzards — is norm-setting by example [A1.3].
- **Example in context:** "Respect, empower, include" appeared on the wall of "every [OFA] office," handed to everyone from field director to team leader. Its constant presence is what turned three words into a recognizable, portable culture across thousands of independent local teams.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader sets culture whether or not they mean to; the choice is whether to do it deliberately. OFA's social-movement *feel* — what made volunteers say they'd never go back to the old county-party experience — was a culture built from consistently enacted norms.
- **Related concepts:** values-as-glue [A2.3] (the content of the norms); lead-by-example [A1.3] (the main mechanism); psychological-safety [A6.5] (a norm worth setting — though the source is largely silent on it).

### Direction on the *what*, autonomy on the *how*
concepts:    [A7.3 autonomy-vs-direction]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions] [S:no-one-takes-initiative]
handoff:
anchors to:  Direction on the what, autonomy on the how
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Be clear and firm about the goal; be generous and hands-off about how the team meets it. The OFA model "worked most successfully in places where teams bought into the goals the national campaign developed, but had local autonomy in figuring out how to implement them." Don't dictate methods; dictate the destination and trust local knowledge for the route.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Autonomy on the *how* is what activates members' local knowledge and produces ownership; direction on the *what* is what keeps many autonomous teams pulling the same way. Remove the autonomy — by over-staffing or micromanaging — and you get the "more staff, less ownership" collapse, where volunteers' roles "contract" and motivation drains [A3.2].
- **Example in context:** NTL Ann Cherry credited a "strategy that allowed us to develop the tactics on a local level… because it depended on our knowledge of our area." When 2012 over-staffing pushed organizers to do tasks "themselves because they can," veteran volunteers like Ellen Gangnon felt their roles shrink and their contact with teams "diminished greatly."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Can't make decisions" and "no one takes initiative" are often over-direction in disguise — a team given no room to decide *how* won't decide anything. Calibrating this balance was, the authors conclude, one of the key strategic challenges of the whole model.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (what autonomy produces); just-enough-hierarchy [A7.6] (how much direction); collective-agreement-tension [A7.5] (the limit of autonomy).

### Goals are collective, not individual
concepts:    [A7.5 collective-agreement-tension] [A7.2 decisions-as-shared-process]
situations:  [S:cant-make-decisions]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.6, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Frame goals as the team's shared responsibility, so that members are accountable to each other and not just to the leader. In OFA, "the goals were collective" even though each role was distinct — meeting your piece depended on others meeting theirs, and vice versa.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Collective goals turn "individual resources into collective power" and make people feel "part of something bigger" rather than "an individual cog in a machine." Because each leader could only meet their goal by supporting their direct reports in meeting theirs, the structure built mutual dependence into the numbers themselves [A8.3].
- **Example in context:** Field organizers were responsible for *all* the voter contact in their turf, regional directors for their whole region, and so on — so meeting a goal *required* developing the people below you, not out-producing them. Lifton-Zoline framed it as "we're all doing the same thing."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A team that "can't make decisions" or relitigates them often lacks a shared goal everyone has agreed to own; collective goals give decisions a common reference point. This collective framing was, the authors argue, part of why the work felt transformative rather than extractive.
- **Related concepts:** peer-accountability [A8.3] (the flip side); ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (collective ownership); autonomy-vs-direction [A7.3] (agreed goal, free method).

### Power comes from people, not from money or message
concepts:    [A7.1 building-and-using-power]
situations:
handoff:
anchors to:  Organize, don't mobilize
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.1, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Understand that a group's power is built by developing people and bringing them together to act, not by raising more money or crafting a better message. For a volunteer leader with no budget, this is good news: your real lever is the capacity and commitment of the people you develop.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Community organizing "develops power within individuals, organizations, and communities" — organizers "build power by developing the leadership capacities of individuals, and bringing those individuals together to engage strategically in collective action." Power is a product of organized relationships, which is why it's available to the under-resourced [A4.1].
- **Example in context:** Jonathan Alter observed that "the Koch brothers couldn't just buy a first-class field organization from afar; it had to be painstakingly built at the local level." Even powerful data was "most meaningful in the hands of local organizers" who knew their communities — money couldn't substitute for organized people.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A first-time leader often assumes they're powerless without resources; the organizing tradition says the opposite — people, organized, *are* the resource. The authors note the practices are "virtually free of cost after fixed outlays," so this power is genuinely accessible to small efforts.
- **Related concepts:** relationships-are-strategy [A4.1] (power's medium); developing-leaders [A9.1] (power-building work); ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (felt power).

### Just-enough hierarchy: coordination without overburdening
concepts:    [A7.6 hierarchy-just-enough]
situations:  [S:group-too-big-or-chaotic]
handoff:
anchors to:  Structure makes shared work possible
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold exactly as much structure and clear authority as coordination requires — no more, no less. OFA rejected both the leaderless ring (no one resolves impasses or "takes ultimate responsibility for the outcome") and the all-on-one-person model. Someone must be accountable for the whole; everyone else owns a real, interdependent part.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Without anyone coordinating, "the team cannot produce strategic results"; with everything on one person, "many voices are excluded and team performance can be reduced to a single person's efforts." The snowflake threads the needle — a clear leader for coordination, distributed roles for capacity [A5.4].
- **Example in context:** OFA's training literally diagrammed the two failures and the solution: a structure that gives "the coordination and clarity necessary… without overburdening any one person." The leader's success was defined not by their own output but by getting the team "to work autonomously."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Both "chaotic, no one's in charge" and "everything runs through me" are hierarchy mis-calibrations. OFA's "rigid yet transparent structure" — light but real — is widely credited as why it out-organized rivals; even GOP strategists conceded it.
- **Related concepts:** the-snowflake [A5.2] (the calibrated form); functional-shared-leadership [A9.5] (distributed authority); the-leader-in-the-middle trap [A9.3] (too little distribution).

### Peer accountability through shared, visible goals
concepts:    [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:volunteers-flaky] [S:decisions-keep-getting-relitigated]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.5, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Hold people accountable through shared, visible goals and mutual dependence — not through bossing, which a volunteer leader has no standing to do anyway. Make each person's piece known to the team, and accountability becomes peer-to-peer: people follow through because their teammates are counting on them.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Metrics made each person's responsibility "visible to everyone," so commitments were owed to the group, not extracted by a superior [A7.5]. Crucially, accountability must come *with* support: "using metrics to hold people accountable without providing them with the technical, emotional, and skills-based support… can be more destructive than constructive."
- **Example in context:** Jenni Boyle-Smith found that when "volunteers knew what my goals were, they wanted to help"; sharing goals downward turned her targets into the team's shared business. Bird's rule: "You need to have volunteers who know their goals."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** "Flaky" volunteers and endlessly relitigated commitments are often accountability vacuums — nobody's responsibility is visible or shared. Shared goals plus support is how peers hold each other without anyone being "the boss," which is exactly a volunteer leader's situation.
- **Related concepts:** goals-are-collective [A7.5] (the basis); goal-design [A5.3] (what's shared); numbers-without-support [A10.6] (the failure mode to avoid).

### The recommitment conversation: repair through *why*
concepts:    [A8.5 repair-and-mutual-accountability]
situations:  [S:lost-momentum-after-launch]
handoff:
anchors to:  Relationships are the strategy
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.3
- **Claim / recommendation:** When someone (including yourself) hits a crisis of confidence and wants to quit or "blow it up," the repair move is to reconnect them to *why* they started — not to argue the tactics. Organizers had a name for this: the "recommitment one-on-one."
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** A low point is usually a loss of purpose-contact, not a real verdict on the work; returning to the originating motivation restores the will to push through the messy middle [A2.1]. Because the relationship is the medium, repair happens *in* a one-on-one [A4.2].
- **Example in context:** A month into the South Carolina pilot, a discouraged Bird told Cushman "It's not working… We've got to blow this up." Cushman didn't debate the numbers; she asked, "Why did you do this in the first place, and what do you want out of this?" — reconnecting him to his deeper aim. The model survived and proved out.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Every group hits a trough where someone wants out; a leader who knows how to hold a recommitment conversation can save people and momentum that would otherwise be lost. This single conversation arguably saved the team model itself.
- **Related concepts:** shared-purpose [A2.1] (what recommitment returns to); the-1:1 [A4.2] (the repair medium); failure-as-learning [A1.6] (reframing the low point).
- **Source reach:** The book gives one strong instance and a name, not a method; present it as a stance and a question to ask, not a formal repair protocol. The register's broader A8.5 (repairing interpersonal ruptures between members) is *not* covered by this source — see Coverage gaps.

### People-focused *and* metrics-driven: don't let numbers crowd out care
concepts:    [A10.6 satisfaction-and-retention] [A8.3 peer-accountability-without-authority]
situations:  [S:burning-out]
handoff:
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.6
- **Claim / recommendation:** Track what matters, but never let the metric become the point. OFA's mantra was "people-focused but metrics-driven" — in that order. A leader should use numbers to coordinate and motivate, while watching for the moment metrics start to flatten people into outputs and demotivate them.
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Reducing rich human work to comparable numbers (what scholars Espeland and Stevens call *commensuration* — to "reduce and simplify disparate information into numbers that can be easily compared") is necessary for coordination but erases real differences: a one-on-one might be a careful 45-minute relationship-build or a "brisk, 15-minute chat," counted the same. Over-weighting results "at the expense of the process" frustrates and demotivates the very people the work depends on [A3.2].
- **Example in context:** In Allentown, NTL teams doing painstaking list-scrubbing showed "low" door-knock numbers in the database; their leader had to advocate upward, insisting "it's more important that we win" than that the metric look tidy. Pressed too hard, volunteers like the chocolate-factory worker could only say, "I can't do this."
- **Why it matters / evidence:** Members burn out and drift when they feel measured but not cared for. Satisfaction is, in effect, half of a group's health and the driver of who stays — so protecting the "people-focused" half of the mantra is retention strategy, not softness.
- **Related concepts:** ownership-and-voice [A3.2] (what numbers should serve); peer-accountability [A8.3] (numbers used well); volunteer-burnout [A10.1] (the cost of getting it wrong).

source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Mark wins and recognize contribution out loud and often. Build small celebrations into the rhythm of the work, and let recognition be specific and shared. It costs little and feeds the relationships that sustain effort.

### Who can afford to lead: the inequality of time
concepts:    [A5.1 composition-and-skills] [A10.1 volunteer-burnout]
situations:  [S:cant-recruit]
handoff:     [H:org-level]
anchors to:  Give people real responsibility for real outcomes
source:      [McKenna & Han](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/groundbreakers-9780199394609?cc=us&lang=en&) — Groundbreakers, ch.7
- **Claim / recommendation:** Recognize that the demanding team-leader role — 30+ hours a week — is not equally available to everyone, and don't read someone's limited time as limited commitment. A first-time leader should match asks to people's real constraints and resist squeezing those who genuinely can't give more. Designing the *program* to lower these barriers is largely an org-level question. (Route program design → H:org-level.)
- **Reasoning / mechanism:** Volunteering capacity is shaped by class and life circumstance: people working unforgiving hours or multiple jobs can't pour in the time the model assumes, which skews who becomes a leader and can quietly narrow a team's composition and diversity [A4.6]. Pushing past a real limit doesn't build commitment — it breaks people.
- **Example in context:** A factory worker who processed chocolate bars all day would call her organizer on her 1:07 p.m. break, register people during it, then do OFA work after her shift — at her ceiling, with "no other hard ask" possible. In contrast, retired surgeon Harlan Kutscher hosted phone banks in his "big open contemporary house." Field director Greg Jackson found a low-income community could be "fruitful in support" but not in volunteer hours.
- **Why it matters / evidence:** A leader who treats time-constrained members as flaky will lose exactly the diverse voices the group needs. The authors frame the real challenge as keeping the commitment to developing leaders "among a diverse set of voices" who can shape the work to fit their own circumstances.
