## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

**Organize, don't mobilize.** *(mobilizing-vs-organizing lens)* Build people's capacity and relationships so they can act for themselves, rather than extracting one-off action from them. It holds because developing a leader compounds — that leader recruits and trains others — while mobilizing produces a number and then evaporates; the slow early investment is what makes the later peak explosive.
flagship example: Wisconsin field director Michelle Kleppe taught new staff the difference between organizing and "slash-and-burn" mobilizing, and protected what she called "the space in between" the mobilizing peaks for the real organizing work of finding and developing leaders.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.4
governs: [A2.5] [A3.3] [A4.1] [A9.1] [A1.2]

**Never do for people what they can do for themselves (enable, don't do).** The leader's job is to set conditions and build others' capacity, not to perform the work. A leader who does the work caps the group at their own hours and energy; a leader who develops others multiplies what the group can do and frees themselves to lead.
flagship example: When a regional director balked that Alex Watters — a quadriplegic applicant who couldn't knock doors — couldn't do the job, Zack Davis answered that he wasn't hiring a doorknocker: "I need an organizer." Watters built one of the largest volunteer teams in Iowa.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.1, ch.4
governs: [A1.2] [A9.1] [A9.2] [A9.3]

**Relationships are the strategy, not the soft part.** People do long, thankless, unpaid work because of their commitment to each other, not because of the cause or the candidate alone. Relationships are the medium through which motivation, accountability, and capacity actually flow.
flagship example: Seventy-five percent of interviewees attributed the hours they gave to a personal relationship with someone on the campaign or to its human presence in their community — not to Obama himself. A North Carolina team postponed its New Year's party until leader Laura Diviney's hospitalized husband came home.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.4
governs: [A4.1] [A4.2] [A4.3] [A2.4] [A3.4]

**Structure makes shared work possible (the snowflake).** Without a structure of interdependent roles, a group either fragments (everyone pulling in different directions) or collapses onto one overloaded person. The right structure gives coordination and clarity without overburdening anyone, and nests small teams into something larger.
flagship example: OFA's training drew two failure diagrams from Marshall Ganz — a ring of people working at cross-purposes, and a single "leader in the middle" with every arrow pointing at them — and replaced both with the "snowflake": a field organizer linked to several neighborhood-team leaders, each the nucleus of their own small team.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.5
governs: [A5.2] [A5.4] [A9.5] [A7.6] [A9.3]

**Give people real responsibility for real outcomes.** People commit to what they own. Hand teams meaningful, measurable outcomes — not chores — and the ownership turns effort into identity and sustains it.
flagship example: Field organizer Clinton Thomas found that giving volunteers ownership of the campaign "made them feel like they owned it," so they put in 10, 15, 20 hours a week at the end. Skeptical NTL Staci O'Brien only became "a believer" after she watched turnout in her town rise 20% against single-digit gains nearby.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.5
governs: [A3.2] [A5.3] [A7.2] [A9.2] [A8.3]

**Direction on the *what*, autonomy on the *how*.** Hold people to a clear, shared goal; leave them free to figure out how to meet it locally. Buy-in to a common goal plus local autonomy is what lets organizing work at scale; over-direction kills the ownership that makes it work.
flagship example: NTL Ann Cherry described a campaign "strategy that allowed us to develop the tactics on a local level" — and credited that division for the program's success, since it ran on volunteers' knowledge of their own turf. The later "more staff, less ownership" problem showed the cost of swinging too far toward direction.
source: McKenna & Han — Groundbreakers, ch.5, ch.7
