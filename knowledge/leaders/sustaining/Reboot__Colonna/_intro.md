# _RAGSRC__ Reboot (Colonna) — Source Summary for the Group eCoach RAG

**Source:** Jerry Colonna, *Reboot: Leadership and the Art of Growing Up* (Harper Business, 2019)
**Register:** Group eCoach Tag Register (10 domains · 60 leaves · 22 situations · 6 handoffs)
**Audience:** first-time volunteer leader of a small group · **Gates applied:** leader-not-org · understanding-not-execution · minus-irrelevant
**Depth/quote policy:** verbatim quotes are short (well under 15 words), attributed, ~one per entry, used only where the exact wording carries the concept. Paraphrase otherwise; this is a retrieval aid, not a substitute for the book.

**Coverage note — read first:** Reboot is the corpus's **inner-game source** — the one book that asks not how to run a group but how to *face yourself* so you can lead it. Its thesis is that **better humans make better leaders**, and that the people we lead are the mirror in which we come to see ourselves. The whole book passes Gate 1 cleanly: it is about the individual leader's interior, never about org or program design, so it carries **no sibling-coach handoffs and effectively no `H:org-level`**. The gate that does the work here is altitude of a different kind. Colonna writes through CEO, founder, and investor stories, but the *inner work* — imposter feelings, the fear of asking, projecting your own patterns onto a difficult member, burnout, the pull to do everything yourself — transfers whole to a volunteer leading three to seven people. Per the owner's direction, this summary is deliberately reframed **away from the CEO role and toward facing ourselves to grow as leaders**, and leans its flagship examples on the **scale-free teaching stories** (Milarepa and the demons, the Crow, Grandfather Boulder, the warrior pose) rather than the executive case studies. Sized at the **band floor (~15%)**: the executive/finance machinery and the memoir's clinical detail are excluded heavily, but the inner-game seam is kept at full depth because **A1 and A10 are the two domains Boundary 2 keeps permanently and fully** — and this is *the* source for them. The floor is by design, not thinness: like the McAlevey doc, a large share of the book gates out and the relevant seam is rich rather than the whole.

**Register-gap flag — the inner-work cluster has no Axis A home.** Reboot's core concepts — **radical self-inquiry** (the method), **complicity** ("how am I complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"), the **shadow** and **projection**, **inherited family scripts** ("ghosts in the machine"), the **inner critic** ("the Crow"), and **equanimity** as the goal — have no clean leaf. A1's leaves describe *role* identity (earned authority, enabling-vs-doing, lead-by-example, imposter, fear-of-asking, failure-as-learning), not the deeper psychological self-work that is the entire book. These chunks are tagged to their nearest leaves — mostly **A1.4** (stepping-into-the-role / imposter-feelings), **A1.6** (failure-as-learning), **A10.2** (leader-self-care), **A10.5** (resilience-through-setbacks) — and **flagged**. **No codes were invented.** This is the strongest case in the corpus so far for an **A1 inner-work / self-awareness leaf**, and it sits squarely on the standing A1+A10 merge question. **MARK for the register pass; surfaced here, not resolved.** One consequence: **A1 and A10 carry by far the heaviest entry load in this doc** — genuine, not padding.

**Standing Source-reach caveat (applies doc-wide):** Colonna's business cases are pitched at the executive scale (a CEO being fired, a board pulling funding, a billion-dollar company). The *principle* transfers to a 3–7-person volunteer group; the *scale and stakes of the illustration* usually do not, and entries say so. Where possible the flagship example is the wisdom-tradition story, which carries no scale at all.

**Sensitive-content handling:** the book includes Colonna's own account of past self-harm and suicidal feeling. It is referenced here only at the why-level, where it carries the depletion-to-equanimity thread, **non-graphically and with no method detail**. The coach should treat it the same way: never reproduce or dwell on it; if a user surfaces anything similar about themselves, drop the teaching frame and respond to the person.

## Layer 0 — Foundational Principles

**Better humans make better leaders — and the group is the mirror.** Growing as a leader and growing up as a person are the same work; an unexamined leader's patterns leak into and are reflected back by the group they lead. Because we meet our own disowned material in the people around us, leading others is one of the few reliable ways to finally see ourselves — which is why facing yourself is not a detour from the work but the work itself.
flagship example: a leader who keeps complaining about the same kind of conflict in his people, until he sees that the trait that maddens him is the one he has refused to own in himself; the people are a funhouse mirror.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, intro & ch.7
governs: [A1.4] [A1.6] [A1.3] [A6.1] [A6.5] [A8.1]

**Radical self-inquiry: the why and the who, before the how.** There is no leadership playbook; the durable skill is knowing yourself well enough to face uncertainty and generate your own answer. Self-inquiry is the slow, compassionate practice of exposing self-deception until, as Colonna puts it, "no mask can hide us anymore" — and it is *radical* because it demands you stop blaming others for your life.
flagship example: Colonna pictures the Buddha as a frustrated man from Brooklyn who finally sits down under the tree and refuses to get up until things make sense; the breakthrough comes from sitting, not from a technique.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, intro & ch.1, ch.3
governs: [A1.4] [A1.2] [A1.6]

**Complicity.** We unconsciously co-create the very conditions we say we don't want; seeing our own hand in the problem is the move from blame to agency. The question — "how am I complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" — is the book's central diagnostic, and it is asked with compassion, not as self-punishment.
flagship example: a leader rages about a "greedy" person he himself hired and promoted; he had quietly outsourced his own fear of never being safe to someone more acceptable, and the hire was simply doing the job he was hired to do.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, ch.1
governs: [A1.6] [A1.4] [A8.1]

**The crucible.** The hardest moments — failure, loss, being let go, open conflict — are rites of passage that, faced rather than fled, forge a steadier leader. Facing the demon ("eat me if you wish") without collapsing into self-flagellation "turns up the heat," and without heat there is no alchemy.
flagship example: the saint Milarepa returns to a cave full of demons; waving them off and lecturing them fails, but asking "what are you here to teach me?" makes them vanish — all but one, into whose mouth he puts his own head, and which then disappears too.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, ch.2 (citing Warren Bennis)
governs: [A1.6] [A10.5] [A1.4]

**Strong back, open heart.** Lead with both firmness and vulnerability: the strong back of clarity, direction, and holding people accountable, and the open heart of caring, of admitting fear, of letting people in. Either one alone fails — firmness without heart becomes cold control, heart without a back collapses.
flagship example: Colonna has a leader stand in the warrior pose, feet planted, palms open; asked what he feels, the man says he is strong "but also exposed." That is the stance.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, ch.2 (Parker Palmer's "strong back, soft front")
governs: [A1.3] [A1.5] [A6.5] [A8.5]

**Resilience is the path; equanimity is the goal.** The aim is not to get better at absorbing blows but to find a steadiness that holds through them. Heartbreak, felt rather than fled, breaks the heart open, and that openness is the source of the steadiness — so the point is to learn to "appreciate the roller coaster from the ground," not to ride it forever.
flagship example: Grandfather Boulder, a rock face Colonna names on a desert fast, teaches him that "listening opens that which pain has closed" — steadiness comes from turning toward the pain, not away.
source:  Colonna — Reboot, ch.8, ch.1, afterword
governs: [A10.5] [A10.2] [A1.6]
