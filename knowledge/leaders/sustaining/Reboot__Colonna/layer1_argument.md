# Reboot — ## Layer 1 — The Overall Argument


**1. The formula: why over how.** Colonna opens with a whiteboard equation — practical skills, plus radical self-inquiry, plus sharing the experience with peers, equals enhanced leadership and greater resiliency. Everyone wants the *how*; he argues the *why* and the *who* are what actually let you face the endless, unscriptable hows. This frames the whole book as inner work, not technique.
concepts: [A1.4] [A1.2]

**2. Money and worth (ch.1).** Our earliest belief systems form in childhood and silently drive adult leadership; Colonna traces his own equation of money with safety and worth back to a Brooklyn childhood. The chapter introduces the book's central move — *complicity* — and the idea that the patterns we never examine end up running us.
concepts: [A1.4] [A1.6]

**3. The crucible and the warrior (ch.2).** Loss and failure are reframed as the forge of growth; the chapter gives the working tools — "take your seat," "living in the not-knowing," "strong back and open heart," and facing your demons. It sets up that the leader who moves through their crucible enables those around them to grow too.
concepts: [A1.6] [A1.4] [A1.3]

**4. Standing still (ch.3).** Before you can inquire, you have to stop; the chapter names busyness and over-doing as a flight from the self, and depletion as its cost. Slowing down is positioned as the precondition for every other move in the book.
concepts: [A10.2] [A10.1]

**5. Remembering who you are (ch.4).** We carry inherited family scripts — "ghosts in the machine" — and the chapter argues that authenticity and vulnerability are leadership acts: when you withhold the truth, people invent a worse story to fill the silence. It introduces "this being so, so what?" as the move toward facing reality.
concepts: [A1.4] [A6.5] [A1.6]

**6. The Irrational Other (ch.5).** Relationships are the hardest part of leading, and the difficult other is a mirror: we meet our own ghosts projected onto them. The chapter offers a concrete relational tool (OFNR) and ends in forgiveness — seeing the other as human lets us see ourselves the same way.
concepts: [A8.1] [A8.5] [A4.3]

**7. The pathless path (ch.6).** Purpose and meaning rarely arrive as a clean map; the chapter dismantles the "up and to the right" delusion and argues that feeling lost can be part of the path. Your *why* often hides in your own history.
concepts: [A2.1] [A1.4]

**8. The Crow and the shadow (ch.7).** The inner critic ("the Crow") and the Jungian shadow are the chapter's subject: the parts of ourselves we disown don't disappear, they run the show from behind us and surface in the people and conflicts around us. This is where "who I am shapes how I lead" becomes concrete.
concepts: [A1.4] [A8.1]

**9. Heartbreak and resilience (ch.8).** Resilience is recast as the path and equanimity as the destination; fear, not a lack of grit, is named as the real block to action. The "Loyal Soldier" is the protective inner voice that keeps us small.
concepts: [A10.5] [A1.5]

**10. Leadership and growing up (ch.9 + afterword).** The synthesis: the leader's job is to enable and bear witness, not to save or fix — rescuing people stunts their growth. What makes hard things *complicated* is leaders avoiding their own work; the examined leader frees the group, the unexamined one warps it. The aim is "enough" — equanimity, and knowing when to let go.
concepts: [A1.2] [A9.6] [A9.4] [A10.2]
