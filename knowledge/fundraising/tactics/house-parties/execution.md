# House Parties / Salon Fundraisers — Execution Plan

**Experience flag:** First-timers receive full rationale, guidance on the ask moment, and how to manage the room. Experienced leaders receive direct action items.

**Solo vs. group:** The solo path means the leader is both host and asker — viable but demanding. The group path can split these roles, which generally works better.

---

## Your Team (group only)

Populated from Block 3 role mapping:
- **Host** — opens their space, invites guests personally, sets the tone
- **Ask lead** — makes the in-room fundraising ask (can be the host or someone else)
- **Guest coordinators** — group members who bring their own personal networks
- **Logistics support** — handles setup, materials, cash handling if applicable, email collection
- **Follow-up coordinator** — sends thank yous and tracks donations

---

## Phase 1 — Prepare

**Step 1: Confirm host and venue**

The host and their space are the foundation. Confirm: who is hosting, where the event will be, and that the host is comfortable facilitating a fundraising ask in their space.

**Step 2: Build the guest list**

The host should personally invite their own network. With a group, each group member can also invite their own personal contacts — this expands the room while preserving the relationship-driven dynamic that makes this tactic work. Everyone inviting should be able to personally vouch for their guests.

[Generate guest list framing note: focus on people who care about this cause, trust the inviter personally, and have the capacity to give at the ask amount. A room of 15 well-chosen people outperforms a room of 50 casual acquaintances every time.]

**Step 3: Send personal invitations**

Invitations should feel personal — not a mass event invite. A personal message or phone call from the inviter outperforms an event link every time. Each person inviting sends their own personal invitations.

[Generate invitation message. Personalised to: host name, cause, story, event date, time, RSVP method. Warm, personal tone.]
[If with a group, generate group member version with [YOUR NAME] placeholder.]

**Step 4: Confirm payment methods**

Before the event, confirm exactly how guests can give — donation page URL, whether cash or cheques will be accepted, and any alternative payment methods. Every person helping must know the answer to "how do I give?" before guests arrive.

**Step 5: Confirm approval needs**

Check whether any materials or messaging need approval before use. Build this into your timeline.

**Step 6: Prepare the ask**

The ask moment is where house parties succeed or fail. Prepare a short, structured ask — 2–3 minutes maximum.

[Generate in-room ask script. Personalised to: confirmed story, ask amount, audience warmth, how to give. Structure: personal story opening → why this moment matters → specific ask with amount → what their gift will do → three ways to give right now. Includes guidance on reading the room.]

**Step 7: Prepare leave-behind materials**

Every guest should leave with something — a card with a QR code, a small flyer, or a pledge form. People who don't give on the night need a frictionless way to give later.

**Step 8: Set up email collection**

Confirm you have a way to capture email addresses from everyone who gives — through the donation page or a sign-up sheet at the door.

---

## Phase 2 — Host the Event

**Step 9: Welcome guests and set the tone**

The host should personally greet every guest. The warmth of the welcome sets the emotional tone for the ask. This is a social gathering first and a fundraiser second.

**Step 10: Make the ask**

Time the ask for after guests have had a chance to connect and settle — usually 30–45 minutes into the event. Make it clearly, confidently, and briefly. Refer to the script but don't read from it.

[If cash or cheques are being accepted: have the logistics support person ready to receive them immediately after the ask while momentum is high.]

**Step 11: Thank donors before they leave**

A personal thank you before guests leave — from the host or ask lead — reinforces the gift and strengthens the relationship. Don't let donors walk out without acknowledgement.

## Phase 3 — Follow Up

**Step 12: Thank you messages (within 24 hours)**

Send a personal thank you to everyone who attended — donors and non-donors alike. The person who invited each guest should ideally send the thank you, keeping the relationship personal.

---

📌 **The ask timing — 30–45 minutes in — is specific and practical.** Too early feels transactional; too late loses the energy of the room. Most leaders won't know this instinctively.

📌 **The two-version thank you (donors vs. non-donor attendees) is important.** Treating them the same misses a real opportunity with sympathetic non-donors who are clearly engaged with the cause.

📌 **Solo leaders carry both roles.** If the leader is doing this alone, acknowledge it directly in the brief: they are both the host setting the tone and the person making the ask. That's doable — plan for it.
