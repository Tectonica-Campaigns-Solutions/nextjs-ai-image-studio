# Story Elicitation — Personal Track

Use this track when the leader confirms in Step 1 that the cause has touched their life directly, or when they have a direct personal or witnessed connection to the story.

Source: Shannon Miller, Emerging Gifts — Story Development Input (May 2026).


## Step 1: Personal relevance (runs before both tracks)

Ask these two questions before any story elicitation — they determine which track to follow.

**Q1:** "Why are you personally doing this — what is your connection to this cause?"

**Q2:** "Is this something that has touched your life directly, or is it something you care deeply about even though it hasn't?"

**Routing logic after Step 1:**
- Personal connection → continue with Step 2a below (personal track)
- Issues-based or ideological connection → route to issues-based track
- Self-evident community cause (garden, neighbourhood fund, local school) → surface the reason-based tier, do not force a full narrative. Acknowledge the cause directly: "Some causes are their own story — the value is obvious. Let's make sure your ask reflects why this matters to your community right now."


## Step 2a: Personal story elicitation

For leaders who confirm a personal or direct connection in Step 1.

**Q3:** "Tell me about something you witnessed or experienced that made this cause important to you — not what the issue is, but what happened."

**Q4:** "Can you describe that moment as if you're telling it to a friend over coffee — where were you, who was there, what did you see or hear, how did it make you feel?"

**Q5:** "What was different after that moment compared to before?"

**What to listen for:** a person's name or description, a specific location, a specific event. These three things together signal a usable story.

**If the leader jumps to the abstract:** redirect without dismissing — "That's a great description of the issue — help me find the person inside it. Who is the most affected person you can picture right now?"

**If the leader says their cause isn't dramatic enough:** "Some of the most effective fundraising stories are not about crisis. They are about belonging, place, and the small things that make a community worth staying in. What is one thing your group has or does that you would genuinely miss if it were gone?"

This redirect almost always unlocks the story. It works especially well for neighbourhood and community organisations.


## Step 3: 'Why now?' prompt for chronic situations

**Trigger:** the leader describes an ongoing or chronic situation rather than a specific acute event — signals include phrases like "this has been a problem for years" or "we've always struggled with this."

**Follow-up question:** "This has clearly been an ongoing issue — what makes this the moment to act? Why now, rather than six months ago or six months from now?"

The 'why now?' answer becomes a required component of the story for brief population. Urgency must be constructed for chronic situations — it does not come built in.


## Step 4: Six Ws completeness check

Run this internal check after the story is shared and before locking it for the brief. Apply only when a full story has been confirmed — not for reason-based or issues-based-with-evidence tiers.

For each W that is missing, ask the corresponding question. Do not ask all six sequentially if most are already present — only fill the gaps.

| W | What to check for | If missing — ask |
|---|---|---|
| Who | A named person or clearly described protagonist | "Who is the person at the center of this story — can you give them a name or a description?" |
| What | A specific event, situation, or challenge | "What specifically happened — or what is the ongoing situation that needs to change?" |
| Where | A location, even approximate | "Where did this happen — or where is this situation playing out?" |
| When | A time reference (can be ongoing) | "When did this happen — or how long has this been the situation?" |
| Why | Why this matters — the stakes | "Why does this matter? What is at risk or what would be lost if nothing changes?" |
| How | What the gift makes possible | "How does this fundraising drive help — what specifically changes because of what donors do?" |


## Step 5: Reflect back, confirm, and lock

Once the story is sufficiently complete, reflect it back in one or two sentences and ask for confirmation before locking.

**Reflect back:** "So if I've got this right — [1–2 sentence paraphrase of the story in your own words]. Is that the story you want at the center of your fundraising drive?"

**Confirm and lock:** "Got it. [Story summary]. That's a strong foundation for this campaign." Once confirmed, the story is locked. Use it throughout the brief — execution steps, CTA language, messaging.

Do not re-elicit or re-confirm after this point.


## Org-sourced story handling

If the leader indicates the story came from their org's comms team rather than personal experience, lock the story as confirmed but flag it in the brief. See `/framework/story-elicitation/story-elicitation.md` for the exact brief note language.


## Genuine failure state

If no story surfaces after Step 2a, the 'not dramatic enough' redirect, and the 'why now?' prompt: the leader genuinely does not have a story yet. They care about the cause but have not been close enough to the work to witness it in action.

Flag the gap in the brief explicitly and make story development the first step before any outreach begins:

**Brief note:** "Before you launch this fundraising drive, find your story. Talk to a beneficiary, a colleague, or someone whose life has been touched by this work. Ask them to describe a specific moment — not what the issue is, but what happened. The ask will land differently when it comes from a real moment."
