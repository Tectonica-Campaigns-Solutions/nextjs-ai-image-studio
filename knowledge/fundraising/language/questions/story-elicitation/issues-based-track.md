# Story Elicitation — Issues-Based Track

Use this track when the leader confirms in Step 1 that they care deeply about the cause but the issue has not touched their life directly. The cause itself carries the moral weight. Facts and figures may help carry some of the weight that narrative carries in personal stories.

Source: Shannon Miller, Emerging Gifts — Story Development Input (May 2026).


## Step 1: Personal relevance (shared with personal track)

Same two questions run before both tracks. See `/language/questions/story-elicitation/personal-track.md` for Q1 and Q2 and the routing logic.

Route to this track when: the leader confirms an issues-based or ideological connection — they care deeply but it has not touched them directly.


## Step 2b: Issues-based story elicitation

**Q3:** "Tell me about someone you know, or a story you've heard, where this issue made a real difference in someone's life — for better or worse."

**Q4:** "What would have been different for that person if the support hadn't been there — or if the problem hadn't been addressed?"

**Q5:** "Why does this particular issue matter to you personally, even if it hasn't affected you directly? What do you want other people to understand about it that they might not already know?"

**Q6:** "What data or facts do you have that make the scale of this problem concrete?"

Q6 surfaces the evidence tier. Facts do not replace story but they support it — especially when the personal connection is more ideological than experiential. A single strong quote or statistic can do the work of a paragraph of description.

**What to listen for:** a person's name or description (even secondhand), a specific situation, and a clear answer to why this matters to the fundraiser personally. If none of these are present after Q3–Q5, the leader may be operating in reason-based territory — see below.


## Reason-based routing

If the leader cannot identify any person or situation (even secondhand) and the cause is self-evident and community-focused — a garden, a neighbourhood fund, a school library — route to the reason-based tier.

Acknowledge the cause directly rather than continuing to push for a narrative: "Some causes are their own story — the value is obvious to people in this community. Let's make sure your ask reflects why this matters right now and why people should act today."

For reason-based stories, the minimum to proceed is: a clear, honest answer to 'why does this matter and why now?' The brief does not require a protagonist but notes that a personal story, if developed later, would strengthen the ask.


## Step 3: 'Why now?' prompt for chronic situations

**Trigger:** the leader describes an ongoing or chronic situation rather than a specific acute event — signals include phrases like "this has been a problem for years" or "this has always been an issue."

**Follow-up question:** "This has clearly been an ongoing issue — what makes this the moment to act? Why now, rather than six months ago or six months from now?"

The 'why now?' answer becomes a required component of the story for brief population. For issues-based stories in particular, urgency is rarely self-evident — it must be surfaced explicitly.


## Step 4: Six Ws completeness check (for issues-based stories with a human protagonist)

If the leader surfaced a secondhand story with an identifiable person or situation in Q3–Q4, run the same six Ws completeness check as the personal track. Apply only when a full or near-full story emerged — not for reason-based tier.

| W | What to check for | If missing — ask |
|---|---|---|
| Who | A named person or clearly described protagonist (even secondhand) | "Who is the person at the center of this story — even just a description?" |
| What | A specific event, situation, or challenge | "What specifically happened — or what is the ongoing situation that needs to change?" |
| Where | A location, even approximate | "Where did this happen — or where is this situation playing out?" |
| When | A time reference (can be ongoing) | "When did this happen — or how long has this been the situation?" |
| Why | Why this matters — the stakes | "Why does this matter? What is at risk or what would be lost if nothing changes?" |
| How | What the gift makes possible | "How does this fundraising drive help — what specifically changes because of what donors do?" |

For reason-based tier: the six Ws check does not apply. Proceed once the 'why now?' answer is confirmed.


## Step 5: Reflect back, confirm, and lock

Same as the personal track. Reflect back in one or two sentences, confirm with the leader, and lock. For issues-based stories that include secondhand narrative, acknowledge the nature of the story: "This is a story you heard rather than lived — that's still a strong foundation. Use it as shared with you and be straightforward with donors if they ask for more detail."

For reason-based tier, the reflect-back focuses on the argument rather than the story: "So the case you're making is: [paraphrase of why this matters and why now]. Is that the argument you want at the center of your ask?"


## Genuine failure state

If no person, situation, or compelling argument for urgency surfaces after the full issues-based sequence: flag the gap in the brief and make story development the first step before outreach.

**Brief note:** "Before you launch this fundraising drive, find the human face of this issue. Talk to someone whose life has been affected — even secondhand through a colleague or community member. Ask them to describe a specific moment — not what the issue is, but what happened. Alternatively, identify the strongest data point or fact that makes the scale of this problem concrete. Either will significantly strengthen your ask."
