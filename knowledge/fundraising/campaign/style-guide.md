# All Out — Editorial Style Guide (EN)

**What this document is.** All Out's editorial rules for English-language copy. Apply these when generating scripts, emails, social posts, CTAs, and any other written content for All Out leaders. Rules here override generic writing defaults. This pilot serves both US and UK audiences — dialect notes below.

---

## Organisation name

Always **All Out** — two separate, capitalized words. Never "AllOut," "all out," or abbreviated.

**Short description (use when space is limited):**
> a global movement for the rights of LGBT+ people.

**Long description (use in emails or longer pieces):**
> All Out is a global movement for love and equality. We are mobilizing thousands of people to build a world where no person will have to sacrifice their family or freedom, safety or dignity, because of who they are or who they love.

**Mission statement (use when referencing All Out's purpose):**
> All Out is mobilizing thousands of people to build a world where no person will have to sacrifice their family or freedom, safety or dignity, because of who they are or who they love.

## English dialect — US vs. UK

This pilot serves both US and UK audiences. Key differences to apply:

| US English | UK English |
|---|---|
| organization | organisation |
| mobilize | mobilise |
| neighbor | neighbour |
| color | colour |
| canceled | cancelled |
| mom | mum |
| center | centre |
| program | programme |
| vacation | holiday |
| apartment | flat |
| college | university |

When a leader's audience is US: use American English throughout.
When a leader's audience is UK: use British English throughout.
When mixed or unclear: default to American English and note the variation for the leader to adjust.

## Identity and community language

**Use the most radically inclusive LGBTQ+ language always.** This is All Out's standing rule and it overrides any default.

**Be specific, not generic.** If the piece is about trans people, say trans people — not LGBT+. If it's about lesbian mothers, say lesbian mothers. Use LGBT+ only when the piece genuinely addresses the full spectrum.

**Culturally specific identity terms:** use in italics without translation (e.g. *travesti*, *two-spirit*, *hijra*). A brief footnote can clarify if context doesn't make it clear.

**Pronouns:** never assume. Where gender identity is unknown, avoid gendered language entirely. Never use "s/he," "him/her," or "his/her." Use they/them.

**Words that must never appear in All Out copy:**
- homosexual/ity (except when quoting an opponent)
- transexual/ity
- homosexualism / transexualism
- transvestite
- transgendered (use transgender, not transgendered)
- sodomy

**On "conversion therapy" / "cure":** always put "cure" in quotation marks. "Conversion therapy" and "reparative therapy" are acceptable terms. Avoid "anti-gay cure" — use "gay cure" (the point is that these practices are not a cure for anything, not that they target anti-gay people).

## Numbers and currency

**Spell out numbers below 10** in body copy. Always spell out a number at the start of a sentence.

**Commas for numbers over 999:** 1,000 / 24,634 / 100,000

**Currency:** specify always. This pilot uses USD ($) and GBP (£).
- USD: $1,000 / $2,000 / $19,000
- GBP: £1,000 / £2,000 / £15,000
- In email, both currencies can appear: "$2,000 (that's over £1,600)"

**Percentages:** "20 percent" in formal copy; "20%" is fine in email.

**Round numbers:** express above 10 million in numerals + words: 20 million, $2M.

---

## Punctuation

**Oxford comma:** always, in English. "We fight for love, equality, and justice."

**En dashes in email:** use – not - for em/en dash contexts. HTML: `&ndash;`

**Quotation marks:** punctuation goes inside quotation marks. He said, "We won."

**Bolding in email:** if you read only the bolded parts, the email should still make sense. Bold the most important information and/or the call to action. Do not over-bold.

**All caps:** avoid. Occasional use for 1-3 word emphasis in a CTA is acceptable (e.g., "We need to act NOW!"). Never entire sentences.

**Em dashes as clause connectors:** use sparingly. One per piece maximum. This is a known AI writing habit — do not default to it.

---

## Formatting by channel

**Email:**
- Short paragraphs — one idea each, maximum three sentences
- Bold the key information and CTA; reading only bolded text should make sense
- No all-caps sentences
- No underlining except for links
- Italics sparingly, for emphasis or quotations only
- Do not over-format; visual clutter undermines credibility

**Social media:**
- One idea per post
- Oxford comma optional
- Native to the platform — not a press release in disguise
- Clear next step / CTA

**Press releases / formal materials:**
- Minimal formatting: no bolding, no all-caps
- Italics for translated quotations and publication names only

## Dates and geography

**Date format:**
- US English: March 15, 2026
- UK English (and international): 15 March 2026
- Pilot deadline: July 15 (US) / 15 July (UK)

**Geography:** be specific. "Lawmakers in the state of Georgia, US" — not "Lawmakers in Georgia" (could be the country). All Out is a global movement; assume a global audience.

**Avoid seasons as time references.** Not "this spring" — say the month. Seasons don't apply everywhere.

---

## Quoting team members

Structure: "[Name], [title] at/with All Out"
Example: "Joe Macaré, Senior Manager of Online Fundraising at All Out"

---

## Think globally

All Out's audience is worldwide. Avoid:
- Metaphors, puns, or cultural references that only land in one country
- "America/American" when you mean US/US-based
- Assumptions about what the reader's government, legal system, or conventions look like

Use: United States / US, not America. The Americas is a continent.

---

## What this means for the Pride Pilot

Leaders are writing as themselves — All Out members and supporters — not as All Out staff. Apply these rules to help their copy sound like they belong to this movement without sounding like they work for it.

Key applications:
- Identity language: radically inclusive, specific, never the banned terms
- Currency: confirm US or UK audience before generating dollar/pound amounts
- Dialect: confirm US or UK audience before generating; default to US if mixed
- Dates: July 15 is the hard deadline — use the correct format for the audience
- Tone: warm, direct, personal — not polished or corporate
