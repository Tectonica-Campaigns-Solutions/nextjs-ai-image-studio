# All Out — Voice Reference & Appeal Examples

**What this document is.** Three real appeals from All Out's Queer-to-Queer peer fundraising series. Use these when generating CTAs, scripts, emails, social posts, and any other copy for All Out leaders. The goal is not to copy these — it is to absorb how All Out writes and apply that register to the leader's own story and context. Read the examples first. Then generate.

---

## How All Out writes — the pattern

Before the examples, the pattern that runs through all three:

**Opens with a person, not a cause.** Every appeal starts with a human being — someone with a name, a relationship, a reason. The cause follows from the person; the person does not follow from the cause.

**Stakes are concrete, not abstract.** Not "LGBT+ rights are under threat" but "keeping a shelter open through winter," "overturning a 10-year sentence," "relocating queer people from war zones." The specificity is what makes the urgency real.

**The ask is relational.** "People like you" and "members like you" recur constantly. Donors are not a funding stream — they are the movement. The ask is to join something, not to give to something.

**Tone is warm and direct, never corporate.** First names, contractions, personal pronouns. Sentences that sound like a person talking. Nothing polished in the way that sounds like it came from a communications department.

**Opposition is named plainly.** "Well-funded bigoted extremists." No euphemism, no hedging. The fight is real and the other side is named as such.

**Urgency is earned, not manufactured.** It comes from the specificity of what the money does — not from artificial deadlines or guilt.

## Example 1 — Queer-to-Queer launch appeal (Jaime Maron, February 2023)

**What this is:** The platform launch email. Announces Queer-to-Queer to the list. Sets the framing for the whole program.

**Full text:**

> Friend –
>
> All Out was founded on the idea that in today's world, people power is the greatest force for change.
>
> Since we started 11 years ago, we've harnessed the collective power of millions of people all over the world. Together, people like you have lifted up the fights of LGBT+ activists and grassroots organisations facing the most urgent threats to their safety and dignity.
>
> All Out members have always been this movement's strongest resource. And now, there's an even easier way to make a BIG difference.
>
> I'm so proud to announce the launch of All Out's brand new peer-to-peer giving platform: Queer-to-Queer!
>
> Queer-to-Queer is a custom fundraising platform, built and hosted by All Out, and exclusively for LGBT+ rights. It allows you to take your support to the next level.
>
> In just a few steps, you can launch your own fundraiser to help fuel All Out's campaigns for LGBT+ rights around the world. It just takes a few minutes to get set up and our team of fundraising professionals will help make sure you reach your goal.
>
> Got a birthday coming up? Getting married? Instead of asking for more stuff, you can ask your friends and family to give something even better, by chipping in and supporting the fight for LGBT+ rights!
>
> All Out members like you have accomplished some amazing things. And with Queer-to-Queer, I can't wait to see what we do next.
>
> Create an account and start your Queer-to-Queer fundraising campaign today!
>
> Thanks for going All Out,
> Jaime Maron, Senior Manager of Online Fundraising (she/her)

**Craft notes for generation:**
- "People power is the greatest force for change" — the core frame; use it or something close when the leader is framing why this matters
- The occasion hook ("Got a birthday coming up? Getting married?") is a model for connecting personal moments to the ask — adapt to Pride season context
- "Thanks for going All Out" — the sign-off is a brand signature; when generating thank-yous in All Out's voice, this is the right close
- Short paragraphs, one idea each. Never more than three sentences per paragraph in email.

## Example 2 — Fundraising hero profile (Joe Macaré, November 2023)

**What this is:** A recurring email type — profiling a member who fundraised and why. This is the model for story-based appeals where the fundraiser is the protagonist, not the organization.

**Full text:**

> Hi Sarah –
>
> My name is Joe and I'm All Out's new Senior Manager of Online Fundraising. I do this work because my conscience won't let me ignore it. When LGBT+ people around the world are being threatened or oppressed, I have to take action. I know you feel the same way.
>
> As the holiday season approaches, I wanted to tell you about a very special opportunity to give back and fuel real change for LGBT+ people around the world.
>
> It's called Queer-to-Queer – All Out's new peer-to-peer giving platform. Instead of asking for gifts and more stuff (that you might end up regifting anyway!), you can invite your friends, family, and community members to support All Out's campaigns for LGBT+ rights.
>
> How does it work? In just a few minutes, you can create your own fundraising page and share it with people you know on social media, by email, or whichever way you usually keep in touch! We'll also send you a few emails with helpful tips and encouragement to help you reach your goal.
>
> Ask your friends and family to chip in and support a cause you care about. Their donations will enable wins around the globe: shutting down dangerous "conversion therapy" practices, relocating vulnerable LGBT+ people in war-torn regions, supporting shelters, reversing anti-LGBT+ laws, and so much more.
>
> All Out is a people-powered movement for LGBT+ rights around the world. This movement is made up of thousands of people who organise petitions and campaigns in the fight for LGBT+ rights, spread the word in their communities, and support our work with donations.
>
> Our opponents are well-funded, bigoted extremists. We can only stop them when we work together, not alone. That's what we mean by people power. And creating your fundraiser for All Out is a great way to show your people power!

**Craft notes for generation:**
- "My conscience won't let me ignore it" — this is the personal connection line; when eliciting a leader's story, this framing invites the why-I-care answer
- The impact list ("shutting down conversion therapy, relocating LGBT+ people, supporting shelters, reversing anti-LGBT+ laws") is a model for concrete impact language — use specifics from All Out's current work, drawn from the impact_statement in CLIENT CONTEXT
- "Our opponents are well-funded, bigoted extremists" — plain, direct, no hedging; use this register when the leader wants to name the opposition
- "We can only stop them when we work together, not alone" — the solidarity frame; use when the ask is about joining a collective effort rather than making a transaction

## Example 3 — Peer fundraiser success story (Eric and Adrian, December 2023)

**What this is:** The most important example. A real couple who fundraised for their wedding, raised $19,000. This is the gold-standard story-based appeal — personal motivation, concrete result, invitation to do the same.

**Full text:**

> Sarah,
>
> Everyone should have the right to marry whomever they love. This isn't just what we believe – it's deeply personal for us.
>
> So when we got married ourselves, we knew we wanted to raise money for an organisation that is fighting for love, equality, and justice, all around the world.
>
> To do that, we used Queer-to-Queer – All Out's new peer-to-peer giving platform. It's a great way to get your friends, family, and community members to support All Out's campaigns for LGBT+ rights.
>
> Just like we did, you can create a fundraising page for All Out and invite people you know to donate. You can use social media or email to ask your friends and family to chip in and support All Out's work. Their gifts will help All Out win victories in the fight for marriage equality across the globe, reunite rainbow families, support LGBT+ shelters, and relocate queer people threatened by war, natural disasters, and bigoted laws.
>
> The two of us raised over $19,000 (that's over £15,000!) to support All Out's mission, almost double our goal. You can pick whatever goal you think is best – any amount helps. Just imagine how much we can all raise together, if you create a fundraising page for All Out today.
>
> The holidays are a great time to start your own fundraiser to All Out. Instead of asking for gifts, why not ask friends and loved ones to support a cause you really care about?

**Craft notes for generation:**
- "This isn't just what we believe – it's deeply personal for us" — the pivot from principle to personal story; this is the exact move to make in story elicitation when a leader has a cause connection but hasn't named their personal angle yet
- Opening with the universal value ("everyone should have the right to marry") before the personal moment — a reliable structure for story-led emails: value → personal moment → invitation
- "$19,000 — almost double our goal" — social proof through a real number; when a leader has a goal, anchor the ask to what's possible, not just what's required
- "Any amount helps. Just imagine how much we can all raise together" — the scale frame; use when the leader worries their goal is too small or their network is too narrow
- "Just like we did, you can..." — the invitation structure; this is the template for any CTA where the leader is sharing their story to recruit donors: here's what I did, you can do it too

## Applying this to the Pride Pilot

The Queer-to-Queer examples are P2P fundraising for individual pages. The Pride Pilot uses group-based fundraising. The voice is the same; the frame shifts:

- **From:** "I started a fundraising page for my wedding"
- **To:** "Our group is fundraising this Pride season"

The core moves carry over unchanged: open with a person or a moment, name the stakes concretely, frame the ask as joining something rather than giving to something, close with a clear action.

When generating copy for All Out leaders in this pilot:
- Use the leader's own story as the opening, not All Out's mission statement
- Draw impact language from the `impact_statement` in CLIENT CONTEXT — those are the specific, current examples
- The "we are one" frame (from `relational_goal` in CLIENT CONTEXT) is the Pride Pilot's equivalent of "people power" — use it when the ask is about community solidarity, not just fundraising
- Sign-offs: "Thanks for going All Out" belongs to All Out staff; leaders sign as themselves
